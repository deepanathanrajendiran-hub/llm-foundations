Decoding & Sampling Strategies

The last mile of generation: how a model turns raw logits into actual tokens — greedy, beam, temperature, top-k/p, min-p, penalties, contrastive, constrained, and speculative decoding.

←→ navigate · S speaker notes · O overview · F fullscreen · append ?print-pdf to export

Welcome. This module is about the step everyone forgets is even a choice: the model gives you a score for every word in the vocabulary at every position, and decoding is the rule that picks the actual next token. The same trained model, with the same weights, can be a boring robot or a wild improviser depending purely on these settings — and they cost nothing to change at inference time. The thesis of the whole lecture is one sentence: decoding navigates a single trade-off — coherence versus diversity, and every knob (temperature, top_p, top_k, penalties, seed) is just a different way to ride that dial. We'll climb the full ladder classic to SOTA, do the softmax math, write the code, and end on the surprisingly hard problem of making any of it reproducible.

Core ideas to land

Five things to take away

  • Logits are not probabilities. Softmax (and temperature) turns scores into a distribution first.
  • The universal trade-off = coherence vs diversity. Every knob rides that one dial.
  • Truncate THEN renormalize. After dropping the tail, the survivors must sum to 1 again.
  • Speculative decoding is exact, not approximate. Same output distribution, just faster.
  • Determinism is harder than it looks. Even temperature 0 isn't a guarantee on a GPU.
This is the executive summary — I'll return to it at the end as the recap. Idea one: a logit is an unbounded raw score; only after softmax is it a probability, and temperature acts on the logits before that. Idea two: the spine of the whole deck — coherence (safe, high-probability, on-topic) versus diversity (surprising, creative, varied) — and tuning is just choosing where on that line to sit. Idea three: the single most common bug — people truncate to a candidate set and forget to renormalize, or they renormalize wrong. Idea four: a beautiful result — speculative decoding provably preserves the target model's distribution, so it's a free speedup, not a quality trade. Idea five: reproducibility is deceptively deep — fixing a seed isn't enough once you add batching and GPU floating-point nondeterminism. If they remember nothing else: "coherence vs diversity" and "truncate then renormalize."

Why it matters

Decoding is the last mile

Logitsraw scores, one per token → Softmaxscores → probabilities → Selection rulegreedy? sample? truncate? → Tokenappend & feed back
  • The model only ever outputs a vector of logits — one score per vocabulary entry.
  • Decoding is the selection rule that turns that vector into one concrete token.
  • Same weights, same prompt — the rule alone decides robot vs. poet.
Frame this as the part of inference people skip over. A trained LLM does one thing per step: produce a logit vector over the whole vocabulary. It does not output text — it outputs scores. Softmax converts those scores into a probability distribution; then a selection rule collapses that distribution to a single token; then you append it and loop. The punchline is that the model's weights are fixed — all the behavioral difference between a deterministic factual assistant and a wild creative writer comes from this last-mile rule. That's why it's worth a whole module: it's high-leverage, runs entirely at inference time, and is the thing you actually tune in production.

The cheapest lever you have — and the easiest to misconfigure.

Open any chat-completion API and the sampling block is right there: temperature, top_p, top_k, the two penalties, seed, stop. Every one of these is a decoding choice. What makes them special — and worth understanding deeply — is that they are free: no fine-tuning, no extra weights, no architecture change, applied per request and changeable on the fly. That's also the trap: because they're free and hidden, teams ship bad defaults (temperature 1.0 on a factual extractor, top_p 1.0 with no truncation) and quietly degrade quality without realizing the model itself is fine. Intuition

How do you pick the next word?

  • Each step, the model hands you a scorecard — a number for every possible next word.
  • Always take the top word? → safe, predictable … and often boring & loopy. (greedy)
  • Roll weighted dice? → sometimes pick a lower word in proportion to its score. (sampling)
  • Keep a shortlist, then roll? → ignore the long tail of nonsense, gamble only among good options. (top-k / top-p)
  • The whole field is just how much risk you let into that pick.
This is the mental model I lean on all lecture. Picture the model as a contestant who, every turn, writes a score next to every word in the dictionary. Decoding is your policy for reading that scorecard. Policy one: always circle the highest number — that's greedy, totally deterministic, and exactly why it gets stuck repeating itself. Policy two: roll dice weighted by the scores, so a word twice as likely gets picked roughly twice as often — that's plain sampling, more varied but it can wander into the garbage in the long tail. Policy three, the smart middle: first cross off everything below a shortlist, then roll dice only among the survivors — that's top-k and top-p. Every technique in this deck is a variation on "how big is the shortlist and how loaded are the dice." That single framing — risk dial — is what I want them holding.

Here is the engine of all text generation: the autoregressive loop. Start with the prompt tokens; run a forward pass; get logits; apply your decoding rule to pick one token; append that token to the context; and loop. The dotted branch is the exit — you stop when the model emits an end-of-sequence token or hits a stop sequence. Two things to stress. First, the feedback: the token you choose becomes part of the input for the very next step, so one risky pick can steer everything after it — errors compound. Second, the decode box is the only place your knobs act; everything else (the forward pass, the logits) is fixed by the trained model. This loop is the skeleton that the entire "how it works" section hangs off.

How it works · the pipeline

From raw logits to a sampled token

One step of decoding, in order. Watch the distribution get reshaped, trimmed, renormalized, then sampled.

Drive the whole pipeline off this widget — it's the spine of the section. Step one: the model gives logits, raw real-valued scores that can be negative or huge; emphasize they are NOT probabilities. Step two: softmax exponentiates each and divides by the sum, producing a proper distribution. Step three is the subtle one — temperature is applied to the logits before softmax (divide each logit by T), so it must come conceptually before or fused with the softmax; T above 1 flattens toward uniform, below 1 sharpens toward the peak. Step four: truncation drops the unreliable tail — top-k by count, top-p by cumulative mass, min-p by a relative threshold. Step five is the bug magnet — after you zero out the tail the survivors no longer sum to 1, so you must renormalize. Step six: draw a token from that final distribution; greedy is just the degenerate case where you take the argmax. Then append and run the loop again.

How it works · the distribution reshapes

Same logits, four different distributions

  1. Raw (T=1): the model's natural distribution — tallest bar is the most likely token, with a long low tail.
  2. T > 1 flattens: bars even out, low-probability tokens get a real shot → more diversity.
  3. T < 1 sharpens: the peak grows, the tail shrinks toward zero → more coherence.
  4. top-p truncates: grey out everything past the cumulative mass cutoff — the nucleus survives, the tail is banned.

Temperature reshapes; truncation removes. They compose.

This is the visual payoff of the section — one set of logits, four pictures. Start with the raw distribution at temperature 1: one dominant bar, a moderate second, and a fat tail of unlikely tokens. Advance: temperature above 1 squashes the bars toward each other — the gaps shrink, so unlikely tokens become genuinely samplable, which is diversity. Advance again: temperature below 1 does the opposite — it stretches the gaps, the top bar towers, the tail collapses toward zero, which is coherence and safety. Final advance: top-p (nucleus) is a different operation entirely — it doesn't reshape, it cuts. Sort by probability, walk down accumulating mass until you hit p (say 0.9), and ban everything after that. The greyed bars are gone; you'll renormalize and sample only from the nucleus. The takeaway on the right is the one to hammer: temperature reshapes the whole curve, truncation removes the tail, and you typically apply both together — temperature first, then truncate, then renormalize, then sample.

How it works · the decode loop

Why one risky pick echoes forever

Contexttokens so far → Forwardlogits → Decodepick token → Appendgrow context ↻ Repeatuntil EOS / stop
  1. Feed the current context (prompt + everything generated so far) through the model.
  2. Get logits for the next position; apply temperature, truncate, renormalize.
  3. Pick one token — argmax (greedy) or a weighted draw (sampling).
  4. Append it to the context — the chosen token now conditions every future step.
  5. Loop until the model emits EOS or you hit a stop sequence / max length.

Because each pick is fed back in, a single off-distribution token can derail the rest — errors compound.

Close the section by making the loop concrete and explaining why it matters for everything downstream. The mechanics: context in, logits out, decode to a single token, append, repeat. The KV cache makes the forward pass cheap per step (we cover that in another module), but conceptually it's a fresh prediction conditioned on the growing prefix each time. The teaching point is the append step — this is autoregression, and it means the token you commit to is not a throwaway: it becomes evidence the model conditions on next. So an unlucky sample early on doesn't just add one odd word; it shifts the model into a region of context it would never have reached on its own, and the rest of the generation can spiral — this is exposure bias and it's why repetition loops and topic drift happen. That's the bridge into the approaches section: every decoding strategy is, in part, a way to manage the risk that this feedback loop amplifies.

The unifying frame I want them to hold: the model hands you the same distribution over the vocabulary at every step; all six strategies differ only in how they pick a token from it. Approaches · 1 of 6

Greedy decoding classic

What: at every step take the single highest-probability token. How: compute logits → softmax → argmax → append → repeat until EOS. Solves: the simplest possible decoder — deterministic, fast, zero hyperparameters.

Pros
  • Fully deterministic & cheap (1 forward pass/token)
  • Strong for short factual answers
Cons
  • Locally optimal ≠ globally most-probable string
  • Bland, gets stuck in repetition loops

vs previous: this is the baseline — every later method exists to fix something greedy gets wrong.

Greedy is the floor everything else builds on. The defining rule is one word: argmax. It is deterministic given a fixed prompt and model, costs nothing extra, and works fine for short, closed-form answers like "what is 2+2." The first big idea to surface: argmax at each step is locally greedy and does not guarantee the globally most probable sequence — a slightly lower-probability token now can unlock a much higher-probability continuation later, and greedy can never see that. The second weakness is degeneration: because the highest-likelihood next token is often a word it just used, greedy famously falls into "I am very very very..." loops. Those two failures — no lookahead, and repetition — motivate beam search and sampling respectively.

How it works · Greedy decoding

Greedy decoding — step by step

Logitsvocab scores → Softmaxprobs → Argmaxpick top-1 → Appendto sequence ↻ Repeatuntil EOS
  1. Model emits a logit vector over the whole vocabulary.
  2. Softmax turns logits into a probability distribution.
  3. Take argmax — the single most probable token.
  4. Append it to the running sequence; feed it back in.
  5. Loop until the EOS token (or a length cap) is produced.
Walk the loop literally. Note that softmax is unnecessary for greedy — argmax of the logits equals argmax of the probabilities, so an efficient implementation skips the softmax entirely and just takes the top logit. Each chosen token is fed back in as context for the next step; that autoregressive feedback is why a single bad early pick can derail the whole generation. Emphasize the loop arrow: nothing here is reconsidered. Once a token is committed it is permanent, which is precisely the limitation beam search relaxes by keeping several candidate sequences alive at once.

Approaches · 2 of 6

Beam search classic

What: keep the k best partial sequences (hypotheses) alive instead of one. How: at each step expand all k beams, score by sum of log-probs, keep the top k; apply length normalization so long hypotheses aren't penalized. Solves: greedy's no-lookahead — explores a wider slice of the search tree.

Pros
  • Higher-likelihood sequences than greedy
  • Excellent for tasks with one right answer (MT, summarization)
Cons
  • k× the compute/memory of greedy
  • Short, generic, repetitive in open-ended chat

vs previous: greedy commits to one token; beam hedges across k hypotheses and can recover from a locally-bad first pick.

Diverse beam search ↗

Beam search is greedy with a budget of k parallel guesses. Width k=1 is exactly greedy; larger k explores more of the tree and tends to find higher-likelihood strings. Two technical points interviewers probe. First, why sum log-probs rather than multiply raw probabilities: probabilities are tiny numbers in [0,1], multiplying hundreds of them underflows to zero in floating point, so we add their logs instead — mathematically identical, numerically stable. Second, length normalization: because every extra token adds another negative log-prob, raw scores favor shorter sequences, so we divide the cumulative score by length (often length to a power alpha) to stop beam from truncating early. The deeper insight: beam optimizes likelihood, which is the right objective for machine translation (one faithful rendering) but wrong for open chat — the highest-likelihood chat reply is bland and safe ("I don't know," "Thank you"), and beams collapse onto near-identical variants, which is why diverse beam search adds a penalty between groups to spread them out.

How it works · Beam search

Beam search — step by step (width k = 2)

Startk beams → Expandall next tokens → ScoreΣ log-prob → Prunekeep top-k → Normalizeby length
  1. Hold k partial hypotheses, each with a cumulative log-prob score.
  2. Expand: for each beam, consider every possible next token.
  3. Score each candidate = parent score + log P(next token).
  4. Prune the k×vocab candidates back down to the top k.
  5. At the end, divide scores by length (normalization) and return the best.
Drive this as the search-tree picture. At each depth we have k living beams; expanding them gives k times the vocabulary candidates, and we keep only the best k — that prune step is what bounds the cost to roughly k times greedy per step in both compute and memory. The score is purely additive in log space: a beam's score is its parent's score plus the log-probability of the token it just appended. The final length-normalization step is where many implementations differ — without it the model prefers to emit EOS early because every extra token only ever lowers the score; dividing by length (or length^alpha) levels the playing field. Note completed beams (those that hit EOS) are set aside and compared at the end against the normalized scores of the rest.

Approaches · 3 of 6

Temperature scaling the shape knob

What: a single knob that controls how peaked vs. flat the distribution is. How: divide every logit by T before softmax: softmax(z / T). Solves: gives sampling a continuous dial between deterministic and wildly creative — without it, sampling is all-or-nothing.

Pros
  • One intuitive knob: creativity vs. focus
  • T→0 recovers greedy; T=1 is the raw model
Cons
  • High T alone reaches deep into the bad tail
  • Doesn't truncate — needs top-k/top-p as a guard

vs previous: greedy/beam pick a token; temperature instead reshapes the distribution — it's a pre-step, not a selection rule.

Temperature is conceptually different from everything around it — it does not choose a token, it reshapes the distribution that some sampler will then draw from. The operation is exact: divide the logits by a scalar T, then softmax as usual; placement matters — it is on the logits, before softmax. T<1 sharpens (the model gets more confident, closer to greedy), T>1 flattens (more uniform, more surprising), and the limit T→0 is exactly greedy because all the mass piles onto the top logit. The reason it works lives in the exponential: softmax exponentiates, and dividing the exponent by T>1 shrinks the gaps between logits so the ratios between probabilities compress toward 1 (flatter); dividing by T<1 magnifies those gaps (peakier). Crucially it never changes which token is the argmax — scaling preserves order — so temperature only ever redistributes mass, it never reranks. Note T=0 is undefined in the formula (division by zero); in practice "temperature 0" is implemented as "just take argmax."

Reveal them left to right: at T<1 the top token towers and the rest are squashed — nearly deterministic; at T=1 you see the model's honest distribution; at T>1 the bars level out so even unlikely tokens get a real shot. End by stressing the dependency that motivates the next rung: high T floods the tail with probability mass, so in practice you almost always pair temperature with a truncation method.

Approaches · 4 of 6

Top-k sampling modern

What: sample, but only from the k highest-probability tokens. How: sort logits, keep the top k, zero out the rest, renormalize the survivors to sum to 1, then sample. Solves: pure sampling's fatal flaw — the long tail of bad-but-nonzero tokens that occasionally get drawn.

Pros
  • Cuts the garbage tail; cheap & simple
  • Keeps diversity within the top set
Cons
  • k is fixed — ignores how confident the model is
  • Too small when flat; too big when peaked

vs previous: temperature reshapes mass but keeps the whole tail; top-k cuts the tail off so a high temperature can't drag in nonsense.

Top-k is the first true truncation method. Pure sampling from the full distribution sounds principled but has a killer bug: the tail holds thousands of low-probability tokens, and collectively they carry enough mass that you eventually draw one, derailing the text. Top-k fixes this bluntly — keep the k most probable tokens, discard everything else, renormalize the kept probabilities so they form a valid distribution again (this renormalize step is mandatory; otherwise the surviving probabilities sum to less than 1), then sample. The weakness is that k is a constant: when the model is razor-sharp (one token at 0.95) a k of 50 still admits 49 junk tokens; when the model is genuinely uncertain (mass spread over hundreds of plausible tokens) the same k=50 amputates good options. That mismatch — a fixed budget against a variable distribution — is exactly what nucleus sampling was invented to fix.

How it works · Top-k

Top-k sampling — step by step (k = 3)

All probsfull vocab → Sortdescending → Keep top-kcut tail → Renormalizesum → 1 → Sampledraw
  1. Start from the full probability distribution over the vocabulary.
  2. Sort tokens by probability, highest first.
  3. Keep only the top k; set every other token's probability to 0.
  4. Renormalize the k survivors so they sum back to 1.
  5. Sample one token from that truncated, renormalized set.
The five steps in order — the renormalize step is the one candidates forget on a whiteboard. A subtle interaction worth flagging: order of operations with temperature. Most implementations apply temperature to the logits first, then truncate to top-k, then renormalize; if you instead truncate first and scale after, you get a slightly different distribution, so libraries fix the order and you should know which one yours uses. Mention top_k=1 as the trick question: keeping exactly one token and sampling from it is deterministic argmax — you have reproduced greedy decoding. That observation bridges nicely back to slide one: every strategy is the same machine with a different cutoff.

Approaches · 5 of 6

Top-p / nucleus sampling modern

What: keep the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability ≥ p (Holtzman et al., 2019). How: sort by probability, walk down adding mass until the running total reaches p, keep that "nucleus," renormalize, sample. Solves: top-k's fixed cutoff — the set size now adapts to the model's confidence.

Pros
  • Adaptive: small set when confident, large when unsure
  • The de-facto default for open-ended generation
Cons
  • When confident, a fixed p can still admit a long flat tail
  • Absolute threshold ignores the top token's height

vs previous: top-k always keeps k tokens; top-p keeps however many it takes to cover probability p — a variable-size nucleus.

The Curious Case of Neural Text Degeneration ↗

Nucleus sampling is the single most-used decoder in production chat, so spend time here. The core move: instead of a fixed count, set a probability budget p (say 0.9) and keep the smallest group of top tokens whose probabilities sum to at least p. That makes the candidate-set size adapt to the distribution: when the model is confident and one token holds 0.95, the nucleus is tiny (maybe one token); when the model is uncertain with mass smeared over hundreds of tokens, the nucleus grows to include all the plausible ones. That is the precise fix for top-k's rigidity. The honest limitation, which sets up min-p: top-p uses an absolute cumulative threshold and doesn't look at how tall the top token is — in a confident-but-not-peaked distribution, reaching 0.9 can still require scooping up a long flat tail of mediocre tokens. Renormalize after selecting the nucleus, same as top-k.

How it works · Top-p (nucleus)

Top-p / nucleus — step by step (p = 0.90)

Sort probshigh → low → Accumulaterunning sum → Stop at ≥ pnucleus → Renormalizesum → 1 → Sampledraw
  1. Sort tokens by probability, highest first.
  2. Walk down, accumulating a running cumulative probability.
  3. Stop as soon as the running sum reaches p — those tokens are the nucleus.
  4. Renormalize the nucleus so it sums to 1.
  5. Sample one token from the nucleus.
Use the widget to make "adaptive" tangible. Step through once on a peaked distribution — the nucleus closes after one or two tokens — then narrate the contrast: on a flat distribution the same p=0.9 walk keeps accumulating and the nucleus swells to cover dozens of tokens. That self-sizing is the whole point and the reason it beat top-k in practice. Reinforce that the cumulative comparison is against the budget p, not against any token's individual height — that exact blind spot is what the next rung, min-p, attacks by anchoring the threshold to the top token instead. Renormalization is again non-optional.

Approaches · 6 of 6

Min-p sampling recent

What: keep tokens whose probability is at least a fraction of the top token's probability. How: set a relative threshold p · max_prob; keep every token above it, renormalize, sample (Nguyen et al., 2024). Solves: top-p's absolute-budget blind spot — the cutoff now scales with the model's peak confidence.

Pros
  • Robust across peaked vs. flat distributions
  • Stays coherent even at high temperature
Cons
  • Newer; another hyperparameter to tune
  • Less battle-tested than top-p defaults

vs previous: top-p uses an absolute cumulative budget; min-p uses a relative threshold tied to the most likely token.

Min-p Sampling ↗

Min-p is the recent rung and a favorite "do you read papers" question. The idea is to make the cutoff relative to the model's confidence rather than an absolute mass budget. You set a base p (e.g. 0.1), compute the threshold as p times the probability of the most likely token, and keep every token clearing that bar. Walk the two regimes: when the model is confident (top token at 0.95), the threshold is 0.095, so only genuinely strong contenders survive — it stays tight where top-p might still rake in a flat tail. When the model is uncertain (top token at maybe 0.10), the threshold drops to 0.01, so it keeps a broad set — appropriately permissive. That single relative rule behaves sensibly across both shapes, which is why it pairs especially well with high temperature: even after flattening, the cutoff rescales to whatever the new peak is. The cost is maturity — it is newer and less defaulted-to than top-p, and it is one more knob.

How it works · Min-p

Min-p sampling — step by step (base p = 0.1)

Find maxtop prob → Thresholdp · max → Keep ≥ threshrelative cut → Renormalizesum → 1 → Sampledraw
  1. Find max_prob — the probability of the single most likely token.
  2. Compute a relative threshold = p · max_prob.
  3. Keep every token whose probability ≥ that threshold.
  4. Renormalize the survivors to sum to 1.
  5. Sample — the kept set auto-scales with peak confidence.
Step the widget and keep contrasting with top-p the whole way. The defining line: top-p's bar is "cumulative mass reaches p," min-p's bar is "individual probability reaches p times the tallest bar." Because the threshold is anchored to max_prob, it automatically tightens when the model is sure and loosens when it isn't — no fixed budget to mis-set. Approaches · 7 of 12

Typical sampling info-theoretic

What: keep tokens whose surprisal is close to the distribution's entropy, not just the most probable ones (Meister et al., 2022). How: compute entropy H of the step distribution; score each token by |−log p − H|; keep the smallest-deviation set until cumulative mass ≥ τ; renormalize & sample. Solves: top-p over-keeps boring high-prob tokens; typical aims for "expectedly informative" ones.

Pros
  • Grounded in information theory (target = entropy)
  • Avoids both the bland peak and the absurd tail
Cons
  • Less intuitive to tune than top-p
  • Can drop the single most-likely token

vs previous: top-p/min-p keep tokens by raw probability; typical keeps tokens by how close their surprisal is to expected surprisal.

Read more ↗

The idea comes from "typicality" in information theory: a sample is typical when its information content (surprisal, −log p) is near the source entropy H. Human text behaves this way — it isn't maximally probable at every token, it's expectedly informative. So instead of keeping the most-probable tokens (top-p) you keep the tokens whose surprisal is closest to H, summing their mass up to threshold τ, then renormalize and sample. The payoff: it can skip an over-likely-but-bland token and avoid the absurd long tail at the same time. The cost: it's less intuitive to tune, and because it can drop the argmax, it's a poor fit for factual tasks where you want the safe answer.

How it works · Typical sampling

Typical sampling — step by step

Walk it concretely. Suppose H is 3 bits. A token at 0.6 has surprisal ~0.7 bits — far below H, so it's "too predictable" and may be dropped. A token in the deep tail has surprisal of 12 bits — far above H, "too surprising," also dropped. Typical sampling keeps the band around H. The threshold τ (often ~0.95) plays a role like top-p's p but on the deviation-sorted list, not the probability-sorted list. That sort key is the whole difference from top-p: top-p sorts by probability descending; typical sorts by |surprisal − H| ascending.

Repetition control control

What: a family of penalties that push logits down for tokens the model has already produced. How: repetition penalty divides/scales the logit of any seen token; frequency penalty subtracts ∝ count, presence penalty subtracts a flat amount once-seen (OpenAI-style); no-repeat-ngram hard-bans any token that would complete a seen n-gram. Solves: likelihood-maximizing decoders loop ("the the the…").

Pros
  • Cheap, drop-in, no extra model
  • no-repeat-ngram guarantees no repeated n-gram
Cons

  • Divisive penalty is sign-sensitive on the logit

vs previous: truncation/typical pick from the distribution; penalties edit the logits first to fight degeneration directly.

Read more ↗

Three distinct mechanisms people conflate. (1) Repetition penalty (CTRL, Keskar et al.) divides the logit of every previously-seen token by θ>1 — but dividing only lowers a positive logit; for a negative logit, dividing makes it less negative, i.e. raises it, so implementations multiply when the logit is negative. That sign sensitivity is the classic gotcha. (2) OpenAI's frequency penalty subtracts an amount proportional to how many times the token appeared; presence penalty subtracts a flat amount the moment it appears once — frequency scales with count, presence is binary. (3) no-repeat-ngram is a hard constraint: it sets the logit of any token that would complete an already-seen n-gram to −∞. The contrast to teach: penalties are soft and tunable but can over-penalize; ngram-blocking is a hard guarantee but blindly forbids legitimate repeats like indentation or "New York, New York."

Show the sign-safe rule explicitly: logit = logit/θ if logit>0 else logit*θ — that's how HuggingFace implements it, because a naive divide would reward negative-logit tokens. Frequency vs presence: with frequency, a token said five times gets penalized five times harder; with presence, the penalty is the same whether it appeared once or five times. no-repeat-ngram is categorical — great for stopping verbatim loops, terrible for code where indentation repeats legitimately. The practical recipe: prefer a mild repetition penalty (~1.1-1.3) plus a small frequency penalty; reach for ngram-blocking only when you truly cannot tolerate any repeated phrase.

The key exam point is distinguishing the two — they're easy to confuse. Contrastive decoding uses two models: subtract a small "amateur" model's log-probs from a large "expert" model's log-probs (with a plausibility filter). The amateur captures the generic, degenerate failure modes both models share; subtracting it leaves the expert's distinctive knowledge, which improves open-ended generation and reasoning. That degeneration penalty directly discourages producing tokens whose representation looks like something already said — isotropy-driven anti-repetition. So: CD = strong minus weak logits (two models); CS = confidence minus self-similarity (one model, hidden states).

Mirostat perplexity target

What: a feedback controller that holds the output surprisal at a target value instead of fixing a static k/p (Basu et al., 2020). How: pick a target surprisal τ (a target perplexity); each step, dynamically choose a top-k cutoff; measure the observed surprisal; update an estimate μ with the error so the running cross-entropy converges to τ. Solves: static top-k/top-p let perplexity drift → either boredom or incoherence.

Pros
  • Directly targets perplexity — stable "interestingness"
  • Adapts the cutoff per step automatically
Cons
  • Another knob (τ, learning rate η) to set
  • Feedback lag; less common in mainstream stacks

vs previous: top-k/top-p/typical fix the selection rule; Mirostat fixes the outcome (surprisal) and adapts selection to hit it.

Read more ↗

Mirostat is a control-theory take on sampling. The observation: with static top-k or top-p, the actual perplexity of the generated text wanders — drift low and you get repetitive boredom, drift high and you get incoherence. Mirostat treats target surprisal τ as a setpoint and runs a feedback loop: it maintains an estimate μ (roughly 2τ, the effective vocabulary mass), at each step truncates using a k derived from μ and the local Zipfian decay, observes the actual surprisal of the chosen token, computes the error e = observed − τ, and nudges μ by −η·e. Over the sequence the running cross-entropy converges to τ. So you set the desired "perplexity" once and the controller adjusts truncation every step to maintain it — that's the whole pitch.

How it works · Mirostat

  1. Set a target surprisal τ (equivalently a target perplexity).
  2. Truncate the vocab using a cutoff derived from current estimate μ.
  3. Sample a token; measure its actual surprisal −log p.
  4. Compute error e = observed − τ.
  5. Update μ ← μ − η·e → loop; cross-entropy converges to τ.
Drive it as a thermostat: τ is the temperature you set on the wall, μ is the valve position, and the error term opens or closes the valve each step. If the model just emitted a very surprising token (e > 0), μ shrinks so the next step truncates harder and calms down; if it emitted something too predictable (e < 0), μ grows so the next step admits more candidates. The net effect is a steady, controllable level of surprise across the whole generation — Mirostat v1 estimates the Zipf exponent, v2 (the common one) drops that and just clips on surprisal directly. It's the only method here with an explicit closed feedback loop, which is why it gets the loop arrow.

Speculative decoding SOTA · fast

Pros
  • 2–3× faster, no change to output distribution
  • Self-draft variants (Medusa, EAGLE) need no separate model
Cons
  • Speedup hinges on draft acceptance rate & draft cost
  • A bad draft can be slower than plain decoding

Read more ↗

The headline efficiency trick and the one to spend the most time on. The bottleneck: decoding is memory-bound — each token needs a full forward pass that's underutilizing the GPU, so verifying many tokens at once is nearly as cheap as verifying one. Speculative decoding exploits this: a cheap draft model autoregressively proposes k tokens, then the target runs one parallel forward pass over all k, giving a probability for each. The accept/reject rule is the magic — accept token i with probability min(1, p_target/p_draft); on the first rejection, sample a corrected token from the normalized positive part of (p_target − p_draft); if every proposed token is accepted, you also get one bonus token from the target for free. Crucially this is provably equivalent to sampling from the target alone — same distribution, just faster. Speedup = expected accepted tokens per target pass, traded against draft cost; a draft that's too weak (low acceptance) or too expensive can erase the gain.

The math · (a) temperature softmax

Temperature reshapes the softmax

Divide each logit by T before exponentiating:

$$ p_i = \frac{e^{z_i/T}}{\sum_j e^{z_j/T}} $$

\(z_i\) = the raw logit for token \(i\) (the model's pre-softmax score); \(p_i\) = its sampling probability after normalization.

\(T\) = temperature. The exponent \(z_i/T\) scales every gap between logits by the same factor \(1/T\).

Why \(T<1\) sharpens: dividing by a number <1 magnifies the gaps \((z_i-z_j)\), so the exponential pulls mass onto the top token. \(T>1\) shrinks the gaps → flatter, more uniform.

Limits: \(T\to 0^+\) → one-hot on the argmax (= greedy); \(T\to\infty\) → uniform over the vocabulary.

Start by naming the pieces: z is the logit vector, the raw scores straight out of the final linear layer, and p is the probability after softmax. Temperature enters in exactly one place — you divide the logits by T before the exponential, never after softmax. The key reasoning to walk through on the board: softmax only cares about the differences between logits, so multiplying the exponent by one-over-T scales every gap uniformly. When T is below one you are dividing by something less than one, which blows the gaps up, the exponential rewards the largest logit even harder, and the distribution sharpens toward the top token. When T is above one the gaps shrink and the distribution flattens toward uniform. The two limits are clean: T going to zero is one-hot, identical to greedy decoding, and T going to infinity is the uniform distribution. Crucial subtlety for the hard questions: temperature never changes which token is the argmax — it only rescales how peaked the distribution is around it.

The math · (b) nucleus set

Top-p: the smallest set that covers mass p

Sort tokens by probability, then take the smallest prefix whose mass reaches \(p\):

$$ V_p = \arg\min_{V'} |V'| \quad \text{s.t.} \sum_{i \in V'} p_i \ge p $$

\(p_i\) = probability of token \(i\) (post-temperature); \(p\) = the cumulative-mass threshold (e.g. 0.9).

\(V_p\) = the nucleus — the fewest top tokens whose probabilities sum to at least \(p\). Its size adapts to confidence: peaked dist → tiny \(V_p\); flat dist → large \(V_p\).

Then renormalize over the nucleus and sample: \( \tilde p_i = p_i / \sum_{j\in V_p} p_j \) for \(i\in V_p\), else 0.

Nucleus sampling, from Holtzman et al. 2019, is the workhorse default. The construction: sort the vocabulary by probability descending, walk down accumulating mass, and stop the instant the running sum reaches p. That prefix is the nucleus V-sub-p. The whole point versus top-k is that the set size is dynamic, not fixed: when the model is confident and one token sits at 0.95, the nucleus is just that one token; when the model is unsure and probability is spread over thousands of tokens, the nucleus grows to include many of them. After you truncate you must renormalize — divide the surviving probabilities by their own sum so they form a valid distribution again — otherwise you are sampling from something that does not sum to one. That renormalization step is identical for top-k, top-p, and min-p, and it is a common interview gotcha to forget it.

The math · (c) speculative acceptance

Speculative decoding samples exactly the target

A cheap draft proposes token \(x\) with prob \(q(x)\); the target gives \(p(x)\). Accept \(x\) with probability

$$ \alpha = \min\!\left(1,\ \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\right) $$

\(q(x)\) = draft-model probability of the proposed token; \(p(x)\) = target-model probability of the same token.

If \(p(x)\ge q(x)\) the draft under-proposed it → accept ( \(\alpha=1\) ). If \(p(x)<q(x)\) the draft over-proposed → accept only with prob \(p/q\).

On reject, resample from the normalized positive residual: \( p'(x) = \dfrac{\max(0,\ p(x)-q(x))}{\sum_{x'}\max(0,\ p(x')-q(x'))} \).

Result: the emitted token is distributed exactly as \(p\) — speculative decoding is lossless, only faster.

This is the most-asked hard question on the topic, so the math has to be airtight. A small fast draft model proposes a token with probability q; the big target model scores that same token with probability p. The accept rule is min of one and p-over-q. Intuition: if the target likes the token at least as much as the draft did, p-over-q is at least one, so you accept outright. If the target likes it less than the draft did, you accept only proportionally, with probability p-over-q, which trims the over-eager draft proposals back down. When you reject, you do not just resample from p — that would double-count the mass you already accepted. Instead you sample from the normalized positive part of (p minus q): take p-minus-q, clip negatives to zero, renormalize, and draw from that residual. The beautiful result, provable in a couple of lines, is that the token you finally emit is distributed exactly according to the target p. So speculative decoding changes nothing about the output distribution — it is a pure latency optimization. The speedup comes entirely from verifying several draft tokens in one target forward pass; acceptance rate times draft cost determines the win.

In code · one decode step

Penalty → temperature → top-k → top-p → sample

import torch

def decode_step(logits, prev_ids, temperature=0.8, top_k=50,
                top_p=0.9, rep_penalty=1.1):
    # logits: (vocab,) raw scores for the NEXT token
    logits = logits.clone()

    # 1) REPETITION PENALTY: push down tokens already generated
    for t in set(prev_ids.tolist()):
        if logits[t] > 0:
            logits[t] /= rep_penalty      # sign-aware: divide if >0
        else:
            logits[t] *= rep_penalty      # multiply (more negative) if <=0

    # 2) TEMPERATURE: scale logits BEFORE softmax
    logits = logits / max(temperature, 1e-6)

    # 3) TOP-K TRUNCATION: keep only k largest logits
    if top_k > 0:
        kth = torch.topk(logits, top_k).values[-1]
        logits[logits < kth] = float("-inf")

    # 4) TOP-P (NUCLEUS) TRUNCATION
    probs = torch.softmax(logits, dim=-1)
    sp, idx = torch.sort(probs, descending=True)
    cum = torch.cumsum(sp, dim=-1)
    cutoff = cum > top_p                   # tokens past the mass p
    cutoff[1:] = cutoff[:-1].clone()       # shift: always keep 1st
    cutoff[0] = False
    probs[idx[cutoff]] = 0.0               # drop the tail

    # 5) RENORMALIZE + SAMPLE
    probs = probs / probs.sum()            # back to a valid distribution
    return torch.multinomial(probs, num_samples=1)
Walk the highlighted blocks in order, because order is the whole point. Block one, repetition penalty: for every token already in the context, push its logit toward less likely — and note the sign-aware trick, you divide a positive logit by the penalty but multiply a negative one, because naively dividing a negative logit would make it larger and reward repetition, the opposite of what you want. Block two, temperature, applied to logits before softmax. Blocks three and four are the truncation steps the line-highlight calls out: top-k masks everything below the k-th largest logit to negative infinity; then top-p sorts the resulting probabilities, accumulates mass, and zeroes out the tail beyond p — with that shift so the single most likely token is never dropped even if it alone exceeds p. Block five is the step people forget: renormalize so the surviving probabilities sum to one, then draw with multinomial. If you skip the renormalize, multinomial still works because it normalizes internally, but writing it makes the math explicit and avoids surprises if you switch samplers.

In code · notes on order & edge cases

Why this order, and the gotchas

  • Penalty first: it edits raw logits; doing it after temperature would entangle the two knobs.
  • Temperature before truncation: T reshapes the distribution that top-k/top-p then measure — flip the order and the same p keeps a different set.
  • Always keep the top token in nucleus (cutoff[0]=False): if one token alone exceeds p, you must still allow it.
  • top_k and top_p compose: applying both = intersection of the two surviving sets, then renormalize once.
  • Greedy = argmax, not T=0 in this formula — division by zero; libraries special-case T=0 to argmax.
This slide answers the "does order of operations matter" interview question with a yes and the reasons. Repetition penalty operates on raw logits, so it goes first; if you applied it after temperature the penalty strength would silently depend on T. Temperature must come before truncation because top-k and top-p measure the distribution — if you truncate first and then heat, the same nominal p value selects a different set, which is a real source of "why did changing temperature change my nucleus" confusion. The keep-the-top-token guard in nucleus is essential: when the argmax token already carries more than p of the mass, the naive cumulative-sum cutoff would drop it, leaving you sampling from the tail only. When you stack top-k and top-p you keep the intersection and renormalize exactly once at the end. And the practical note: you can't literally pass T equal to zero into the softmax formula, it divides by zero, so every library detects T equal zero and routes to a plain argmax — that is what "temperature 0" actually means in practice.

Now run the four decoders on those identical numbers. Greedy is trivial: argmax, it emits " the" every single time, and because the context never changes its mind that is exactly how greedy falls into repetition loops. Temperature point-seven divides the logits by point-seven, which magnifies every gap by about one-point-four times, so the ranking is unchanged but the peak sharpens — " the" climbs from sixty-one to roughly seventy-two percent and the tail thins; this is the safe-creativity setting. Temperature one-point-five does the opposite, shrinking the gaps, so " the" drops to about forty-four percent and " zebra" climbs from a fifth of a percent toward a full percent — you have bought surprise at the cost of coherence. Top-p at nine-tenths walks the cumulative sum: point-six-one, then point-eight-three, then point-nine-one which crosses the threshold, so the nucleus is exactly the top three tokens, " the", " a", " cat"; " dog", " run", and " zebra" are dropped to zero, and you renormalize the survivors to roughly point-six-seven, point-two-four, point-zero-nine before sampling. The closing point is the one interviewers want: the temperature methods reshape but keep all tokens, while top-p truncates — and crucially the nucleus size is adaptive, so on a flatter distribution top-p would have kept many more than three.

This is the cheat-sheet interviewers love. Walk the five branches. Factual QA and extraction: you want the single best-supported answer and reproducibility, so greedy or temperature near zero. Structured/JSON: low temperature plus constrained decoding so the output is valid by construction. Creative and brainstorming: crank temperature to roughly 0.8–1.0 with top-p around 0.9 for variety. Need speed: speculative decoding, which is exact — same output distribution, just fewer expensive target passes. Need the best of many: sample N candidates and rerank with a verifier or take a self-consistency majority vote. The meta-point worth repeating out loud: pick the decoder from the task's tolerance for diversity versus its demand for correctness.

Every knob tunes one axis: coherence & factuality ↔ diversity & creativity.

The whole panel of sampling knobs navigates one trade-off — coherence and factuality on one side, diversity and creativity on the other. Two failure directions, two opposite responses. If it's boringly repetitive, you're too conservative: raise temperature, widen top-p or top-k, and add a frequency or presence penalty (or no-repeat-n-gram) to break loops — start with the penalty since repetition is the specific symptom. If it's creative but incoherent or off-topic, you're too permissive: lower temperature, shrink top-p/top-k or switch to min-p to clip the noisy tail, and check that a too-strong penalty isn't forcing weird word choices. Tune one knob at a time and incrementally, or you'll overshoot to the opposite failure.

Pitfalls · misconceptions

People think X, but actually Y (1/2)

⚠ Pitfall
  • Think: temperature 0 is bitwise-deterministic. Actually: on GPU, floating-point reduction order, batch size, and kernel choice can flip a near-tie argmax — same prompt, different token.
  • Think: top_k=1 is something other than greedy. Actually: keeping one token then "sampling" is exactly argmax — greedy decoding by another name.
  • Think: raising temperature can change the chosen token. Actually: temperature is a monotonic, order-preserving rescale — it never changes which token has the highest probability (the argmax).
First three. One: "temperature 0 guarantees identical bits every run." It does not on GPU. Floating-point addition is non-associative, so the reduction order in matmuls changes the low-order bits of the logits; different batch sizes/padding and kernel autotuning shift it too. When two top logits are nearly tied, those tiny differences can flip the argmax and emit a different token even at temperature 0. Two: top_k=1 keeps only the single highest-probability token, renormalizes to mass 1, and "samples" — that's literally argmax, i.e. greedy. Three: temperature divides every logit by the same T, which only compresses or expands the gaps between logits; the ordering never changes, so the top token stays the top token for any finite positive T. Temperature changes the odds of the runners-up, never the winner of the argmax.

Pitfalls · misconceptions

People think X, but actually Y (2/2)

⚠ Pitfall
  • Think: a strong repetition penalty is always safe. Actually: it's blind to legitimate structure — it breaks code indentation, list bullets, and necessary function words.
  • Think: constrained decoding guarantees a correct answer. Actually: it guarantees syntax — valid JSON can still carry hallucinated or wrong field values.
  • Think: beam search is the best decoder everywhere. Actually: it hurts open-ended generation — maximizing likelihood lands on bland, repetitive, degenerate text (the "likelihood trap").
Three more. One: an aggressive repetition penalty is blind to structure that should repeat — code needs repeated indentation and keywords, lists need repeated bullets/numbers, prose reuses "the" and "and." Push it too hard and you get broken syntax and awkward phrasing because the model is forced to avoid tokens it genuinely needs. Two: grammar/JSON-constrained decoding masks invalid tokens, so the output always parses — but syntactic validity is not semantic correctness; the values can be hallucinated, mistyped-but-plausible, or factually wrong, and forcing structure can even crowd out chain-of-thought. Three: beam search shines in machine translation, where there's essentially one correct rendering, but in open-ended generation the single most-likely sequence is bland and repetitive — the likelihood trap says human-quality text sits in a moderate-probability band, not at the maximum, so sampling beats search there.

What's new

Decoding is an active research frontier

🚀 What's new
  • Speculative decoding went mainstream. A small draft proposes, the big target verifies in parallel — exact same distribution, 2–3× faster. Now standard in vLLM, TensorRT-LLM, llama.cpp.
  • Self-drafting beats separate drafts. Medusa adds extra heads to the model itself; EAGLE / EAGLE-2 draft at the feature level with dynamic trees — no second model to host.
  • Better truncation samplers. min-p gates relative to the top token, staying coherent at higher temperatures than top-p.
  • Degeneration-aware decoders. Contrastive search and Mirostat fight repetition/blandness without pure likelihood maximization.
Where decoding is right now. Speculative decoding is the headline win — a cheap draft model proposes a block of tokens, the big target verifies them all in one parallel pass, and a rejection-sampling correction makes it provably exact (same output distribution as the target). It's shipped in every major serving stack for a 2–3× latency cut. The frontier moved to self-drafting: Medusa bolts extra prediction heads onto the target model so there's no separate draft to host; EAGLE and EAGLE-2 draft at the hidden-feature level with dynamic draft trees, pushing acceptance rates higher still. On the sampling side, min-p anchors its threshold to the top token's probability, which stays coherent at higher temperatures than top-p — useful for creative work. And degeneration-aware decoders — contrastive search (a degeneration penalty over the model's own representations) and Mirostat (feedback control on surprisal) — attack repetition and blandness directly instead of just maximizing likelihood.

Practice — click a question to reveal the answer

Full bank: 10 · Decoding & Sampling — from the answer key, condensed.

  • What is greedy decoding, and why doesn't it guarantee the globally most probable sequence? Easy▸ reveal
    Greedy picks the argmax token at each step — locally optimal, no lookahead. A high-probability first token can lead into a region where every continuation is poor, while a slightly lower-probability start opens a higher-probability overall path. The true global argmax is intractable, so greedy is just a cheap heuristic. Beam search is also a heuristic, not a global optimizer.
  • A teammate sets top_k=1. What decoding strategy did they reproduce? Easy▸ reveal
    Greedy decoding. Keeping only the single highest-probability token and "sampling" from a one-element set is identical to taking the argmax every step.
  • What does temperature do, and where in the pipeline is it applied? Easy▸ reveal
    Temperature rescales the distribution's peakiness: T<1 sharpens (more deterministic), T>1 flattens (more random). It divides the logits by T before softmax (z_i/T), not the output probabilities. It scales logits before softmax, not probabilities after.
  • Explain top-p (nucleus) sampling and its advantage over top-k when confidence varies. Med▸ reveal
    Sort tokens by probability, accumulate until cumulative mass reaches p (e.g. 0.9), keep that nucleus, renormalize, sample. The set size is dynamic: small when the model is peaked, large when uncertain. A fixed k can't adapt — too permissive on peaked steps, too restrictive on flat ones.
  • What is min-p sampling, and why is it robust across peaked vs. flat distributions? Med▸ reveal
    Keep every token with probability ≥ p_min × P(top token), then renormalize. Because the bar is anchored to the top token, a confident step sets a high absolute cutoff (few junk tokens survive) and a flat step lowers it (generation isn't strangled). That relative gate handles both shapes, allowing higher usable temperatures than top-p.
  • After truncating to a top-k / nucleus set, what must you do before sampling, and why? Med▸ reveal
    Renormalize the survivors so they sum to 1 (divide each by the kept sum). After zeroing the discarded tokens the remaining mass is <1, so the multinomial sampler would misbehave or implicitly reserve mass for dropped tokens. Order matters: usual pipeline is temperature → truncate → renormalize → sample.
  • Why can an aggressive repetition penalty degrade quality? Med▸ reveal
    It's blind to legitimate repetition. Code needs repeated indentation/keywords, lists need repeated bullets, prose reuses function words. Penalizing all previously-seen tokens too hard breaks indentation and syntax and produces awkward phrasing the model was right to repeat.
  • When you force valid JSON via grammar-constrained decoding, what failure modes remain? Med▸ reveal
    Syntactic validity ≠ semantic correctness. The JSON can still hold hallucinated or wrong field values, omit needed info, or be plausibly-typed but factually wrong; forcing structure can even crowd out chain-of-thought. Validity guarantees shape, not truth.
  • Distinguish frequency penalty from presence penalty (OpenAI-style). Med▸ reveal
    Both subtract from a token's logit by prior occurrence. Frequency penalty scales with the count — grows with overuse. Presence penalty is a flat, one-time hit applied as soon as a token appears at all, nudging toward new tokens/topics. Frequency = count-scaled; presence = binary "has it appeared."
  • Explain speculative decoding: roles of draft and target, and does it change the output distribution? Hard▸ reveal
    A small draft proposes γ tokens; the large target scores them all in one parallel pass and accepts/rejects each via a rejection-sampling check, resampling from a corrected residual on the first reject. It's provably distribution-preserving — identical to sampling from the target. It only speeds things up; it is not a quality-for-speed approximation.
  • What determines speculative speedup, and how do Medusa / EAGLE extend the basic scheme? Hard▸ reveal
    Speedup grows with acceptance rate and block length γ, dragged down by draft cost — there's an optimal γ. Medusa adds extra prediction heads to the target itself (no separate draft) verified as a tree; EAGLE drafts at the feature level with dynamic draft trees, raising accepted tokens per pass.
  • Why does temperature 0 not guarantee identical outputs on GPU? Hard▸ reveal
    Floating-point addition is non-associative, so parallel reduction order changes the low-order bits of the logits; batch size/padding and kernel autotuning vary it too. When the top two logits are nearly tied, those tiny differences flip the argmax — different token despite greedy. Greedy is deterministic only modulo floating-point nondeterminism.
  • Why does beam search suit machine translation but hurt open-ended chat? Hard▸ reveal
    MT output is tightly constrained by the source — one high-probability correct rendering — so maximizing likelihood lands on it. Open-ended generation has many valid continuations; the single most-likely sequence is bland and repetitive (the "likelihood trap"). Human-quality text isn't max-probability text, so search optimizes the wrong objective there.
  • How do self-consistency and temperature interact for reasoning tasks? Med▸ reveal
    Self-consistency samples many chains at nonzero temperature and majority-votes the final answer. Temperature must be high enough for diverse paths — at T=0 all chains are identical and voting is pointless — but not so high they go incoherent; ~0.5–0.8 with top-p works well. Running self-consistency at T=0 makes the vote meaningless.
  • For factual QA vs. creative brainstorming, what decoding settings would you choose? Med▸ reveal
    Factual QA: favor determinism/faithfulness — T=0 (greedy) or very low (≤0.3) with tight top-p, optionally constrained output. Creative: favor diversity — T≈0.8–1.2, top-p≈0.9–0.95 (or min-p), modest repetition/frequency penalties; generating best-of-n helps surface interesting options.

Takeaways

What to remember

  • Logits → softmax → token. Every strategy is just a different way to pick from that distribution.
  • Greedy/beam maximize likelihood — great for one-answer tasks, degenerate for open-ended (the likelihood trap).
  • Temperature rescales gaps (before softmax); it never reorders the argmax. top_k=1 = greedy.
  • Truncation samplers: top-k (fixed), top-p (adaptive mass), min-p (top-relative) — then renormalize.
  • One trade-off: coherence & factuality ↔ diversity & creativity — the task picks the point.
  • Speculative decoding is free speed — exact distribution; Medusa/EAGLE remove the separate draft.
  • Determinism is fragile: only greedy is reproducible, and even then only modulo GPU floating-point order.
The recap as a through-line. The pipeline is always logits → softmax → token, and decoding is just how you pick. Likelihood maximizers (greedy, beam) win where there's one right answer and degenerate on open-ended text. Temperature only compresses or expands the logit gaps before softmax — it never reorders the top token — and top_k=1 is literally greedy. The truncation family is top-k (fixed cap), top-p (adaptive cumulative mass), and min-p (threshold relative to the top token), always followed by renormalization. Every knob navigates the same coherence-versus-diversity axis, and the task chooses where you sit. Speculative decoding buys speed for free because it's exact, and self-drafting (Medusa, EAGLE) drops the separate model. Finally, "deterministic" is fragile — only greedy, and even that only up to GPU floating-point reduction order.
