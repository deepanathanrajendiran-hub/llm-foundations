PHASE 2 · TRAINING & ALIGNMENT · MODULE 31

Synthetic Data & Data-Centric AI

When the web runs dry, models make their own training data — generation, self-improvement, verification, and the model-collapse risk you must engineer around.

←→ navigate · S speaker notes · O overview · F fullscreen · append ?print-pdf to export

Welcome. This is the module about what happens when the easy game — scrape more web, train a bigger model — stops working. For a decade that was the recipe, but high-quality human text is finite. The field's answer is synthetic data: have a capable model write the training data, then filter it down to the good stuff. The whole lecture turns on one sentence: generation is cheap, verification is the moat. We'll walk the full ladder of approaches classic → SOTA, do the math of model collapse, write the code, and end on the one engineering rule that keeps the whole thing from eating itself — accumulate, don't replace.

Three: the single most important concept — verification is what separates a guess from a label. Why it matters

The recipe just broke

Scrape webfinite + Bigger modelmore compute → Better LLM...until the text runs out
  • For a decade: scrape more web, train a bigger model.
  • But usable human-written text is finite — projected exhaustion in the late 2020s.
  • That ceiling is the "data wall."
Scaling laws say loss keeps falling as you add data and parameters together — but they assume you have the data. Villalobos et al. (2022) estimated the stock of high-quality public text and projected we'd consume the usable stock within a few years. Appetite for tokens grows faster than the supply of good text. So the lever everyone reached for is: stop finding more human text, start making it. That's why this module sits right after pretraining and fine-tuning in the stack — it's the data-supply problem for every training stage downstream.

Where it fits in the LLM stack

Data-centric vs model-centric AI

Model-centric
  • Hold data fixed
  • Tune architecture, optimizer, scale
Data-centric
  • Hold model fixed
  • Improve data: quality, diversity, mixture, labels

Empirically: on a fixed compute budget, better data often beats another architectural tweak.

The big mental shift in this module: most ML training treats data as a given and optimizes the model. Data-centric AI flips the lever — freeze the model, and make the data better. Synthetic data is the most aggressive form of that, because you're literally manufacturing the data. Andrew Ng popularized "data-centric AI" as a discipline. The empirical punchline — that curating data beats tweaking architecture on a fixed budget — is exactly what the phi "textbooks" line and LIMA demonstrated, which we'll cover in the approaches section.

Intuition

The master chef analogy

  • A master chef (strong teacher model) can't clone themselves...
  • ...but can write thousands of annotated recipes for apprentices (the student).
  • The trick isn't the writing — it's tasting every dish before it goes in the cookbook (verification).
  • A recipe nobody tasted is a guess; one checked in a real kitchen is a label.
  • Apprentices who only cook from apprentice cookbooks drift into a bland average → model collapse.
I lean on this analogy the whole lecture. The chef is the teacher model; the apprentices are the students. Writing recipes is generation — cheap and abundant. Tasting is verification — the expensive, decisive step. The key insight is the difference between a guess and a label: a fluent, confident, wrong solution is a guess that looks like a label; only a real check turns it into a label. And the cautionary tale — apprentices cooking only from apprentice books, never tasting real food again — is model collapse: the cuisine slowly forgets its rare dishes and converges to a bland average. Hold onto these three: generation = writing, verification = tasting, collapse = inbreeding.

This is Figure 1 from the source. Read it left to right: the finite web is the starting fuel; a teacher (or the model itself) generates raw candidates that are noisy and redundant; then the three filters — verify, filter, dedup — keep only the trustworthy ones; and finally you mix with real data before training. The lecture's whole thesis is that everyone fixates on the generation box (B) when the leverage is entirely in boxes D and E. A weak filter lets garbage in; dropping the real data in box E causes collapse. Generation is a solved, cheap problem. Verification and mixture are where the engineering lives.

How it works

Three moving parts — in this order

  1. Generate. Prompt a model; for a prompt x, sample k completions y₁…y_k at temperature for diversity.
  2. Verify / filter. Decide which candidates are actually correct. Gold standard: a hard verifier v(x,y)∈{0,1}.
  3. Retrain. Fine-tune on the accepted set; optionally repeat — new outputs become next round's candidates.
Three parts, and the order matters. Generate: don't take the greedy answer — sample k completions with temperature so you get diversity to filter from. Verify: this is the crux. The gold standard is a hard verifier that returns 0 or 1 — run the code against unit tests, execute or symbolically check the math, validate JSON against a schema. Where no hard check exists, you fall back to weaker signals: majority-vote self-consistency, a reward model, rubric-graded LLM-as-judge, or human spot-checks — in roughly that order of reliability. Retrain: SFT on the accepted set, then optionally loop. The loop is what makes this a flywheel.

How it works · verification ladder

How do you know a candidate is good?

Hard verifierrun tests · solver · schema ▸ Self-consistencymajority vote ▸ Reward modellearned score ▸ LLM-as-judgerubric grading ▸ Humanspot-check

Left = most reliable, scales best. Prefer the hardest verifier your domain allows.

Think of verification as a ladder from most trustworthy to least. A hard verifier — execution, a symbolic solver, schema validation — is a ground-truth oracle, not a guess; it's why math and code are the home turf of synthetic data. When you can't run the answer, self-consistency (sample several solutions, require a majority to agree) is a cheap proxy. Below that, a learned reward model. Below that, an LLM grading against a rubric — useful but biased (we'll cover the biases later). Humans are the most reliable for taste but don't scale. The engineering instinct: always climb as high up this ladder as your domain permits.

How it works · the flywheel

The self-improvement data flywheel

One full turn: generate → verify → accumulate → retrain → repeat. Watch the verified yield climb.

This is the animation to teach off. Walk through one full turn: start from seed prompts; the model generates many candidates per prompt at high temperature; the verifier keeps only the correct ones — this is the crux, the step that makes synthetic data safe; you accumulate the verified samples onto the real data rather than replacing it; then retrain on the enlarged set and the model improves a little. The quality meter on the right is the payoff: because the model is now slightly better, its next batch of candidates is slightly better, so more pass the filter — the loop ratchets upward. This is exactly the STaR / rejection-sampling pattern behind modern reasoning models. The two things that make or break it: verifier reliability (weak verifier → it learns garbage) and diversity preservation (no fresh real data + no dedup → the distribution narrows every round, the seed of collapse). Hold both and it spins; lose either and it grinds the distribution into mush.

How it works · what breaks it

Two things make or break the loop

Verifier reliability
  • Weak verifier → garbage passes the filter
  • The model then learns the garbage
Diversity preservation
  • No fresh real data + no dedup
  • Distribution narrows every round → collapse

Hold both and the flywheel spins. Lose either and it grinds the distribution into mush.

Make this concrete. Verifier reliability: if your "verifier" is just an LLM judge that approves fluent-looking answers, wrong-but-confident solutions get accepted, you fine-tune on them, and the model gets more confidently wrong each round — the loop amplifies the verifier's blind spots. Diversity preservation: even with a perfect verifier, if every round you train only on the model's own narrow output and never inject fresh real data or dedup, the distribution gets tighter and tighter — you're sampling from your own sampling. These two failure modes are the bridge into the rest of the lecture: verification quality drives the approaches section, diversity preservation drives the model-collapse section.

The approaches · landscape

The full ladder, classic → SOTA

Human web→ Augment→ Distill→ Self-Instruct→ Evol-Instruct→ Persona→ Textbooks→ STaR→ Self-reward→ Verifiable→ RLAIF

Each rung solves a specific weakness of the one before it. Let's climb it — for each: what / how / pros & cons, then a step-by-step animation.

This is the heart of the deck and where interviewers spend the most time — "why did we move from X to Y" gets asked constantly. The ladder: start with the human-data baseline and its data wall; augmentation remixes what you have; distillation borrows a stronger teacher; Self-Instruct lets the model bootstrap its own tasks; Evol-Instruct hardens them; personas force diversity; "textbooks" pushes quality over quantity; STaR closes the verify-retrain loop; self-rewarding removes the human judge; verifiable data is the gold-standard oracle; RLAIF/Constitutional scales alignment data. Each exists because the previous one had a gap. I teach each with: what it is, how it works step by step, what it solves, pros/cons, and crucially how it differs from the rung below.

Approaches · 1 of 11

Human-curated web data baseline

What: the default pretraining corpus — filtered web crawl, books, code, Wikipedia. How: scrape → language-ID → dedup → quality-filter → mix domains. Solves: nothing to generate — real human text, maximally diverse & grounded.

Pros
  • Real, diverse, grounded in the world
  • No generator bias / recursion risk
Cons
  • Finite — exhaustion projected late 2020s (the data wall)
  • Noisy; now polluted with LLM spam

vs previous: this is the "before" — the baseline every synthetic method tries to extend or replace.

Read more ↗

Common Crawl, books, code, Wikipedia — heavily filtered, but real. The virtue is genuine human text: maximally diverse, grounded, with no recursion risk and no generator bias. The fatal limitation is supply — finite, growing slower than our appetite, and increasingly polluted with LLM-generated spam (an accidental collapse risk in the wild). Reference: Villalobos et al., "Will we run out of data?" — the paper that framed the data wall.

How it works · Human-curated web data

Human-curated web data — step by step

Crawlraw web → Lang-IDkeep target → DedupMinHash → Quality filterspam out → Mix domainscorpus
  1. Crawl — pull raw bytes from the open web (Common Crawl, books, code).
  2. Language-ID — drop garbled or unwanted languages.
  3. Dedup — exact then near-dedup (MinHash/LSH) removes copies.
  4. Quality-filter — heuristics + classifier strip boilerplate & spam.
  5. Mix domains — blend web/code/books/math into the final corpus.
This is the standard pretraining hygiene pipeline. Crawl is cheap and abundant but raw; the value is in the filters. Language-ID first so downstream tools see clean text; dedup spreads gradient budget across unique content and cuts memorization; quality filtering removes the SEO/boilerplate junk; and the final mix decides capability trade-offs (more code → more reasoning). Everything that follows in this section is about what to do once this real supply runs thin.

Approaches · 2 of 11

Augmentation — back-translation & rephrase cheap

What: the oldest synthetic data — transform existing text into new variants. How: back-translation (EN→DE→EN) for paraphrases; LLM rephrasing into cleaner prose / Q&A; synonym & word-order perturbation. Solves: boosts diversity & effective token count from text you already have.

Pros
  • Cheap; stays near the real distribution
  • Great for low-resource languages
Cons
  • No new knowledge — just remixing
  • Paraphrase artifacts; inflates token count

vs previous: doesn't find new text like the baseline — it remixes existing text into variants.

Read more ↗

The bridge from baseline to synthetic. Back-translation — push English to German and back — is the classic NLP trick; it yields paraphrases that stay close to the real distribution. Modern LLM rephrasing rewrites messy web docs into clean prose or Q&A. The crucial limitation, and the reason we keep climbing: augmentation only recombines knowledge already in the source. It can't add a fact the corpus lacked, can amplify the source's biases, and risks inflating the apparent token count without adding real value.

How it works · Augmentation

Augmentation — step by step

Sourcereal doc → EN→DEtranslate out → DE→ENback → Paraphrasenew variant → Filterdrop bad
  1. Start from a real source document.
  2. Translate out to another language (EN→DE).
  3. Translate back (DE→EN) — wording shifts, meaning stays.
  4. Result is a paraphrase: a new training variant grounded in real content.
  5. Filter out broken or low-fidelity round-trips before keeping.
Walk the round-trip. Because the pivot language forces different surface forms, you get genuine paraphrase diversity while the semantics stay anchored to real data — that's why the risk is milder than free generation. LLM rephrasing is the modern equivalent: "rewrite this messy page as a clean tutorial." Always filter: bad translations or hallucinated rewrites slip in, and you do not want to inflate token counts with garbage that merely looks new.

Approaches · 3 of 11

Distillation from a stronger teacher SFT data

What: a more capable model writes instruction-tuning demos for a weaker student (Alpaca-style). How: prompt teacher → collect responses → fine-tune student on (instruction, response) pairs. Solves: injects new capability fast — Alpaca cloned much of a strong model on ~52K examples for a few hundred dollars.

Pros
  • Very cheap, very effective for SFT
  • Transfers format / style fast
Cons
  • Inherits teacher errors & biases ("style collapse")
  • ToS / licensing risk; capped near the teacher

vs previous: augmentation only remixed source text; distillation injects new capability from a smarter model.

Read more ↗

Now we cross from remixing to borrowing capability. A strong teacher writes the demonstrations, the weak student learns from them. Stanford Alpaca is the famous proof — ~52K instruction-response pairs from a strong model, fine-tuned into a small model for a few hundred dollars, and it suddenly "talked like" the big one. The catch that motivates the next rung: the student inherits the teacher's style, biases, and factual errors, and can never exceed the teacher. There's also a real ToS/licensing landmine in distilling from commercial models. The field wanted a way to bootstrap without a fixed external teacher.

How it works · Distillation

Distillation — step by step

Instructionsprompts → Teacherstrong model → (instr, resp)~52K pairs → SFTfine-tune → Studentmimics teacher
  1. Collect a pool of instructions (prompts to teach against).
  2. Prompt a strong teacher to answer each one.
  3. Pair them into (instruction, response) demonstrations.
  4. Supervised fine-tune the small student on those pairs.
  5. Student now imitates the teacher's behavior — capped near it.
This is sequence-level imitation: the student trains on the teacher's sampled text, not its full logit distribution (that would be classic "dark-knowledge" distillation). It works because clean, well-structured demonstrations are hard to learn from raw web text but easy to copy. The dotted ceiling at the end is the key teaching point — absent verification or selection, the student is bounded by the teacher and inherits its blind spots.

Approaches · 4 of 11

Self-Instruct bootstrapping

What: a model bootstraps its own instruction data from a tiny seed set (Wang et al., 2022). How: seed ~175 tasks → prompt model to invent new instructions in-context → generate input–output instances → filter near-dups & invalid ones → loop. Solves: removes the fixed external teacher — a tiny seed grows into tens of thousands of tasks.

Pros
  • Tiny seed → tens of thousands of tasks
  • Diversity (ROUGE) filter built in
Cons
  • Quality bounded by the generator itself
  • Noisy instances; needs aggressive filtering

vs previous: distillation needs a stronger external teacher; Self-Instruct lets the model bootstrap from a tiny seed instead.

Read more ↗

The template the whole field copied — generate → filter → loop. The clever move over distillation: no fixed external teacher; the model bootstraps from ~175 hand-written seed tasks. It prompts itself to invent new instructions, decides whether each is a classification task (which needs different instance generation), produces input-output pairs, then — critically — filters near-duplicates using ROUGE overlap plus invalid ones. The built-in diversity filter is what keeps it from collapsing onto one topic. Limitation: quality is still bounded by the generator, and raw instances are noisy, so the filter must be aggressive.

How it works · Self-Instruct

Self-Instruct — step by step

Seed pool~175 tasks → Generatenew instr → Instancesinput/output → ROUGE filterdrop dups ↺ Grow poolloop back
  1. Start with ~175 hand-written seed tasks.
  2. Prompt the model (seeds in-context) to generate new instructions.
  3. Classify each (classification-or-not) → generate input–output instances.
  4. Filter: drop near-dups (ROUGE overlap) & invalid ones.
  5. Add survivors to the pool → loop, so coverage compounds.
Drive the loop off the widget. The seed is the only human input — everything after is bootstrapped. The classification branch matters: classification tasks generate the output first then the input, others go input→output. The ROUGE filter is the load-bearing step; without it the pool collapses onto redundant near-duplicate instructions. Each accepted item conditions the next round, so coverage grows — a self-amplifying instruction set built from almost nothing.

Approaches · 5 of 11

Evol-Instruct / WizardLM difficulty control

What: evolve simple instructions into harder, more varied ones (Xu et al., 2023). How: in-depth evolution adds constraints / reasoning steps / complicates input; in-breadth evolution mutates into a rarer topic; then eliminate failed or trivial evolutions. Solves: Self-Instruct gave breadth but not difficulty control — too many easy questions.

Pros
  • Dials difficulty up on demand
  • Broadens topic coverage
Cons
  • Over-evolution → unnatural / unanswerable
  • Controls the question, not answer correctness — still needs a verifier

vs previous: Self-Instruct's generation is flat in difficulty; Evol-Instruct adds a controllable complexity gradient.

Read more ↗

Self-Instruct gave breadth but not control over difficulty — you got a lot of easy questions. Evol-Instruct fixes that by repeatedly rewriting each seed along two axes. In-depth makes a question harder: add constraints, deepen reasoning, increase steps, complicate the input. In-breadth mutates into a brand-new, rarer topic for coverage. After each evolution you eliminate the ones that failed or became trivial. WizardLM, trained on Evol-Instruct data, beat models trained on plain Self-Instruct data. The danger is over-evolution — push too hard and prompts become unnatural or unanswerable — and note this controls question difficulty, not answer correctness; you still need a verifier for that.

How it works · Evol-Instruct

Evol-Instruct — step by step

Simple instreasy seed → In-depth+ constraints → In-breadthnew topic → Eliminatedrop trivial/fail → Harder setWizardLM
  1. Take a simple seed instruction.
  2. In-depth: add constraints, deepen reasoning, complicate the input → harder.
  3. In-breadth: mutate it into a brand-new, rarer topic → wider.
  4. Eliminate evolutions that failed or stayed trivial.
  5. Result: a difficulty-graded, broadly-covering instruction set (WizardLM).
Two orthogonal axes — depth (harder) and breadth (more topics) — applied repeatedly, with an elimination step between rounds. Push the depth too hard and you get over-evolution: unnatural or unanswerable prompts, the main failure mode. Critically, evolving the question does nothing for the answer's correctness — a harder question with a wrong reference answer is worse than useless, which is exactly why a verifier (next rungs) becomes the missing piece.

Approaches · 6 of 11

Persona-driven / templated generation diversity

What: condition generation on a persona or structured template to force breadth. How: sample a persona ("a tax auditor," "a 9-year-old," "a Rust engineer") from a large pool, or fill schema slots and let the LLM realize them. Solves: free-form prompting collapses onto the model's favorite topics & one default voice.

Pros
  • Strong, controllable diversity
  • Templates enforce structural validity
Cons
  • Persona pool must itself be diverse
  • Templates can feel rigid / unnatural

vs previous: Evol-Instruct controls difficulty; personas/templates control diversity — they sit alongside, not replace, it.

Read more ↗

A diversity tool that sits alongside Self-Instruct and Evol-Instruct. The problem it solves: say "generate a question" and the model collapses onto its favorite handful of topics and its one default voice. Conditioning on a persona — sampled from a pool of thousands of roles (Persona Hub scales this to ~1B personas) — spreads coverage across viewpoints and styles. Templates do the same for structured data: fill schema slots and let the LLM realize them, which also enforces structural validity, essential for function-call JSON or tabular rows. The meta-catch: your persona pool or template set must itself be diverse, or you've just moved the bottleneck up a level.

How it works · Persona / templated

Persona / templated — step by step

Persona pool1000s of roles → Sampleone persona → Condition+ template slots → Generatein that voice → Validateschema check
  1. Maintain a large pool of distinct personas (and/or schema templates).
  2. Sample one persona per example for breadth.
  3. Condition the prompt on that persona, filling any template slots.
  4. Generate — each persona writes from a different viewpoint & style.
  5. Validate structured output against the schema; reject invalid rows.
The trick is that diversity comes from the conditioning, not from asking the model to "be creative." Sampling a different persona per example spreads topical and stylistic coverage cheaply and breaks single-teacher style collapse. For structured data, the template slots plus a schema validator give you both diversity and guaranteed validity — JSON-schema/grammar-constrained decoding is the production version. Remember the meta-bottleneck: a bland persona pool produces bland diversity.

Approaches · 7 of 11

"Textbooks Are All You Need" — phi quality-first

What: a little textbook-quality synthetic data beats a mountain of raw scrape (Gunasekar et al., 2023). How: generate clean, pedagogical content (textbooks + exercises); train phi-1 (1.3B) on it. Solves: shifts the goal from more data to better data — quality & information density > raw quantity.

Pros
  • Tiny models punch far above weight
  • Dense, clean, low-noise signal
Cons
  • Narrow coverage; teacher-bounded
  • Hard to audit for contamination

vs previous: everything before pushed for more or more-diverse data; phi pushes for denser, cleaner data instead.

Read more ↗

A philosophical shift, not just a technique. Gunasekar et al. generated clean, pedagogical, textbook-style content with exercises and trained phi-1, a 1.3B model, reaching strong coding ability at a tiny fraction of the usual data and parameters. The core hypothesis — quality and information density matter more than raw quantity — pairs with LIMA (≈1K curated examples rival massive SFT). Honest limits: the generator is a strong teacher, so it's partly distillation; coverage can be narrow; and because the teacher may have seen benchmarks, contamination is hard to audit.

How it works · Textbooks (phi)

Textbooks (phi) — step by step

Topic spec+ persona → Generatetextbook prose → Exercises+ solutions → Filterkeep dense/clean → Train phi-11.3B
  1. Specify a topic (and persona) to steer coverage.
  2. Generate clean, pedagogical "textbook" prose for it.
  3. Generate matching exercises with worked solutions.
  4. Filter for density & clarity — keep only high-signal docs.
  5. Train a tiny model (phi-1, 1.3B) — strong coding at a fraction of the data.
The mechanism is "manufacture a curriculum." Topic + persona steering gives coverage; the textbook+exercise format packs structured reasoning and worked solutions into every token, which is exactly the high-information-density signal the hypothesis bets on. The filter is what makes it "quality-first" — only the cleanest, densest docs survive. The punchline: a 1.3B model reaches coding ability that brute-force scrape needs far more data and parameters to match.

Approaches · 8 of 11

STaR / rejection-sampling fine-tuning on-policy

What: generate solutions, keep only the verified-correct ones, fine-tune, repeat (Zelikman et al., 2022; aka RFT). How: sample chains-of-thought for problems with known answers → keep traces whose final answer matches → rationalize the rest → retrain → loop. Solves: the central weakness of every rung above — label correctness, via a verifier.

Pros
  • On-policy: trains on the model's own correct reasoning
  • Verifier guarantees label correctness
Cons
  • Needs a reliable verifier / known answers
  • Can reinforce lucky-but-wrong reasoning

vs previous: textbooks/distillation trust the generator's output; STaR closes the loop with a verifier that filters to correct.

Read more ↗

The engine behind modern reasoning-model data pipelines. The big leap: it closes the loop with a verifier. For each problem with a known answer, sample several chains-of-thought, keep only the traces whose final answer matches, fine-tune on those. STaR's twist is rationalization — for problems the model can't solve, give it the answer as a hint and keep whatever rationale reaches it, so you still harvest a reasoning trace. Then loop. "On-policy" because you train on the model's own correct reasoning, not a teacher's. The danger is lucky-but-wrong traces — flawed reasoning that hits the right number — which get reinforced. This is the rejection-sampling math formalized in the math section.

How it works · STaR / RFT

STaR / RFT — step by step

Problemknown answer → Sample CoTk traces → Verifyanswer matches? → Keep correct+ rationalize ↺ Retrainloop
  1. For each problem with a known answer, sample several chains-of-thought.
  2. Verify: keep only traces whose final answer matches the known answer.
  3. Rationalization: for unsolved ones, give the answer as a hint, keep the rationale that reaches it.
  4. Retrain (SFT) on the accepted rationales.
  5. Loop — the better model samples better traces, so more pass.
Drive the flywheel off the widget — watch the yield meter climb. The verifier is the load-bearing step: on math/code it's near-perfect, turning a noisy generator into clean supervised data. Rationalization rescues hard problems by working backward from the answer, but it is also where lucky-but-wrong reasoning sneaks in, so process-level checks matter. Because each retrained model samples slightly better, a larger fraction passes the filter — the loop ratchets upward, the defining property of self-improvement.

Approaches · 9 of 11

Self-rewarding / self-play frontier

What: one model is both author and judge — generates responses and scores them to build its own DPO preference pairs (Yuan et al., 2024). How: generate several responses → same model ranks them → form chosen/rejected pairs → run DPO; the improved model judges better next round. Solves: removes even the external judge STaR needed.

Pros
  • No human/teacher labels for preferences
  • Reward model improves alongside the policy
Cons
  • Self-preference / verbosity bias; reward hacking
  • Drift if not anchored to real data & checks

vs previous: STaR needed a verifier or known answer; self-rewarding tries to remove even the external judge.

Read more ↗

The frontier of removing humans from the loop. One model plays both roles: generates several responses per prompt, ranks them itself with LLM-as-judge, forms chosen/rejected pairs, runs DPO — and because the policy improves, it becomes a better judge next round, a virtuous spiral. The danger is the whole story of the collapse section: a model judging itself can reward-hack its own biases — self-preference, verbosity. Guardrails matter: keep a real seed set, cap iterations, monitor the judge's calibration against gold, use external verifiers wherever possible. Without those anchors, self-judging drifts.

How it works · Self-rewarding

Self-rewarding — step by step

Promptone task → Generate kresponses → Self-judgesame model ranks → Pairschosen/rejected → DPObetter judge ↺
  1. Generate several responses for each prompt.
  2. The same model judges/ranks them (LLM-as-judge).
  3. Form chosen/rejected preference pairs from the ranking.
  4. Run DPO on the pairs to update the policy.
  5. The improved model is a better judge next round → loop (with guardrails).
The author-and-judge collapse into one model is the whole idea — and the whole danger. The spiral is virtuous when judgments track truth, but a model judging itself can reward-hack its own self-preference and verbosity biases, drifting away from real quality. The guardrails — real seed, capped iterations, gold-calibration of the judge, external verifiers where they exist — are what keep the loop from grinding into sycophantic, over-long outputs.

Approaches · 10 of 11

Verifiable / execution-checked data gold standard

What: for math & code you can run the answer — v(x,y) is a ground-truth oracle, not a guess.   1. Generate a candidate (program, proof/answer, or JSON object).

A perplexity filter asks "does it look right?"; execution asks "is it actually right?"

This slide makes "verification is the moat" concrete. The check is deterministic and cheap, so it scales without humans and produces near-perfect labels — the opposite of a fluency proxy like perplexity, which a confident lie passes. The honest caveats: it only applies where you can run the answer, weak test suites can be gamed (a wrong solution that passes thin tests), and whole regions of a domain may have no easy oracle.

The last rung handles the domain where you can't run the answer — open-ended alignment. RLAIF (Lee et al., 2023) replaces the human rater: an LLM compares two responses and picks the better, producing preference pairs for a reward model or DPO at near-human quality on some tasks but far cheaper. Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022) goes further: the model critiques and revises its own responses against a written "constitution" of principles, generating harmlessness data with zero human harm labels — also sparing annotators traumatic content. The recurring theme: in checkable domains use verifiers; in uncheckable ones use AI feedback, but audit the judge.

The math · (a) rejection sampling

STaR / rejection-sampling as a set

Sample from the current policy, keep only what the verifier accepts:

$$ \mathcal{D}^+ = \{(x,y) : y \sim \pi(\cdot \mid x),\; v(x,y)=1\} $$

\(\mathcal{D}^+\) = the accepted ("positive") set you train on; \(x\) = a prompt/problem; \(y\) = a sampled completion (CoT + answer).

\(\pi(\cdot\mid x)\) = the model's current sampling distribution given \(x\); \(v(x,y)\in\{0,1\}\) = the verifier — 1 if \(y\) is correct, 0 otherwise.

Keep a sample only when \(v=1\), run SFT on \(\mathcal{D}^+\), then repeat.

The whole STaR loop in one line. You're constructing a dataset by sampling completions y from the current policy π given prompt x, and admitting the pair (x,y) only when the verifier v returns 1. Everything that fails verification is thrown away — that's the "rejection" in rejection sampling. Then you do ordinary supervised fine-tuning on D-plus and repeat, because the updated policy samples slightly better completions next round. The elegance is that v converts a stochastic, noisy generator into a clean supervised signal — but only as reliable as v itself. On math/code v can be near-perfect; on open chat v is a biased judge, which is exactly why the loop is trustworthy in some domains and dangerous in others.

The math · (b) model collapse

Why "replace" shrinks the variance

Fit gen \(n\), then train gen \(n{+}1\) on \(M_n\) finite samples from that fit (replacing real data):

$$ \mathbb{E}\!\left[\sigma_{n+1}^{2}\right] = \sigma_n^{2}\Big(1 - \tfrac{1}{M_n}\Big) $$

\(\sigma_n^2\) = variance (spread) at generation \(n\); \(M_n\) = samples drawn to build gen \(n{+}1\); \(\mathbb{E}[\cdot]\) = expectation over the draw.

The biased MLE variance is \( \mathbb{E}[\sigma^2_{\text{MLE}}] = \sigma^2(1-\tfrac{1}{M}) \) — slightly smaller than truth.

The factor \((1-\tfrac{1}{M_n})<1\) ⇒ spread decays every round; iterate and \(\sigma_n^2 \to 0\) — tails forgotten, modes merge. Tail collapse.

This is the mathematical heart of model collapse, the most important equation in the deck. The intuition: when you estimate variance from a finite sample using maximum likelihood, you systematically underestimate it — the biased estimator is sigma-squared times (1 minus 1 over M). Now make that recursive: each generation fits the previous generation's finite output and replaces the real data. So the variance gets multiplied by a factor slightly below one, every single round. Multiply a number by 0.99 forever and it goes to zero. Concretely: the distribution narrows, the rare tails vanish first, the modes merge, and in the Gaussian-fit case it collapses to a single point. This is "tail collapse" — and notice the cause is specifically replacing real data. Keep real data in the mix and you break the recursion, which is the next equation's worth of intuition.

The math · (c) near-dedup

Jaccard similarity & MinHash

Represent each doc as a set of shingles (overlapping n-grams), measure overlap:

$$ J(A,B) = \frac{|A \cap B|}{|A \cup B|} $$

\(A,B\) = shingle sets of two docs (e.g. all 5-word windows); \(|A\cap B|\) = shingles shared; \(|A\cup B|\) = shingles in either. \(J\) ranges 0 (disjoint) → 1 (identical).

The MinHash trick: for a random hash permutation \(h\), \(\Pr[\min h(A) = \min h(B)] = J(A,B)\).

So comparing many MinHash signatures estimates \(J\) cheaply; LSH bands find pairs above a threshold without all-pairs comparison.

Dedup is the other half of preserving diversity, so here's the math behind near-dedup. Two documents are "near-duplicate" if their shingle sets — all overlapping 5-word windows, say — overlap a lot, which we quantify with Jaccard similarity: intersection over union, between 0 and 1. The problem is that comparing every pair of documents is quadratic and infeasible at corpus scale. The MinHash trick is beautiful: for a random hash permutation, the probability that two sets share the same minimum hash equals exactly their Jaccard similarity. So a short signature of 128 min-hashes estimates J cheaply, and LSH banding lets you bucket likely-similar docs so you only compare candidates, not all pairs. This is the standard near-dedup pipeline for both real and synthetic corpora.

In code · rejection sampling

One generate → verify → dedup round

def rejection_sampling_round(model, tokenizer, prompts, verify_fn,
                             k=8, temperature=0.8, seen_hashes=None):
    """One generate -> verify -> dedup round."""
    seen_hashes = seen_hashes if seen_hashes is not None else set()
    accepted = []
    for x in prompts:
        cands = [sample(model, tokenizer, x, temperature=temperature)
                 for _ in range(k)]                 # 1) sample k diverse
        for y in cands:
            if not verify_fn(x, y):                 # 2) HARD verify: v(x,y)==1?
                continue
            h = minhash_signature(y)                # 3) near-dedup
            key = tuple(h[:8])
            if key in seen_hashes:
                continue
            seen_hashes.add(key)
            accepted.append((x, y))
    return accepted
Walk the three numbered steps. Step 1: for each prompt sample k completions with temperature — diversity is the whole point, you want variety to filter from, not the single greedy answer. Step 2: the hard verify — verify_fn is your v(x,y); it runs the unit tests, checks the math, or validates the schema, and anything that fails is dropped with continue. Step 3: near-dedup — compute a MinHash signature and use the first few hashes as a cheap bucket key so you don't pile up identical solutions. Only survivors of all three make it into accepted. This is the literal implementation of the D-plus set equation from the math section. Note what's NOT here: there's no perplexity filter and no "looks good to me" — correctness is decided by execution, not fluency.

In code · the loop

The self-improvement loop & MinHash sketch

def self_improve(model, tokenizer, prompts, verify_fn, rounds=3):
    dataset, seen = [], set()
    for r in range(rounds):
        new = rejection_sampling_round(model, tokenizer, prompts,
                                       verify_fn, seen_hashes=seen)
        dataset += new                 # ACCUMULATE — don't replace
        sft(model, dataset)            # fine-tune on accepted set
        print(f"round {r}: +{len(new)} accepted, {len(dataset)} total")
    return model, dataset

def shingles(text, n=5):
    toks = text.split()
    return {" ".join(toks[i:i+n]) for i in range(len(toks) - n + 1)}

def is_near_dup(sig, buckets, bands=16):
    """Near-dup if ANY LSH band collides with a seen doc."""
    hit = False
    for b, key in enumerate(lsh_bands(sig, bands)):
        if key in buckets[b]: hit = True
        buckets[b].add(key)
    return hit
Top: the loop. Note the load-bearing line — dataset += new. We accumulate: the dataset grows each round, and we re-fine-tune on the whole accumulated set, never throwing away earlier data. This single design choice is what keeps the loop from collapsing, the code embodiment of "accumulate, don't replace." Each round prints how many new accepted samples we got; when that yield stops rising you stop. Bottom: the dedup primitives — shingles turns text into overlapping 5-grams, and is_near_dup checks whether any LSH band collides with a previously-seen doc, which catches high-Jaccard pairs cheaply. Together these two snippets are a minimal but real STaR pipeline.

Worked example

Build 1,000,000 verified math word problems

Goal: a training set where the answer label is trustworthy. Insight: over-generate and verify hard — verification, not generation, is the quality bottleneck.

  1. Seed / template. A few hundred human templates (rate, work, geometry, algebra) with parameter slots + a persona/topic pool for breadth.
  2. Generate ~3,000,000. Instantiate templates into NL problem + step-by-step solution ending in a machine-readable answer (#### 42), temperature for diversity.
The headline insight, repeated because it's the exam answer: verification is the bottleneck on quality, not generation, so you deliberately over-generate. Start from a few hundred human-written templates with parameter slots — rate problems, work problems, geometry, algebra — plus a pool of personas and topics for breadth. Then instantiate each template into a natural-language problem AND a step-by-step solution that ends in a machine-readable final answer, like the GSM8K "#### 42" convention. Generate roughly three million candidates with temperature so you have variety to filter. We're going to throw most of them away — that's fine and expected.

Worked example · the crux

Verify hard, then dedup & mix

  1. Verify (the crux). Parse to symbolic form / run the derivation; check the answer with SymPy. Then self-consistency: sample 5 solutions, require a majority to agree. Yield ~35% → ~1.05M.
  2. Dedup. MinHash-LSH near-dedup removes ~5% near-dup phrasings → ~1.0M unique, verified.
  3. Mix & ship. Blend with real human problems (don't train on synthetic alone — accumulate), hold out a contamination-checked test split, train.

Why this beats perplexity filtering: a perplexity filter asks "does it look like fluent math?" — a confidently-wrong solution scores great. Execution/symbolic checks ask "is the answer actually right?"

Step 3 is where the quality is made. For each candidate, parse the problem into symbolic/computational form (or run the model's own derivation as code) and check the stated answer with SymPy or a calculator. Then layer self-consistency on top: sample 5 independent solutions and require a majority to agree on the final number. A candidate must pass BOTH to survive. A plausible yield is about 35%, so 3M candidates give roughly 1.05M survivors. Step 4: MinHash-LSH dedup removes ~5% near-duplicate phrasings, landing at ~1.0M unique verified problems. Step 5: blend with real human problems — accumulate, never train on synthetic alone — and hold out a contamination-checked test split. The eqnote is the punchline of the whole worked example: perplexity filtering only checks fluency, and a confident lie is fluent. Execution checks correctness. On a checkable domain that converts a noisy generator into near-ground-truth labels — which is exactly why math and code are the home turf of synthetic data.

When to use what · standing rules

Two rules that always apply

  • Iterating a flywheel? Accumulate real + synthetic each round (never replace), cap rounds, monitor for collapse.
  • Choosing data proportions? Use a principled mixture search like DoReMi, not hand-tuned weights.
These two rules sit above the decision tree and apply no matter which generation method you picked. One: whenever you run an iterative loop, accumulate — keep the real data and all prior synthetic in the training mix, cap the number of rounds, and actively monitor diversity metrics for early signs of collapse. Two: when you're deciding how much weight to give each data source (web vs code vs your synthetic math), don't hand-tune; use a principled mixture optimizer like DoReMi, which learns domain weights that speed up pretraining. Hand-tuned mixture weights are a classic source of silent underperformance.

Pitfalls · misconceptions

People think X, but actually Y (1/2)

⚠ Pitfall
  • Think: synthetic data breaks the wall by adding knowledge. Actually: a generator mostly recycles what it knows — new facts come from the real world or verifiers/tools.
  • Think: training on your own outputs always degrades. Actually: the failure mode is replacing real data; if you accumulate, collapse is largely avoided.
  • Think: a stronger teacher can't cause collapse. Actually: distillation still narrows diversity & propagates blind spots — even good teachers degrade tails.
First three misconceptions. One — the biggest: people imagine synthetic data conjures new knowledge and breaks the data wall. It doesn't; a generator reorganizes and densifies what it already knows. New facts still come from the real world, from verifiers, or from tools. Synthetic data buys you signal density and coverage, not new ground truth. Two: "training on your own outputs is always poison" — false, and the nuance is the whole module: the poison is specifically replacing real data each generation. Accumulate and you're largely fine. Three: "use a strong enough teacher and you're safe from collapse" — no; distillation still narrows diversity and propagates the teacher's blind spots and any contamination, so even excellent teachers degrade the tails over many rounds.

Pitfalls · misconceptions

People think X, but actually Y (2/2)

⚠ Pitfall
  • Think: low perplexity = high quality. Actually: perplexity measures fluency — a fluent lie scores well; aggressive filtering wipes out valid minority dialects.
  • Think: an LLM judge is unbiased. Actually: it has position bias, verbosity bias, and self-preference — silently corrupting AI-feedback data.
  • Think: clean benchmarks = no contamination. Actually: synthetic data inherits transitive leakage — if the teacher saw the test set, its outputs encode the answers.
  • Think: more synthetic tokens always help. Actually: without dedup you pile up near-dups that inflate counts & boost memorization.
Four more. Perplexity is a fluency/typicality measure, not a truth measure — a confident lie has low perplexity, and over-aggressive perplexity filtering deletes valid minority dialects and rare-but-correct text. The LLM judge: it is NOT a neutral grader. It has position bias (favors the first or last option), verbosity bias (favors longer answers), and self-preference (favors its own style) — these silently corrupt every AI-feedback dataset, so you must randomize positions, control for length, and calibrate against gold. Transitive contamination is the sneakiest: even if YOUR data has no direct overlap with the test set, if the teacher that generated it had seen the test set, the answers can be encoded in its generations — contamination with nothing to flag. And finally, more tokens isn't more value: without dedup you accumulate near-duplicates that inflate token counts, boost memorization, and add zero diversity.

  • You want 1M math word problems with verified answers — sketch a pipeline that maximizes label correctness. Hard▸ reveal
    Prefer generate-from-solution: sample structured problems with known ground truth (templates + a CAS like SymPy), then have an LLM render natural-language problems around the verified answer. For LLM-generated problems, verify with a symbolic checker, majority vote, and cross-model agreement; drop non-consensus; dedup, stratify, human spot-check. Never trust an LLM's self-reported answer without an independent verifier.
  • Define model collapse. What happens to tails, variance, and modes under recursive self-training? Med▸ reveal
    A degenerative process where recursive training on model outputs loses the true distribution: tails vanish first, variance shrinks, modes merge toward a narrow over-smoothed set. Sampling, finite-sample, and approximation errors compound each generation. It's not just "quality drops" — the signature is tail loss and variance collapse.
  • Synthetic data from a teacher that saw the benchmark can leak downstream. How do you guard against transitive leakage? Hard▸ reveal
    Assume the teacher leaks. Decontaminate the generated data (n-gram + embedding/semantic overlap against all test sets, drop matches); avoid prompting near benchmark items; evaluate on freshly-created/post-cutoff held-outs; use canaries; track provenance end-to-end. Decontaminating only your raw scrape misses the leak carried by teacher-generated synthetic data.

Takeaways

What to remember

  • The data wall is real: high-quality public text is finite, so models train on data they or a teacher generate.
  • Generation is cheap; verification is the moat. A hard verifier turns a noisy generator into near-ground-truth labels — math/code lead.
  • The ladder: augment → distill → Self-Instruct → Evol-Instruct → textbooks → STaR → self-reward → RLAIF.
  • Self-improvement is a flywheel: generate → verify → retrain — spins only with diversity + a reliable verifier.
  • Collapse = recursive "replace." Accumulating real + synthetic largely prevents it.
  • Hygiene is non-negotiable: dedup (MinHash-LSH), mixtures (DoReMi), judge-bias audits, contamination checks.
  • Quality over quantity: phi & LIMA — small clean sets rival massive scrapes.
The recap — same seven points as the roadmap, now earned. Read them back as the through-line: finite real text forces synthetic; verification is what makes synthetic safe; there's a ladder of methods each fixing the last one's gap; the loop is a flywheel that needs diversity and a verifier; collapse comes from replacing not from using synthetic; hygiene is mandatory; and small clean beats large dirty.
