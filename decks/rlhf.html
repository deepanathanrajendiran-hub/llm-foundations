RLHF & Alignment

How human (and AI) preferences turn a fluent-but-feral base model into a helpful, harmless, honest assistant.

Welcome. This is the alignment module — the bridge between "a model that predicts text" and "a model you'd actually ship as an assistant." The thesis of the whole lecture: pretraining gives capability, alignment gives behavior. We will build the canonical three-stage RLHF pipeline, then walk the full preference-optimization landscape from InstructGPT-era PPO to DPO to GRPO to verifiable rewards. Tell the audience to press S to open these notes if they follow along, and that everything here is interview-grade — the "why did we move from X to Y" questions get asked constantly.

Roadmap

The six core ideas

Learn taste → Optimize it → Keep a leash
  • Humans compare better than they score → pairwise preferences
  • A reward model is a queryable proxy for the human panel
  • RL pushes the policy toward high reward — under a KL leash
  • Everything fights Goodhart's law
These four bullets are the spine. "Humans compare better than they score" justifies the whole preference-data design. The reward model decouples "what humans like" from "how to optimize it." The KL leash is the single most important safety valve. And Goodhart — optimizing a proxy eventually destroys the thing you cared about — is the antagonist of the entire field. Keep coming back to these.

Why it matters

A base model has read the internet — and wants nothing

  • It can continue text fluently…
  • …but next-token prediction never taught it "good answer" vs "plausible continuation"
  • Ask a question → it may answer, or autocomplete ten more questions
  • Alignment bends raw capability toward Helpful, Harmless, Honest
The motivating problem: pretraining optimizes likelihood of internet text, which contains both brilliant answers and endless garbage. The base model has no notion of what you want. The classic demo: ask GPT-3 base "What is the capital of France?" and it might continue with five more trivia questions, because that's a plausible document. The HHH triad (helpful, harmless, honest) comes from Anthropic. The hard part, stressed in the doc: "good" is a matter of human taste, and you cannot write a loss function for taste — so we have to learn it.

Intuition

Training a chef

  • SFT = chef copies recipes from a cookbook (imitation)
  • Reward model = food critics taste pairs of dishes, say which plate wins → a stand-in critic you query for free
  • RL = chef experiments; stand-in critic scores each attempt; keep what scores well
  • KL leash = a manager stops the chef drowning everything in salt just because the critic loves salt
Base LM ↓ SFT — imitate ↓ Reward model — taste ↓ RL — maximize r − β·KL
This analogy carries the whole lecture, so land it cleanly. The crucial subtlety in the "salt" line: a reward model is a proxy. If the critic happens to over-value one feature (salt, or in real life: length, bullet lists, confident tone), an unleashed optimizer will exploit that feature to absurdity. The manager — the KL penalty to the frozen SFT reference — is what keeps the chef cooking real food. We will formalize "salt = reward hacking" later.

SFT teaches format & instruction-following · the RM encodes human taste · RL optimizes against it under a KL leash to the frozen reference.

This is Figure 1 from the doc — the canonical InstructGPT recipe. Note the SFT model plays a double role: it's the starting point for RL and it becomes the frozen reference π_ref that the KL leash measures against. Three stages: imitate, encode taste, optimize. Every method we cover is a variation on this skeleton — DPO fuses stages 2+3, GRPO swaps the RL algorithm, RLVR swaps the reward source.

How it works · Stage 1

SFT — supervised fine-tuning

  • Collect (prompt, ideal response) demonstrations
  • Fine-tune with ordinary next-token cross-entropy
  • Result: a model that already follows instructions & formats answers
  • We start RL from here — RL is a local search, it needs a competent start
  • The SFT model also becomes the frozen reference π_ref
Why not RL straight from the base model? Because RL improves what the policy already does well but explores poorly — it's a local search. It needs a starting point that already puts probability mass on good answers. SFT provides exactly that. Emphasize the double role again: the SFT checkpoint is both the RL initialization and the frozen reference for the KL penalty. That's a common interview clarification.

How it works · Stage 2

Reward modeling — encode the taste

  • Sample several responses per prompt; humans rank / compare them pairwise
  • Train a reward model (RM): the SFT model with its LM head swapped for a single scalar "how good?" head
  • Fit with the Bradley-Terry objective → chosen scores higher than rejected
  • The RM is a cheap, queryable proxy for the human panel
The RM is usually initialized from the SFT model — same backbone, scalar head. Why pairwise comparisons instead of 1–10 scores? Humans are far more reliable at "this beats that" than at calibrated absolute ratings, which drift across raters and across days. Bradley-Terry (next section) converts those comparisons into a smooth, differentiable scorer the RL loop can query millions of times for free. The RM is the heart of the "decouple taste from optimization" idea.

How it works · Stage 3

RL optimization — text generation as decisions

  • Policy π_θ generates a response token by token
  • RM scores the finished response → reward is sparse, sequence-level
  • Credit assignment across tokens is hard → that's the value function + advantages
  • Add a per-token KL penalty so the policy stays near π_ref
  • Default algorithm: PPO — clipped surrogate keeps each step small & stable
The reward arrives only at the end of the sequence — one number for a whole completion. So how do you decide which tokens to reinforce? That's the credit-assignment problem, and it's why we need a learned value function (the critic) plus GAE to spread the sequence reward back over tokens as advantages. The per-token KL penalty is what stops the policy collapsing into degenerate RM-gaming text. Without the leash, the policy quickly finds gibberish that the RM happens to score highly. PPO's clipping is the stability trick — we formalize it in the math section.

How it works · the loop

One turn of the RLHF (PPO) loop

Roll out a response, score it with reward minus KL, estimate per-token advantages, take a clipped step — then loop. Press Play, or Step ›.

Walk the seven stages live with the animation. (1) Sample a prompt x matching deployment. (2) The policy π_θ generates a response y — an on-policy rollout, meaning we optimize the model's own current outputs. (3) Now we have a full completion to score. (4) The frozen reward model scores it: r(x,y). (5) Subtract the per-token KL penalty β·KL(π_θ‖π_ref) so it can't drift or reward-hack. (6) A critic plus GAE turn (reward − KL) into per-token advantages A_t — this solves credit assignment. (7) PPO takes a clipped gradient step up the advantage, raising log-probs of high-advantage tokens without moving too far, then loops back. Stress: reference and reward models are frozen; only the policy and critic train. Four models live in memory at once — that's the cost we'll later remove with DPO and GRPO.

How it works · the loop, statically

The PPO-RLHF iteration as a flow

Prompt x → π_θ rollout → RM r(x,y) − β·KL → critic V + GAE → A_t → PPO clipped step ↺ repeat

Two newer shortcuts skip parts of this loop:

  • DPO: optimal policy is an implicit reward → train directly on pairs. No RM, no rollouts.
  • GRPO: keep online sampling but throw away the critic — normalize rewards within a group.
This is Figure 2. The reference and reward models are frozen; only the policy and critic train. Use this slide as the pivot into the approaches: DPO removes stages 2–3 entirely (no reward model, no rollouts) by showing the optimal policy and the reward are two views of the same thing. GRPO keeps the online rollouts but throws away the critic, estimating advantages by normalizing rewards within a group of completions sampled for the same prompt. We cover all of these next.

Approaches · 1 of 9

3-stage pipeline (SFT → RM → RL) classic

What: the original InstructGPT recipe; still the mental model for everything else. How: imitate demos (SFT) → fit a reward model to human comparisons → optimize the policy with RL under a KL leash. Solves: turns a text-predictor into a model that follows instructions & respects preferences it was never explicitly told.

Pros
  • Decouples "what humans like" (RM) from "how to optimize it" (RL)
  • A reusable RM scores unlimited new samples for free
  • Battle-tested — produced ChatGPT / InstructGPT
Cons
  • Three models in memory + a critic — heavy
  • Many moving parts; brittle, hard to reproduce
  • RM errors propagate into reward hacking

vs previous: this is the baseline — the skeleton every later method trims a piece off.

Read more ↗

Source: InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022). The big conceptual win is decoupling: the RM captures human taste once, then RL queries it for free against unlimited fresh samples. The cost is operational complexity — three (really four, with the critic) models. Everything that follows is an attempt to keep the win while cutting the cost. Stress the SFT model's double role: RL initialization AND the frozen reference for the KL penalty.

How it works · 3-stage pipeline

3-stage pipeline — step by step

demosprompt→ideal → SFTimitate → RMBT on pairs → RL + KLoptimize → alignedHHH
  1. Stage 1 — collect (prompt, ideal response) demos; SFT with cross-entropy → an instruction-follower.
  2. Stage 2 — sample responses from SFT; humans compare them pairwise; fit a reward model.
  3. Stage 3 — RL pushes the policy toward high RM reward, leashed by KL to the frozen SFT reference.
  4. The SFT checkpoint plays two roles: RL start point and the frozen reference \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\).
Walk left to right. SFT consumes demonstrations and produces the policy. The RM consumes pairwise comparisons (sampled from SFT) and produces a scalar scorer. RL consumes both and produces the aligned model. Reference and reward are frozen during RL; only the policy and critic train. Emphasize: RL is a local search, so it needs SFT's competent starting point.

Approaches · 2 of 9

Reward modeling via Bradley-Terry modern

What: a model mapping (prompt, response) → scalar reward. How: init from the SFT model + a scalar head; train with the Bradley-Terry pairwise loss so the winner outscores the loser by a margin proportional to preference strength. Solves: converts noisy, relative human judgments into a smooth differentiable scorer the RL loop queries millions of times.

Pros
  • Trains from cheap pairwise comparisons, not absolute scores
  • One forward pass = a reward on any new text
  • Can ensemble multiple RMs for an uncertainty signal
Cons
  • Identified only up to an additive constant (scale matters, offset doesn't)
  • Latches onto spurious features — length, lists, formatting
  • Generalizes poorly off-distribution — exactly where RL pushes it

vs previous: this is the engine inside stage 2 — it turns comparisons into a queryable function the whole pipeline depends on.

Read more ↗

Source: Stiennon et al., 2020 (Learning to summarize from human feedback) — proved RM + PPO works for LLMs. The "additive constant" point is interview gold: Bradley-Terry constrains only differences of rewards, so absolute scale is meaningless — that's why PPO uses advantages, not raw rewards. The "off-distribution" con is the seed of reward hacking: RL deliberately pushes the policy where the RM is least reliable.

How it works · Reward modeling (Bradley-Terry)

Reward modeling — step by step

x, yw, ylpair → rθscalar head → rw − rlgap → σ(gap)BT prob → −log σRM loss
  1. Take a labeled triple: prompt \(x\), winner \(y_w\), loser \(y_l\).
  2. Score each with the RM head → scalars \(r_w, r_l\); the only thing that matters is the gap \(r_w - r_l\).
  3. Bradley-Terry: \(P(y_w \succ y_l) = \sigma(r_w - r_l)\) — bigger gap = more confident preference.
  4. Loss \(= -\log\sigma(r_w - r_l)\); minimizing it drives the winner's score above the loser's.
Press Play to step the widget, or use Step ›. This is logistic regression on the reward gap — and it is exactly the shape that reappears in the DPO loss; that's not a coincidence, it's the whole DPO trick. The RM is initialized from the SFT model so it already understands language and quality; only the scalar head is new. Saturating log-sigmoid means already-correct, high-margin pairs contribute little gradient.

Approaches · 3 of 9

PPO RLHF (KL-penalized) modern

What: online RL that optimizes the policy against the RM. How: roll out → reward − per-token KL → per-token advantages via critic + GAE → take a clipped policy-gradient step. Solves: lets the model improve on-policy on its own generations and explore beyond fixed data, with stability from clipping and a leash from KL.

Pros
  • On-policy: optimizes the actual deployment distribution
  • KL leash directly controls over-optimization
  • Most expressive — can exceed the preference data it started from
Cons
  • Four models in play (policy, critic, RM, reference) — huge memory
  • Notoriously unstable; sensitive to whitening, KL estimator, minibatch order
  • Needs real RL infrastructure and careful tuning

vs previous: the RM only scores; PPO is what actually moves the policy — the optimizer on top of the scorer.

Read more ↗

Source: Schulman et al., 2017 (PPO). The defining advantage over offline methods is on-policy learning: PPO optimizes the distribution the model actually produces, so it can discover answers better than anything in the preference set. The defining cost: four models in memory and genuine instability. "On-policy" is the phrase to repeat — it's the single capability DPO gives up.

How it works · PPO RLHF

PPO RLHF — step by step

prompt xsample → πθ rollouty → r − β·KLshaped → critic+GAEAt → clipped step↺
  1. Sample a prompt; the policy \(\pi_\theta\) generates a response \(y\) token by token (an on-policy rollout).
  2. RM scores the finished \(y\); subtract a per-token KL penalty \(\beta\,\mathrm{KL}(\pi_\theta\|\pi_{\text{ref}})\).
  3. Critic + GAE turn (reward − KL) into per-token advantages \(A_t\) — this solves credit assignment.
  4. Take a clipped gradient step up the advantage; loop. Reference & RM stay frozen.
Walk the loop live. (1) Sample a prompt matching deployment. (2) On-policy rollout — we optimize the model's own outputs. (3) RM gives one terminal scalar; the KL penalty is per-token so drift is penalized throughout. (4) The critic provides a baseline and GAE spreads the sequence reward over tokens as advantages. (5) PPO clipping keeps each step small and stable. Four models live in memory at once — that's the cost DPO and GRPO later remove.

Approaches · 4 of 9

Rejection sampling / best-of-n / RAFT classic

What: use the RM with no RL gradients. How: sample n responses, keep the top-scoring one(s) — return it at inference (best-of-n) or SFT the policy on the survivors (rejection-sampling FT / RAFT). Solves: captures most of RLHF's quality gain with none of PPO's instability — just filtering + supervised learning.

Pros
  • Dead simple, stable, easy to debug
  • No critic, no policy-gradient bookkeeping
  • Best-of-n needs only inference-time compute
Cons
  • Best-of-n costs n× inference for every query, forever
  • Larger n amplifies reward hacking (you pick the most gamed sample)
  • Less sample-efficient & less expressive than full RL

vs previous: no critic, no policy-gradient bookkeeping versus PPO — but no on-policy gradient signal either; it only imitates the best samples.

Read more ↗

Source: HF TRL best-of-n docs / RAFT. The pragmatic baseline everyone should try first. The subtle con: larger n is not strictly better — bigger n selects the most extreme sample, which is precisely the most reward-hacked one. Best-of-n trades training compute for permanent inference compute; RAFT moves that gain into the weights by SFT-ing on the survivors and iterating.

How it works · Rejection sampling / best-of-n / RAFT

Rejection sampling — step by step

prompt xonce → sample ny₁…yₙ → RM scoresrank → keep top-kfilter → return / SFTBoN / RAFT
  1. Draw \(n\) candidate responses from the current policy for one prompt.
  2. Score every candidate with the RM (or a verifier) and rank them.
  3. Best-of-n: just return the top-scoring candidate at inference time.
  4. RAFT: instead SFT the policy on the survivors, then iterate — no gradient flows through the reward.
Two ways to spend the same samples. Best-of-n is inference-time only: n× cost on every query, forever, but no training. RAFT bakes the gain into the weights — filter, imitate the winners, repeat — so future inference is cheap. Neither differentiates through the reward, which is exactly why they're stable but less expressive than PPO. Larger n surfaces more RM exploits.

Approaches · 5 of 9

DPO — Direct Preference Optimization SOTA

What: train directly on preference pairs with one classification-style loss. How: the optimal RLHF policy is an implicit reward \(\beta\log(\pi_\theta/\pi_{\text{ref}})\); plug it into Bradley-Terry and the RM + partition function cancel, leaving a loss over (prompt, chosen, rejected) triples. Solves: removes the reward model and the entire RL loop — no critic, no rollouts, no KL controller.

Pros
  • One model + frozen reference; simple, stable, cheap
  • No reward model to train or hack at RL time
  • Works on a fixed offline preference dataset
Cons
  • Offline: suffers when prefs are off the current policy's distribution
  • Often drives down log-prob of both chosen and rejected
  • Cannot explore beyond the labeled pairs; no on-policy signal

vs previous: same KL-constrained objective as PPO, but solved in closed form — it trades on-policy exploration for offline simplicity and drops the RM entirely.

Read more ↗

Source: Rafailov et al., 2023 (DPO). The "aha": there's a closed-form map between the optimal KL-constrained policy and the reward. Substituting it into Bradley-Terry makes both the reward model and the intractable partition function cancel. The two cons interviewers love: it's offline (no exploration), and it frequently lowers the probability of both responses — because the loss only cares about the gap.

How it works · DPO

DPO — step by step

(x, yw, yl)offline pair → logπθ & logπref4 log-probs → β·log ratioimplicit r → −log σ(Δ)DPO loss → step policyref frozen
  1. Take an offline triple; compute sequence log-probs for \(y_w, y_l\) under both \(\pi_\theta\) and frozen \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\).
  2. Implicit reward \(\hat r(x,y) = \beta\log\frac{\pi_\theta(y\mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y\mid x)}\) — the policy is the reward model.
  3. Margin \(\Delta = \hat r_w - \hat r_l\); the partition function and explicit RM cancel out of the difference.
  4. Loss \(= -\log\sigma(\Delta)\); backprop through the policy only — the reference is frozen.
Press Play or Step ›. Put this next to the RM widget: identical sigmoid-of-a-gap, but the "reward" is now the policy-vs-reference log-ratio. Because that ratio is the optimal policy's implicit reward, the explicit RM and the intractable partition function both cancel. β here plays the KL-coefficient role: small β = looser, large β = tighter to the reference. Watch the implicit rewards in practice — that's how you catch the "both go down" pathology.

Approaches · 6 of 9

DPO zoo — IPO · KTO · SimPO SOTA

What: three targeted fixes for three specific DPO weaknesses.

  • IPO: swaps logistic loss for a squared objective targeting a fixed margin → regularizes against overfitting near-deterministic preferences.
  • KTO: drops pairs; learns from unpaired good/bad labels using a prospect-theory (Kahneman-Tversky) utility.
  • SimPO: removes the reference model; uses length-normalized average log-prob as the implicit reward + a target-margin term.
Pros
  • IPO: robust when preferences are near-deterministic
  • KTO: uses cheap thumbs-up/down data, no pairing required
  • SimPO: reference-free, length-normalized, simpler & often stronger
Cons
  • More knobs (target margins, length norm) to tune
  • Reference-free variants can drift further from the base model
  • Gains are dataset-dependent; no single winner

vs previous: each patches a DPO flaw — IPO → overfitting 0/1 prefs · KTO → the need for matched pairs · SimPO → the reference-model cost & length bias.

Read more ↗

Sources: SimPO (Meng et al., 2024); IPO (Azar et al., 2023); KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024). Frame as three fixes for three DPO weaknesses. IPO targets DPO's tendency to push toward 0/1 preferences. KTO removes matched pairs — thumbs-up/down data is far cheaper. SimPO removes the reference model (saves memory) and length-normalizes to curb length bias. The meta-lesson: no single winner; gains are dataset-dependent.

How it works · DPO zoo

DPO zoo — step by step

DPO lossbaseline → IPOsquared, margin → KTOunpaired ± → SimPOno ref, len-norm
  1. Start from DPO: \(-\log\sigma\big(\beta(\hat r_w - \hat r_l)\big)\) on matched pairs against a frozen reference.
  2. IPO — replace the logistic loss with a squared term targeting a fixed margin, so saturation can't overfit clean 0/1 prefs.
  3. KTO — drop pairing; each example is just good or bad, scored by a prospect-theory utility around a reference point.
  4. SimPO — delete \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\); use length-normalized average log-prob as the reward and add a target margin \(\gamma\).
Read each as a one-line diff from DPO. IPO: change the loss shape (squared, fixed margin) to stop saturation-driven overfitting. KTO: change the data (unpaired good/bad) via a Kahneman-Tversky value function — no chosen/rejected pairing. SimPO: change the reward parameterization — drop the reference model entirely, length-normalize, add a target margin. Three different axes of fix; pick by your data and constraints.

Approaches · 7 of 9

GRPO — Group Relative Policy Optimization SOTA

What: online RL like PPO but without the value function. How: per prompt, sample a group of completions, score them all, use the group's mean/std to normalize each reward into an advantage — every token in a completion shares that completion's advantage. Solves: eliminates the critic (half the memory + a major instability source) while keeping on-policy learning.

Pros
  • No critic → much lighter than PPO, fewer failure modes
  • Group baseline = clean, low-variance advantage estimate
  • Powered DeepSeekMath & DeepSeek-R1's reasoning gains
Cons
  • Sensitive to group size; needs many samples per prompt
  • Advantage collapses to zero when all samples in a group tie
  • Still prone to length/format bias & reward hacking

vs previous: like PPO it's online, but the group mean replaces the learned value baseline — dropping the critic that DPO never had and PPO paid for.

Read more ↗

Source: Shao et al., 2024 (DeepSeekMath, introduces GRPO). The core trick: PPO's critic exists only to provide a baseline for variance reduction. GRPO gets that baseline for free by sampling a group of G completions and using their mean reward as the baseline — no value network. That halves the model count vs PPO. The "all-tie" failure mode is the gotcha: if every sample in a group ties, std = 0 and the advantage is undefined/zero. GRPO pairs beautifully with verifiable rewards, next.

How it works · GRPO

GRPO — step by step

prompt xone → group of Gy₁…y_G → score allr₁…r_G → (rᵢ−mean)/stdA_i → clipped stepno critic
  1. For one prompt, sample a group of \(G\) completions from the current policy.
  2. Score all of them — with an RM or a verifier — to get rewards \(r_1,\dots,r_G\).
  3. Advantage \(A_i = \dfrac{r_i - \mathrm{mean}(r)}{\mathrm{std}(r)}\), shared by every token of completion \(i\). The group mean is the baseline.
  4. Take a PPO-style clipped step (+ a KL term) — no value network anywhere.
Press Play or Step ›. The critic in PPO existed only to estimate a baseline for variance reduction; GRPO replaces it with the empirical group mean, standardized by std. That halves the model count. The denominator is the Achilles heel: when every completion ties (all correct or all wrong), std = 0 and the advantage is undefined — common with sparse binary rewards. Mitigations: bigger groups, or filtering all-tie prompts.

Approaches · 8 of 9

RLAIF & Constitutional AI modern

What: replace (or augment) human preference labels with a model's own judgments. RLAIF: an LLM labels which of two responses is better → preference data at scale. Constitutional AI: the model critiques & revises its own answers against a written "constitution" (SL phase), then an AI-preference model drives RL (RLAIF phase). Solves: human labels are slow & expensive; AI feedback scales cheaply and targets harmlessness without exposing annotators to toxic content.

Pros
  • Cheap, fast, scalable label generation
  • Constitution makes values explicit & auditable
  • Reduces annotator exposure to harmful content
Cons
  • Inherits & can amplify the judge model's biases
  • Risk of self-preference & reward-model collapse
  • Needs human validation that AI labels track real preferences

vs previous: orthogonal axis — this changes where preferences come from (AI not humans), not the optimization algorithm underneath.

Read more ↗

Sources: Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022) and RLAIF (Lee et al., 2023). The orthogonal axis: this changes where preferences come from, not the optimization algorithm. RLAIF showed AI-generated preferences can match human RLHF. Constitutional AI adds a self-critique-and-revise SL phase against explicit written principles, then RLAIF on top. The honest caveat: AI judges inherit and can amplify their own biases, so validate against a human-labeled holdout.

How it works · RLAIF & Constitutional AI

Constitutional AI — step by step

constitutionwritten rules → critique+reviseSL phase → AI judges pairsRLAIF labels → RM + RLstandard loop → human holdoutvalidate
  1. Write a constitution: explicit principles describing desired behavior.
  2. SL phase — the model answers, then critiques & revises its own answer against the constitution; SFT on the revisions.
  3. RLAIF phase — the model generates response pairs; an AI judge picks the more constitution-aligned one → preference data.
  4. Train an RM on those AI labels and run standard RL; validate AI-vs-human agreement on a human-labeled holdout.
Two phases. Phase 1 is supervised: critique-and-revise distills the constitution into the weights. Phase 2 is RLAIF: an AI judge produces the preference labels that drive an ordinary RM + RL loop — the only change from classic RLHF is the label source. Stress the holdout: never validate RLAIF with another AI judge alone; you need human ground truth to trust the labels and catch self-preference.

Approaches · 9 of 9

RLVR + ORM vs PRM SOTA

What: RL with verifiable rewards — a programmatic checker, not a learned RM, decides correctness. How: for math/code/logic, run the answer through a grader (exact-match, unit tests, a checker) and reward correctness; combine with GRPO. ORM vs PRM: an outcome reward model scores only the final answer; a process reward model scores each intermediate step. Solves: a ground-truth checker can't be flattered → sidesteps reward-model hacking; PRMs give dense credit on long chains.

Pros
  • Ground-truth reward → far less reward hacking
  • Unlocked emergent reasoning (DeepSeek-R1)
  • PRM gives dense per-step signal for long chains
Cons
  • Only applies where answers are checkable
  • PRM step-labels are expensive to collect
  • Models still hack graders (degenerate solutions, regex exploits)

vs previous: swaps the reward source — a learned RM (flatterable) for a ground-truth checker (not flatterable); usually paired with GRPO.

Read more ↗

Source: DeepSeek-R1 (DeepSeek-AI, 2025). The key insight: a learned RM can be flattered into high scores, but a ground-truth checker (did the tests pass? is the answer correct?) cannot — so RLVR sidesteps the main reward-hacking pathway. This powered the reasoning-model wave: pure RL against a checker elicited long chain-of-thought and self-correction. ORM = score the final answer; PRM = score each step (denser, but step-labels are expensive). Caveat: models still hack graders.

How it works · RLVR + ORM vs PRM

RLVR — step by step

promptmath/code → model solvesCoT + answer → gradertests / exact-match → ORM vs PRMfinal vs per-step → GRPOoptimize
  1. Pose a prompt with a checkable answer (math with a known result, code with unit tests).
  2. The model produces a chain-of-thought and a final answer.
  3. A programmatic grader verifies it — exact-match, unit tests, a logic checker — giving a ground-truth reward.
  4. ORM rewards only the final answer; PRM scores each step (dense credit). Feed the reward to GRPO.
The reward source is a deterministic checker, not a learned RM, so it can't be flattered — that's the whole point. ORM is cheap (final-answer correctness) but sparse on long chains; PRM scores each reasoning step for dense credit assignment but needs expensive step-level labels (often Monte-Carlo-rollout-derived). Pairs naturally with GRPO's group baseline. Caveat: graders themselves get hacked — degenerate solutions, regex exploits, printing the expected output.

The math · preferences → reward

Bradley-Terry

Probability that winner y_w beats loser y_l is the sigmoid of their reward gap:

$$ P(y_w \succ y_l) = \sigma\big(r(x,y_w) - r(x,y_l)\big) $$

\(P(y_w \succ y_l)\) = prob the winner is preferred; \(x\) = prompt; \(r(x,y)\) = scalar RM reward; \(\sigma\) = logistic sigmoid \(1/(1+e^{-z})\). Only the difference matters — \(r\) is identified only up to an additive constant.

Start here because everything downstream is Bradley-Terry in disguise. The single takeaway: only reward differences matter, so the absolute scale of r is meaningless — which is exactly why PPO works with advantages, not raw rewards, and why two RMs trained on the same data can have wildly different absolute outputs.

The math · fit the RM

Reward-model loss

Maximum likelihood on comparisons = minimize the negative log-likelihood of the Bradley-Terry probabilities:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{RM} = -\,\mathbb{E}_{(x,y_w,y_l)}\big[\log \sigma\big(r_w - r_l\big)\big] $$

\(\mathcal{L}_{RM}\) = RM loss; \(\mathbb{E}\) = average over labeled triples; \(r_w, r_l\) = rewards of winner & loser. Driving this down pushes \(r_w\) above \(r_l\).

This is just logistic regression on the reward gap. Notice it's exactly the shape we'll see again in the DPO loss — that's not a coincidence, it's the whole DPO trick. If they understand this loss, DPO is a one-line substitution away.

The math · the RL objective

KL-penalized RLHF objective

$$ \max_\theta\; \mathbb{E}_{x,\,y\sim\pi_\theta}\big[r(x,y)\big] - \beta\, \mathrm{KL}\!\big(\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid x) \,\|\, \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot\mid x)\big) $$

\(\pi_\theta\) = trainable policy; \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\) = frozen reference (SFT model); \(\beta\) = KL coefficient (leash strength); \(\mathrm{KL}\) = drift from the reference. Large \(\beta\) = tame but unimprovable; tiny \(\beta\) = over-optimizes & hacks the reward.

This is the objective of the whole field. Read it aloud: maximize expected reward, minus a penalty for drifting from the reference. β is the single most important hyperparameter — it's the dial between "won't improve" and "reward-hacked." Critically: DPO, as we'll show, has a closed-form solution to this exact objective. PPO solves it iteratively; DPO solves it analytically.

The math · PPO

Clipped surrogate

$$ \mathcal{L}^{CLIP} = \mathbb{E}_t\Big[\min\big(\rho_t A_t,\; \mathrm{clip}(\rho_t,\, 1-\epsilon,\, 1+\epsilon)\, A_t\big)\Big],\quad \rho_t = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)}{\pi_{\text{old}}(a_t\mid s_t)} $$

\(\rho_t\) = new/old policy probability ratio for token \(a_t\); \(A_t\) = advantage (how much better than expected, via GAE); \(\epsilon\) = clip width (~0.2). The clip removes the incentive to move \(\rho_t\) far from 1 — that's PPO's stability trick.

The clip is the genius of PPO. Without it, a single batch could move the policy arbitrarily far. The min-of-two-terms construction means: once the probability ratio leaves the [1−ε, 1+ε] band in the helpful direction, the gradient is clipped to zero — no incentive to keep pushing. ε ≈ 0.2 is the standard value. This is what makes large policy-gradient steps safe on a giant language model.

The math · DPO

The closed-form collapse

Implicit reward \(r(x,y)=\beta\log\frac{\pi_\theta(y\mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y\mid x)}\); plug into Bradley-Terry → the whole pipeline becomes one loss:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{DPO} = -\,\mathbb{E}_{(x,y_w,y_l)}\Big[\log \sigma\Big(\beta \log \tfrac{\pi_\theta(y_w\mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w\mid x)} - \beta \log \tfrac{\pi_\theta(y_l\mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l\mid x)}\Big)\Big] $$

\(\pi_\theta(y\mid x)\) = policy sequence log-prob; \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\) = frozen reference's log-prob; \(\beta\) = temperature for how far the policy may deviate. The bracket = the margin between implicit rewards of winner & loser; the partition function and RM both cancel.

Compare this side-by-side with the RM loss two slides back — identical sigmoid-of-a-gap shape. The only change: the "reward" is now the policy-vs-reference log-ratio. Because that ratio is the optimal policy's implicit reward, the explicit reward model and the intractable partition function both cancel in the derivation. That's why DPO needs no RM and no rollouts. β here plays the same role as the KL coefficient: small β = looser, large β = tighter to the reference.

The math · GRPO

Group-normalized advantage

$$ A_i = \frac{r_i - \mathrm{mean}(r_1,\dots,r_G)}{\mathrm{std}(r_1,\dots,r_G)} $$

\(A_i\) = advantage shared by every token of completion \(i\); \(r_i\) = its reward; \(G\) = group size (completions per prompt); mean/std over the group. The group is the baseline → no value network. If all \(r_i\) are equal, \(\mathrm{std}=0\) → advantage collapses (the "all-tie" failure).

This is the entire GRPO idea in one equation. The critic in PPO existed only to estimate a baseline for variance reduction; GRPO replaces it with the empirical mean over a group of completions. Standardizing by std keeps advantages well-scaled. The denominator is also the Achilles heel: when every completion in a group ties (all correct or all wrong), std = 0 and the advantage is undefined — you get no learning signal from that prompt. Mitigations: bigger groups, or filtering all-tie prompts.

In code

Reward-model loss

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def reward_model_loss(r_chosen, r_rejected):
    # r_chosen, r_rejected: (batch,) scalar rewards from the RM head
    # maximize log-sigmoid(r_w - r_l)  ->  minimize its negative
    return -F.logsigmoid(r_chosen - r_rejected).mean()

# toy check: chosen should score higher than rejected
r_w = torch.tensor([2.0, 0.5, 3.0])
r_l = torch.tensor([1.0, 1.2, 0.0])   # middle pair is "wrong" -> bigger loss
print(reward_model_loss(r_w, r_l).item())   # ~0.49

It's just a pairwise logistic loss on the chosen-minus-rejected reward gap.

Point out how tiny this is — the entire RM objective is one line of logsigmoid. The toy check makes it concrete: the middle pair (0.5 vs 1.2) has the rejected scoring higher than the chosen, so it contributes a large loss (1.103) and drags up the mean to ~0.49. The first and third pairs are "correct" and contribute almost nothing.

In code

DPO loss — same shape

import torch.nn.functional as F

def dpo_loss(pi_logp_chosen, pi_logp_rejected,
             ref_logp_chosen, ref_logp_rejected, beta=0.1):
    # each arg: (batch,) = sum of token log-probs for that response
    pi_logratios  = pi_logp_chosen  - pi_logp_rejected      # policy margin
    ref_logratios = ref_logp_chosen - ref_logp_rejected     # reference margin
    logits = beta * (pi_logratios - ref_logratios)          # implicit reward margin
    loss = -F.logsigmoid(logits).mean()
    # for logging: implicit rewards (detach in practice)
    chosen_reward   = beta * (pi_logp_chosen  - ref_logp_chosen)
    rejected_reward = beta * (pi_logp_rejected - ref_logp_rejected)
    return loss, chosen_reward, rejected_reward

Same logsigmoid as the RM — the "reward" is now the policy-vs-reference log-ratio.

Put this next to the previous slide in your head: same logsigmoid, but the "reward" gap is now (policy margin − reference margin). The four inputs are summed token log-probs for the chosen/rejected under both the policy and the frozen reference. β = 0.1 is the common default. The logged "implicit rewards" are what you'd plot to monitor training — and watching them is how you'd catch the "both go down" pathology we cover in the worked example.

Worked example

One DPO gradient step — the numbers

Summed token log-probs for one triple (x, y_w, y_l), with \(\beta=0.1\):

  • Policy: \(\log\pi_\theta(y_w) = -5.0\), \(\;\log\pi_\theta(y_l) = -6.0\)
  • Reference: \(\log\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w) = -4.5\), \(\;\log\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l) = -7.0\)
  • Policy log-ratio: \(-5.0 - (-6.0) = +1.0\)
  • Reference log-ratio: \(-4.5 - (-7.0) = +2.5\)
  • Margin (logit): \(0.1 \times (1.0 - 2.5) = -0.15\)
  • Loss: \(-\log\sigma(-0.15) \approx 0.770\)
Walk the arithmetic slowly, one fragment per line. The two "ratios" are the within-response margins: how much more the model prefers the winner over the loser. The policy currently separates them by 1.0; the reference separates them by 2.5. So relative to the reference, the policy under-separates the pair → negative logit → loss above log(2)≈0.69.

Worked example · the subtlety

Why both log-probs can fall

  • Logit is negative → policy under-separates the pair (margin 1.0 < reference's 2.5)
  • Gradient pushes to raise \(\log\pi_\theta(y_w)\) and lower \(\log\pi_\theta(y_l)\) until the logit turns positive
  • But the loss only cares about the gap…
  • …so it often widens it by suppressing \(y_l\) faster than it lifts \(y_w\)
  • Result: absolute prob of the good answer can drop, yet the loss still decreases
This is the interviewer's favorite DPO gotcha. The loss is a function of the margin, not of either absolute log-prob. The cheapest way to grow the margin is frequently to crush the rejected response, and the chosen one comes along for the ride downward — just slower. As long as chosen falls slower than rejected, the gap grows and the loss drops, even though P(good answer) decreased. Is it a problem? Sometimes — it can degrade generation quality — which is part of why reference-free and on-policy variants exist.

When to use what

Decision flow

Walk the branches. Top question is always infrastructure: if you have RL infra and budget and want maximum quality, PPO can exceed the preference data because it's on-policy. No infra? Ask whether you have a verifiable checker — if it's math/code/logic with ground truth, GRPO + RLVR is critic-free and hack-resistant. Otherwise, for a fixed offline preference set, DPO is the lowest-friction choice; for a quick stable baseline today, rejection-sampling fine-tuning or best-of-n; for unpaired thumbs-up/down data, KTO. And the rule that applies to ALL branches: always watch a true-reward or win-rate curve versus KL and stop before over-optimization.

When to use what · the rules

Quick rules

  • Offline prefs, limited GPU, no RL infra? → DPO
  • Max quality + RL infra + budget? → PPO RLHF
  • Reasoning/math/code with a checker? → GRPO + RLVR
  • Quick stable baseline today? → rejection-sampling FT / best-of-n
  • Human labels too slow, or harmlessness? → RLAIF / Constitutional AI, then validate vs human holdout
  • Thumbs-up/down → KTO · near-deterministic prefs → IPO · save the reference + curb length → SimPO
  • Always: watch true-reward (or win-rate) vs KL and stop before over-optimization
This mirrors the decision flow but lists the variant-level guidance from the doc explicitly. The last bullet is the universal one — independent of algorithm, monitor an independent eval against KL drift and stop early. Over-optimization is the failure that's invisible if you only watch the proxy reward, which is exactly the trap we hit next.

Pitfalls

People think X… but actually Y

⚠ Pitfall The proxy is not the goal.
  • Think: higher reward = better model. Actually: beyond some KL, true quality falls while proxy reward rises — Goodhart / over-optimization. Track an independent eval.
  • Think: RLHF teaches new knowledge. Actually: it mostly re-weights behaviors the base already has — the "alignment tax" is capability you can lose.
  • Think: a verifiable reward can't be hacked. Actually: models game graders — degenerate solutions, regex exploits, printing the expected output.
  • Think: longer answers are genuinely better. Actually: that's length bias — a spurious RM correlation; use length penalties / normalization.
These are straight from the doc and they're the densest interview territory. Goodhart is the headline: "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." The alignment-tax point matters — RLHF can trade benchmark capability for helpfulness. Length bias is the most common real-world reward-hack: RMs systematically prefer longer answers, so an unconstrained policy just gets more verbose.

Pitfalls · continued

More misconceptions

  • Think: the RM's absolute scores are meaningful. Actually: Bradley-Terry rewards are identified only up to a constant — only differences matter (hence advantages).
  • Think: DPO and PPO optimize different objectives. Actually: DPO solves the same KL-constrained objective in closed form — it just trades exploration for offline simplicity.
  • Think: RLHF makes a model safe. Actually: it often produces shallow refusals that jailbreaks bypass — alignment must be red-teamed, not assumed.
  • Think: LLM-as-a-judge win rates are objective. Actually: judges have position, length & self-preference bias — randomize order, control for length, use multiple judges.
The DPO-vs-PPO point is a frequent trap: they are NOT different objectives. DPO is a closed-form solution to the same KL-constrained problem PPO solves iteratively. The safety caveat is important to say out loud — RLHF produces surface refusals, not deep safety; you must red-team. And the LLM-judge biases (position, length, self-preference) are essential if anyone on the team runs automated win-rate evals.

What's new

From human pairs to verifiable, critic-free RL

🚀 What's new the field moved fast (2023–2025).
  • DPO became the default for open-model preference tuning — no RM, no RL loop — spawning IPO/KTO/SimPO
  • GRPO replaced PPO's critic with a group baseline — half the model count, stabler reasoning RL at scale
  • RLVR (verifiable rewards) drove the reasoning wave — DeepSeek-R1: pure RL vs a checker elicits long CoT & self-correction
  • PRMs give dense per-step credit, complementing outcome-only ORMs
  • Constitutional AI / RLAIF moved scalable AI feedback into production
The narrative of the last two years: preference tuning got simpler (DPO), reasoning RL got lighter (GRPO) and hack-resistant (RLVR), and feedback got cheaper (RLAIF). DeepSeek-R1 was the watershed — it showed you don't even need a reward model if you have a correctness checker; the model learns to reason and self-correct from a binary signal.

Practice

Practice — click a question to reveal the answer

Full bank: 08 · RLHF & RL Alignment — from the answer key, condensed.

  • Walk through the three canonical RLHF stages (SFT → RM → RL); what does each produce & consume? Easy▸ reveal
    SFT fine-tunes the base LM on demonstrations → an instruction-follower. Reward modeling trains a scalar RM on pairwise comparisons of SFT outputs. RL (PPO) optimizes the SFT policy against the RM with a KL penalty to the SFT reference. Each stage consumes the previous one's output. RL trains against the reward model, not raw human labels directly.
  • What is preference data, and why pairwise comparisons instead of absolute scores? Easy▸ reveal
    It's human judgments comparing two outputs for the same prompt ("A beats B"). Humans are far more consistent at relative comparisons than at absolute numeric scores, which suffer anchoring, drift, and cross-annotator mismatch. Pairwise data maps cleanly onto Bradley-Terry. Absolute Likert scores aren't "more informative" — they're noisier and uncalibrated across raters.
  • What is the reference model, and why add a KL penalty between policy and reference? Easy▸ reveal
    The reference is the frozen SFT policy (the RL start point). A per-token KL penalty to it is subtracted from the reward, discouraging drift. This prevents collapse into degenerate high-reward-but-incoherent text, preserves fluency, and keeps the policy in the RM's reliable region. The reference (frozen SFT) is not the critic/value model — different components.
  • Write the Bradley-Terry model & explain how it turns a scalar reward into a preference probability. Med▸ reveal
    Given scalar reward \(r(x,y)\), the probability \(y_w\) beats \(y_l\) is \( P = \sigma\big(r(x,y_w) - r(x,y_l)\big) \) — a logistic function of the reward difference. Larger gaps mean more confident preferences. It's logistic in the reward difference; don't drop the sigmoid or write it on raw rewards.
  • State the reward-model loss and explain each term. Med▸ reveal
    The Bradley-Terry NLL: \( \mathcal{L} = -\mathbb{E}_{(x,y_w,y_l)}[\log\sigma(r_w - r_l)] \). \(r_w, r_l\) are RM scores of chosen/rejected; minimizing it pushes the chosen score above the rejected. Log-sigmoid saturates, so easy high-margin pairs contribute little gradient. It's a ranking/classification loss, not regression-to-scores (MSE).
  • What happens if the KL coefficient is too low? Too high? Describe each failure mode. Med▸ reveal
    Too low: the policy drifts far from the reference, over-optimizes the RM (reward hacking), and degenerates into repetitive/gibberish text that scores high but is bad. Too high: the KL penalty dominates, so the policy barely moves from SFT — minimal alignment gain. Higher coefficient = stronger penalty = less drift (not more).
  • Write the PPO clipped surrogate and explain the clipping parameter ε. Hard▸ reveal
    \( L^{CLIP} = \mathbb{E}_t[\min(\rho_t A_t,\ \mathrm{clip}(\rho_t, 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon)A_t)] \), with ratio \(\rho_t = \pi_\theta/\pi_{\text{old}}\). The clip caps \(\rho_t\); the min makes it a pessimistic bound, killing the incentive to push past the trust region. ε (~0.1–0.2) sets step size. Without the min, clipping alone wouldn't stop a large step when the ratio overshoots helpfully.
  • Explain GRPO & how it eliminates PPO's critic. Where does its advantage come from instead? Med▸ reveal
    GRPO samples a group of \(G\) completions per prompt, scores them, and sets each advantage to its reward standardized within the group: \( A_i = (r_i - \mathrm{mean})/\mathrm{std} \), shared by all its tokens. The group mean is the baseline, so no value network is needed; it still uses a clipped objective. It still has a baseline — the group mean; it just isn't a separate critic.
  • Explain DPO's core idea & how it reparameterizes RLHF to drop the explicit reward model. Med▸ reveal
    The KL-constrained RLHF objective has a closed-form optimal policy whose reward equals a scaled log-ratio of policy to reference. Substituting that into Bradley-Terry lets you optimize the policy directly on preference pairs with one classification loss — no RM, no RL loop. The policy implicitly is the reward model. DPO isn't "reward-free" — it has an implicit reward (the log-ratio); it just isn't a separate network.
  • DPO often lowers log-prob of both chosen and rejected. Why — and is it a problem? Hard▸ reveal
    DPO optimizes only the relative margin, not absolute likelihoods. The gradient can widen the gap while pushing both log-probs down (rejected faster than chosen) — mass often flows to unseen sequences. It can be a problem: a falling chosen likelihood may degrade fluency. Fixes: add an SFT/NLL term, or use SimPO/CPO. A growing margin does not mean the chosen response got better — both can drop.
  • Compare DPO with IPO, KTO, SimPO — what specific weakness does each target? Hard▸ reveal
    IPO targets DPO's overfitting of near-deterministic prefs via a bounded squared objective. KTO removes the need for paired data — it learns from unpaired thumbs-up/down via a prospect-theory utility. SimPO removes the reference model and uses length-normalized average log-prob plus a target margin (fixing reference cost & length bias). Don't swap them: KTO = unpaired data; SimPO = reference-free + length-normalized.
  • Define RLVR. What makes rewards "verifiable," and why does that sidestep reward-model hacking? Med▸ reveal
    RLVR uses an automatic ground-truth checker as the reward — math with known answers, code that must pass unit tests. Rewards are verifiable when a deterministic checker decides correctness. With no learned, exploitable RM proxy, the reward is much closer to the true objective, so optimizing it improves real performance. Verifiable ≠ unhackable — weak graders, regexes, and answer-only checks still get gamed.
  • Contrast ORM with PRM. When is process supervision's extra cost justified? Hard▸ reveal
    An ORM scores only the final answer; a PRM scores each intermediate reasoning step. PRMs give denser credit, pinpoint where reasoning fails, and curb "right answer, wrong reasoning" — but need expensive step-level labels. Justified for hard multi-step tasks (math, complex reasoning) where outcome signal is too sparse; otherwise ORM is cheaper. PRM doesn't always win — its labeling cost only pays off on genuinely multi-step reasoning.
  • LLM-as-a-judge win rates: known biases (position, length, self-preference) & how to control them? Med▸ reveal
    Biases: position (favoring a slot), length/verbosity (longer = better), self-preference (its own style), plus format/sycophancy. Controls: randomize and swap A/B order and average both, length-control or report length-matched win-rates, use a different/stronger judge, calibrate vs human labels, average over judges. A single-order judgment is untrustworthy — position bias alone can flip win-rates.
  • What is the "alignment tax," and how would you measure capability degradation from RLHF? Med▸ reveal
    It's the loss in raw capability that alignment training can cause while improving helpfulness/harmlessness — regressions on knowledge, reasoning, or code. Measure it by running held-out capability benchmarks (MMLU, GSM8K, HumanEval) before vs after alignment, alongside helpfulness/safety win-rates. Measuring only safety/helpfulness gains hides the capability tax you're paying.

Takeaways

What to remember

  • RLHF = learn the taste, then optimize it. SFT imitates, a Bradley-Terry RM encodes comparisons, RL pushes toward high reward under a KL leash.
  • PPO is the expressive-but-heavy workhorse; \(\beta\) is the dial between "unimprovable" and "reward-hacked."
  • DPO is the shortcut: the optimal policy is an implicit reward → one logistic loss on pairs, trading exploration for simplicity.
  • GRPO drops the critic via a group baseline — lighter than PPO, the engine behind reasoning RL.
  • RLVR + GRPO sidestep RM hacking & unlocked emergent reasoning; ORMs score answers, PRMs score steps.
  • Everything fights Goodhart: reward hacking, length bias, sycophancy, over-optimization vs KL, the alignment tax, judge biases.
Recap the spine one last time. If they remember six things: (1) learn taste then optimize, (2) PPO + KL leash, (3) DPO = closed-form shortcut, (4) GRPO = critic-free, (5) RLVR = verifiable rewards for reasoning, (6) Goodhart is always the enemy. That's the whole module.
