PHASE 3 · REASONING & INFERENCE · MODULE 28

Reasoning Models & Test-Time Compute

Spending compute at inference, not just training: chain-of-thought as computation, sampling/voting/search, verifiers, RLVR & GRPO, and the long-CoT "thinking" models (o1, DeepSeek-R1).

←→ navigate · S speaker notes · O overview · F fullscreen · append ?print-pdf to export

Welcome. For a decade the only knob we turned was training: more data, more parameters, more pre-training FLOPs. This module is about a second knob that arrived in 2024 — spending more compute at inference, letting the model think longer before it answers. The whole lecture turns on one sentence: chain-of-thought is computation — the intermediate tokens are scratch space the model can read back, so writing more of them buys real reasoning depth. We'll go from CoT-as-computation, through sampling/voting/search and verifiers, into RLVR and GRPO (the RL recipe behind DeepSeek-R1), and finish on the long-"thinking" models and how to control their effort. Two phrases to hold onto the whole time: test-time compute is a new scaling axis, and a strong verifier beats a stronger generator.

Core ideas to land

Five things to take away

  • CoT = trading serial compute for depth — tokens are scratch space the model attends back to.
  • Test-time compute is a NEW scaling axis — orthogonal to parameters & pre-training.
  • A strong verifier beats a stronger generator — checking is easier than producing.
  • RLVR needs a checkable reward — math/code shine; open-ended is the hard part.
  • More thinking is not always better — curves saturate; overthinking can hurt.
This is the executive summary — I'll return to it as the recap. Watch how the five chain together. Because CoT is computation, you can scale it at inference — that's a brand-new axis next to "bigger model, more data." But scaling generation only helps if you can pick the good output, so a reliable verifier is worth more than a smarter generator. That same verifier, turned into a reward, is what powers RLVR — which is why math and code led the way: you can run the answer. And the honest caveat that keeps people grounded: the accuracy-versus-compute curve flattens, and on easy or factual tasks long reasoning can actively lower accuracy. Those five sentences are the spine of the whole deck.

Why it matters

A second scaling axis just opened

Train-time scalingmore params & data + Test-time scalingthink longer at inference → Better answerswithout a bigger model
  • For a decade: scale parameters & pre-training data — one forward pass per answer.
  • New lever: spend more compute at inference — sample more, search, think longer.
  • A small model + big inference budget can match a much larger model run once.
The framing for the whole module. Classic scaling laws turn one knob — grow the model and the data — and you get one forward pass per answer. Test-time compute is an independent axis: hold the weights fixed and let the model do more work per question, by sampling many candidate solutions, searching over reasoning steps, or simply generating a longer chain-of-thought. The striking empirical result — from Snell et al. and OpenAI's o1 reports — is that a modest model given a generous inference budget can rival a far larger model decoded greedily. That reframes a budget question every team faces: do I spend my next dollar on a bigger model, or on letting a smaller one think harder at serve time? The answer depends on the problem, and that trade-off is what this lecture is about.

Why it matters · where they sit

Reasoning models vs standard instruct models

Standard instruct (GPT-4o, Claude base)
  • Answers in one shot — little or no scratch work
  • Fast, cheap, low latency
  • Great for chat, retrieval, formatting, summaries
Reasoning model (o1/o3, R1)
  • Generates a long internal CoT before answering
  • Slower, pricier (you pay for thinking tokens)
  • Wins on math, code, multi-step logic, hard science

Same backbone, different habit: a reasoning model is post-trained (often via RL) to reason first, answer last.

A reasoning model is not a different architecture — it's the same transformer, post-trained to spend tokens thinking before it commits to an answer. The trade is concrete: you pay more latency and more tokens, and in return you get large gains on tasks where intermediate steps matter — competition math like AIME, hard coding, graduate-level science (GPQA). For a lookup, a rewrite, or a simple chat turn, that thinking is wasted cost and sometimes even hurts, which is why the frontier is increasingly hybrid: one model (or a router) that can run in a cheap non-thinking mode or switch on extended reasoning when the query warrants it. The mental model: instruct models blurt; reasoning models show their work — useful exactly when showing your work helps.

Intuition

Show your work vs blurt the answer

  • Ask a student 23 × 47 and demand an instant number — they guess.
  • Give them scratch paper and they decompose it: 23×40, 23×7, add — right answer.
  • A transformer is the same: one forward pass is fixed depth; it can't loop.
  • Intermediate tokens are scratch space the model attends back to — each step reads the last.
  • Writing the steps is the computation — serial compute traded for missing depth.
This analogy carries the whole "how it works" section. Force a student to answer 23 times 47 with no paper and they round and guess; hand them scratch paper and they break it into easy pieces and get it right. The model is identical. A single forward pass has a fixed number of layers — bounded depth, no loops — so some problems literally cannot be solved in one pass. But when the model writes intermediate tokens, those tokens go back into the context, and the next step can attend to them. So a long chain-of-thought turns a fixed-depth network into something that can do many sequential steps: it trades serial decoding time for the computational depth it lacks. The theory people frame this in complexity terms — with a long enough scratchpad, the class of problems a transformer can solve grows — but the intuition is just "let it use scratch paper."

🧠 "Thinking tokens" Operationally just output tokens — generated, KV-cached, and billed — but spent on reasoning, not on the visible answer. Claude's extended thinking exposes a token budget knob to cap them.
Two product philosophies for the same mechanism. DeepSeek-R1 shows the entire reasoning trace: it's transparent, debuggable, and great for research, but it can reveal the model's strategy to competitors and overwhelm everyday users with a wall of internal monologue. OpenAI's o1 hides the raw trace and shows only a short summary — the stated reasons are protecting intellectual property and, importantly, keeping the chain-of-thought un-optimized so it stays a faithful window for safety monitoring (if you train against the visible trace, it stops being honest). Either way, the key billing point students miss: those reasoning tokens are real output tokens — the model generates them, they fill the KV cache, and you pay for them — even when they're hidden. Claude's extended-thinking mode makes this explicit by exposing a thinking-token budget: a bigger budget means more reasoning and higher cost, which is the simplest version of the effort knob we'll cover later.

How it works · the pipeline

The test-time-compute pipeline, step by step

One pass: take the prompt, think in CoT, sample or search many traces, verify or vote, then return the best or consensus answer.

Drive this off the widget — it is the skeleton of the entire approaches section. A prompt arrives. Step two, the model thinks: it generates a chain-of-thought, the scratch space from the last slide. Step three is where test-time compute is actually spent: instead of one trace you draw N of them (parallel sampling) or you expand and prune a tree of partial reasoning steps (search). Step four is the decisive one: you reduce those many candidates to one, either by majority vote — self-consistency — or by scoring them with a verifier and keeping the best. Step five returns that selected answer rather than the model's first impulse. Every technique we cover plugs into box three (how you spend compute) or box four (how you choose the winner) — and the quality of box four is why "a strong verifier beats a stronger generator." Note one thing missing: a single greedy answer is just this pipeline with N equal to one and no verification.

How it works · the scaling curve

Accuracy climbs with compute — then saturates

  1. One long CoT: more thinking tokens → accuracy climbs steeply at first.
  2. Many short + vote: N parallel traces aggregated — cheap early wins.
  3. Both bend toward a ceiling — the model's underlying competence.
  4. Past the knee, each doubling of compute buys little — sometimes it dips.
  5. So budget is a per-problem choice, not "always max."

Note the x-axis is log compute — linear gains here cost exponential FLOPs.

This is the figure to linger on. Plot accuracy against the logarithm of inference compute and you get a rising, concave, saturating curve — the test-time scaling law. The indigo curve is one long sequential chain-of-thought: each extra thinking token can revise and extend the reasoning, so it keeps climbing but slowly. The teal curve is the parallel strategy — many shorter traces aggregated by voting or a verifier — which often wins cheaply at small budgets but plateaus once the right answer is already in the sampled set (more samples can't add an answer the model never produces). Both bend toward a ceiling set by the model's real competence; no amount of sampling fixes a problem it fundamentally can't do. The knee is the practical point: past it, every doubling of compute — remember the x-axis is log, so that's exponential FLOPs — buys almost nothing, and you can even see a dip from majority-voting pulling toward a confident-but-wrong mode. Hence the compute-optimal idea: spend big on hard problems, little on easy ones, rather than a flat max budget for everything.

How it works · two ways to spend

Parallel vs sequential scaling

Parallel — sample & aggregate
trace 1 trace 2 trace N → vote / verify
  • N independent traces, then majority vote or best-of-N.
  • Embarrassingly parallel — great wall-clock latency.
  • Fails when the right answer is never sampled; voting can lock in a popular-but-wrong answer.
Sequential — one self-revising trace
step→ revise→ revise→ answer
  • One long CoT that backtracks & self-corrects.
  • Can reach answers no single short sample would.
  • Serial → slow; can spiral into rambling or get stuck in one wrong frame.

Often combined: many sequential traces, then verify/vote — spend along both axes.

The two ways to convert a compute budget into accuracy, and they have opposite shapes. Parallel scaling draws N independent samples and aggregates them — majority vote for self-consistency, or pick the verifier's favorite for best-of-N. Because the samples don't depend on each other, you can run them all at once, so wall-clock latency stays low; the failure modes are that if the correct answer is never in the N samples no aggregation can recover it, and majority voting can confidently crown a wrong answer that the model happens to produce often. Sequential scaling instead spends the budget on one long trace that revises itself — it can notice a mistake, backtrack, and reach an answer no short sample would, which is exactly the "aha-moment" behavior R1 showed; but it's serial so it's slow, and a long trace can ramble, second-guess a correct answer, or get locked into one wrong framing it never escapes. In practice the strongest setups combine both: generate several long self-revising traces and then verify or vote across them, spending along both axes at once. The right mix is problem-dependent — which is the bridge into the sampling, voting, and search approaches.

The approaches · landscape

The reasoning ladder, baseline → SOTA

Direct→ CoT→ Self-consistency→ Best-of-N + verifier→ PRM / search→ Tree / MCTS→ STaR→ RLVR→ Long-CoT→ Distill

The thread: each rung buys accuracy by spending more inference compute, or by adding a better signal (a verifier / reward) to spend it well. Part A covers the first six rungs.

This is the spine of the whole topic, and it's exactly the territory interviewers probe with "why did we move from X to Y." Read it left to right: a single forward pass is the floor; chain-of-thought spends serial tokens as computation; self-consistency spends parallel samples and votes; best-of-N adds an outcome verifier so you can pick the best instead of the most common; process reward models score every step so you can guide search; tree/MCTS turns that signal into an explicit search over reasoning branches. The green rungs — STaR, RLVR, long-CoT, distillation — are part B, where we bake reasoning into the weights instead of paying for it at inference. Approaches · 1 of 6

Direct answer — single forward pass baseline

What: the model emits the answer immediately, with no intermediate tokens. How: prompt → one forward pass per output token → final answer; no "thinking" shown. Solves: nothing extra — it's the cheapest, lowest-latency baseline every other rung is measured against.

Pros
  • Fastest & cheapest — minimal tokens, lowest latency
  • Great for recall, formatting, simple lookups
Cons
  • Bounded serial depth — one pass = fixed compute per token
  • Fails multi-step math / logic it can't do "in its head"

vs previous: this is the "before" — the floor every reasoning method tries to climb above.

Start here so the contrast lands. A standard instruction-tuned model answers in one shot: the only computation available is the fixed depth of the network applied per token. For factual recall or formatting that's plenty. But a multi-step problem — a long arithmetic chain, a proof, a planning task — can exceed what a fixed-depth transformer can compute without writing anything down. That ceiling is the whole reason the ladder exists. Keep this slide as the reference point: every later rung is "spend more compute or add a signal" relative to this single pass.

How it works · Direct answer

Direct answer — step by step

Promptquestion → Forward passfixed depth → Answer tokenemit ↩ Stopno scratch-pad
  1. Feed the question; no instruction to reason.
  2. Each output token is one forward pass — bounded serial depth.
  3. The model commits to the answer with no intermediate work.
  4. Loop only to finish the (short) answer, then stop.
  5. Compute spent ≈ length of the answer, nothing more.
The key teaching point is "intermediate tokens are computation, and here there are none." With no scratch-pad, the model must map question straight to answer within the fixed depth of the stack. There's no place to store partial results, so anything requiring serial multi-step reasoning has to be compressed into that single pass. This is why a 7B model answering directly can lose to the same 7B model given room to think out loud — same weights, but the second one is allowed to spend more serial compute.

Approaches · 2 of 6

Chain-of-Thought prompting classic

What: ask the model to "think step by step" so it writes its reasoning before the answer (Wei et al., 2022). How: zero-shot ("Let's think step by step") or few-shot (worked examples in the prompt) → the model emits intermediate steps → final answer. Solves: the bounded-depth problem — intermediate tokens are extra computation.

Pros
  • Huge gains on math / logic / multi-step tasks
  • Prompt-only — no training needed
Cons
  • More tokens → more latency & cost
  • Single greedy chain can commit early to a wrong path

vs previous: the direct pass had nowhere to compute; CoT turns the output stream into a scratch-pad — serial compute for depth.

Read more ↗

This is the founding insight of test-time compute: the generated tokens feed back into the context, so a chain of intermediate steps lets the transformer trade serial compute for the depth a single pass lacks. Two flavors: few-shot CoT (Wei et al. — put a couple of worked examples in the prompt) and zero-shot CoT (Kojima et al. — just append "Let's think step by step"). The cost is real — more tokens means more latency and dollars — and a single greedy chain can lock onto an early mistake and confidently carry it to the end. That weakness is precisely what the next rung, self-consistency, attacks.

How it works · Chain-of-Thought

Chain-of-Thought — step by step

Prompt+ "think step by step" → Step 1intermediate → Step 2builds on 1 → Step n... → Answerafter work
  1. Zero-shot: append "Let's think step by step" — no examples needed.
  2. Few-shot: show 1–8 worked examples with their reasoning, then ask.
  3. Model emits step 1; that text re-enters the context.
  4. Each step conditions on all prior steps — serial computation.
  5. Final answer comes after the work, not before.
Walk the autoregressive loop and emphasize that each emitted step becomes input to the next forward pass — that's the mechanism by which writing things down adds effective depth. Few-shot CoT teaches the format by example; zero-shot CoT relies on the magic trigger phrase and works surprisingly well on capable models. Stress the ordering: in a direct answer the model commits first; in CoT the answer is produced last, after the intermediate computation, which is why correctness improves. The remaining fragility — one chain, one chance — sets up sampling many chains next.

Approaches · 3 of 6

Self-consistency classic

What: sample many CoT traces and take the majority final answer (Wang et al., 2022). How: set temperature > 0 → sample K independent chains → extract each final answer → vote → return the mode. Solves: a single greedy chain is fragile; voting marginalizes over many reasoning paths.

Pros
  • Big accuracy jump on math — no extra model needed
  • Embarrassingly parallel; just decoding
Cons
  • K× the inference cost
  • Needs an extractable, discrete answer to vote on

vs previous: CoT bet everything on one chain; self-consistency samples many and lets agreement filter out lucky-wrong paths.

Read more ↗

The intuition is that there are many valid reasoning paths to a correct answer but lots of distinct ways to be wrong, so correct answers cluster while errors scatter — majority vote exploits that. Practically: turn on temperature, draw K chains, parse the final answer from each, and return the most common one. It reliably beats greedy single-chain CoT on math benchmarks for free, model-wise — you only pay K times the decoding. Two caveats to flag: you need a discrete, parseable answer to count votes over (great for math, awkward for open-ended text), and the most common answer isn't always the best answer — which is exactly the gap best-of-N with a verifier fills next.

How it works · Self-consistency

Self-consistency — step by step

One prompt+ CoT → Sample Ktemp > 0 → K answersextract finals → Votecount modes → Majorityreturn
  1. Take one CoT prompt; sample K traces at temperature > 0.
  2. Each trace reasons differently — diverse paths.
  3. Extract just the final answer from each trace.
  4. Tally votes over the discrete answers.
  5. Return the most common answer; discard the reasoning.
Drive the steps off the widget. The load-bearing move is sampling at nonzero temperature so the chains actually diverge — at temperature zero you'd get K copies of the same trace and voting is pointless. The vote tally on screen makes the intuition concrete: seven chains land on 42, a couple wander to wrong answers, so 42 wins. Note we throw the reasoning away and keep only the count — that's both the strength (cheap, no extra model) and the limitation: we trust frequency, not quality. When the right answer is in the minority, voting can't find it.

Approaches · 4 of 6

Best-of-N + outcome verifier (ORM) modern

What: generate N candidates, score each with an outcome reward model, keep the best (Cobbe et al., 2021). How: sample N solutions → an ORM (or checker) assigns each a score → pick the top-scoring one. Solves: voting picks the most common; BoN can pick a rare-but-correct answer if the scorer recognizes it.

Pros
  • Finds correct answers that are in the minority
  • Works on open-ended outputs voting can't count
Cons
  • Needs a trained verifier / scorer that plain voting doesn't
  • Reward hacking: model games an imperfect scorer at large N

vs previous: self-consistency only counts answers; BoN adds a judgment signal so quality, not frequency, decides.

Read more ↗

This is the first rung that needs a new component: a verifier. An outcome reward model scores a whole solution by its final answer's plausibility; a deterministic checker (unit tests, a symbolic math checker) is even better when available because it can't be fooled. The advantage over majority vote is decisive when the right answer is rare — voting buries it, but a good scorer surfaces it — and BoN works on free-form outputs where there's nothing discrete to count. The danger to name: as N grows you increasingly find the candidate that best exploits the verifier's blind spots rather than the truly best one, so verifier quality caps the whole scheme. That motivates scoring the process, not just the outcome.

How it works · Best-of-N

Best-of-N + verifier — step by step

Promptquestion → Sample Ncandidates → Verifier scoresORM / checker → Rankby score → Pick bestargmax
  1. Sample N full solutions (each its own CoT).
  2. Score every candidate with the ORM (or run the checker).
  3. Rank by score — not by how often an answer appears.
  4. Return the single argmax candidate.
  5. Optional: weighted self-consistency — sum scores per answer, then vote.
Run the widget to contrast with the previous slide: same N samples, but now a scorer ranks them instead of a vote counting them. Emphasize the new dependency — you must have built or trained a verifier, which is the cost self-consistency avoided. Mention the hybrid on the last step: weighted self-consistency sums the verifier's scores for each distinct answer and then takes the top-scoring answer, blending the robustness of voting with the discrimination of a scorer. And reiterate the failure mode — a weak ORM can be gamed as N climbs, which is why deterministic checkers are prized and why the field moved to scoring each step.

Approaches · 5 of 6

Process reward models (PRM) modern

What: score each reasoning step, not just the final answer (Lightman et al., "Let's Verify Step by Step", 2023). How: label steps as good/bad → train a PRM to score per-step → use it to rerank chains or guide search at inference. Solves: an ORM only sees the end — a PRM catches the wrong turn that produced a right-looking answer.

Pros
  • Dense, localized feedback — finds where reasoning broke
  • Beats ORM on hard math; can steer search step-by-step
Cons
  • Expensive to label — humans annotating every step
  • Step boundaries & partial-credit are fuzzy to define

vs previous: the ORM scores one number at the end; the PRM scores the whole trajectory, giving a signal you can search against mid-chain.

Read more ↗

The headline result: process supervision beat outcome supervision on the MATH benchmark, and the team released the PRM800K step-level labels. Why it works — an outcome model can be fooled by a lucky-correct answer reached through broken logic, whereas a PRM penalizes the bad step directly, giving dense localized credit. The big cost is labeling: someone must judge every step as helpful/neutral/wrong, which is slow and subjective, and defining where one "step" ends is genuinely fuzzy. The caveat to flag for the hard version of this question: the gains were strongest in-domain on math; how well process supervision generalizes to other domains is an open question. Crucially, a PRM gives you a value signal mid-chain — the precondition for searching over reasoning steps, which is the last rung in part A.

How it works · PRM

Process reward model — step by step

Chainstep 1..n → Label stepsgood / bad → Train PRMper-step score → Score new chaineach step → Rerank / guidesearch
  1. Collect chains; annotate each step as correct / wrong.
  2. Train a model to predict that per-step correctness.
  3. At inference, score every step of a new chain.
  4. Aggregate (e.g. min or product of step scores) to rerank candidates.
  5. Or feed scores into a search to prune bad branches early.
Use the widget to dramatize the ORM-vs-PRM difference: both agree while the chain is healthy, then the PRM flags the exact step where reasoning breaks while an outcome model, seeing only a plausible final answer, can be fooled. Mention the aggregation choice — taking the minimum step score (the chain is only as good as its worst step) or the product is common for reranking. And foreshadow the next rung: because the PRM gives a value at every intermediate node, it's exactly the scoring function you plug into a tree search to decide which branch to expand.

Approaches · 6 of 6

Tree-of-Thoughts / lookahead / MCTS modern

What: search over branches of reasoning steps, guided by a value signal (Yao et al., ToT, 2023). How: expand multiple next-steps → score each with a verifier/PRM or lookahead → keep promising branches, prune dead ends, optionally MCTS-style rollout & backprop. Solves: linear CoT can't backtrack; search explores alternatives and recovers from wrong turns.

Pros
  • Explores & backtracks — best on puzzles / planning
  • Spends compute where the value signal says it pays off
Cons
  • Costly — many model calls per step; cost grows with branching × depth
  • Needs a reliable value signal; weak scorer → misguided search

vs previous: a PRM scores a fixed chain; tree/MCTS uses that score to decide which branches to grow — search, not just rerank.

Read more ↗

This is the most powerful and most expensive rung in part A. The shift from token-level beam search: here a "node" is a whole reasoning step, and you need a step-level scoring signal — a PRM, a lookahead heuristic, or a self-evaluation prompt — to decide which partial solutions are worth expanding. Tree-of-Thoughts does BFS/DFS over thought branches with that value; full MCTS adds the four-phase loop. Be explicit about cost in the interview: roughly branching factor times depth times rollouts model calls, so it can be orders of magnitude more expensive than parallel sampling. The honest takeaway: for problems with a clear value signal and room to backtrack (puzzles, planning, hard proofs) it shines; for everything else, many short CoTs with a vote is usually the better spend of the same FLOPs.

How it works · MCTS over reasoning

Tree search / MCTS — step by step

Rootproblem → Selectpromising node → Expandbranch next-steps → Rolloutsimulate to end ↩ Backpropupdate values
  1. Select: walk the tree to a promising node (value + exploration bonus, UCT).
  2. Expand: generate several candidate next reasoning steps as children.
  3. Simulate / rollout: reason forward to a terminal answer, score it.
  4. Backpropagate: push that value up to every ancestor node.
  5. Repeat; finally pick the best path. Prune dead ends along the way.
Narrate the four MCTS phases with the widget; this exact select / expand / simulate / backpropagate decomposition is a classic hard-question prompt. Selection balances exploiting high-value nodes against exploring under-visited ones, typically via a UCT-style bonus. Expansion is where the LLM proposes candidate next steps; simulation rolls one out to a scored terminal state; backpropagation updates the running value estimates of every node on the path so the next iteration is smarter. The loop arrow signals you repeat within a compute budget. Close by tying the whole part-A ladder together: we climbed from one pass, to one chain, to many chains voted, to many scored, to per-step scored, to searching the step-space — each rung either spent more inference compute or added a better signal to spend it well. Part B asks: can we train the model so it does this itself?

The approaches · part B landscape

From data tricks to trained reasoning — the RL era

STaR / RFT→ RLVR + GRPO→ Long-CoT models→ Distillation→ Budget forcing→ Tool-integrated

Part A scaled compute at inference (voting, search). Part B trains the reasoning in — verifiers become reward signals, then we control and tool-augment the result.

This is the map for part B and the part interviewers grill hardest. Part A was inference-time: sample more, vote, search. Part B is where the modern reasoning models are actually made. The thread: STaR bootstraps reasoning data by keeping verified-correct traces; RLVR turns that verifier into a dense RL reward and GRPO makes the RL cheap by dropping the critic; long-CoT "thinking" models are the products built on large-scale RLVR; distillation copies that reasoning into small models cheaply; budget forcing lets you dial effort up or down at inference; and tool integration offloads the parts LLMs are bad at — arithmetic, lookup — to real code and search. Each rung answers a gap in the one before.

Approaches · 7 of 12

STaR / rejection-sampling fine-tuning bootstrap

What: bootstrap reasoning data — sample chains-of-thought, keep only the verified-correct ones, fine-tune, repeat (Zelikman et al., 2022; aka RFT). How: for problems with known answers, sample several CoT traces → keep traces whose final answer matches → rationalize the rest by feeding the answer as a hint → SFT on survivors → loop. Solves: you have no reasoning traces to train on — the model writes its own and the verifier labels them.

Pros
  • On-policy: trains on the model's own correct reasoning
  • Verifier guarantees the final answer is right
  • Simple SFT loop — no RL machinery
Cons
  • Needs known answers / a reliable verifier
  • Reinforces lucky-but-wrong traces (right answer, bad reasoning)
  • Plateaus once the model can't solve harder problems

vs previous: earlier rungs trust the generator's text; STaR closes the loop with a verifier and only keeps traces that check out.

Read more ↗

STaR — Self-Taught Reasoner — is the seed of every modern reasoning-data pipeline. The leap over plain distillation is that it filters to correctness with a verifier instead of trusting whatever the model wrote. For each problem with a known answer, sample several CoT traces, keep the ones whose final answer matches the gold answer, and fine-tune on those. STaR's clever twist is rationalization: for problems the model can't solve, hand it the answer as a hint and keep whatever rationale reaches it — so you still harvest a reasoning trace for the hard cases. Then loop: the improved model solves slightly more next round. "On-policy" because you train on the model's own correct reasoning, not a teacher's. The danger to flag: a trace with flawed reasoning that happens to land on the right number gets reinforced — this is why later work adds process-level checks. And the loop plateaus once the model simply cannot reach the harder answers even with hints.

Walk the loop off the steps. The key insight is that you never need human-written reasoning traces — only known final answers, which math and code give you for free. Each round the SFT'd model is slightly stronger, so the verified yield climbs — the flywheel. The math · (a) self-consistency

Self-consistency = marginalize over reasoning paths

Sample \(N\) chains-of-thought; each trace \(i\) ends in a final answer \(a_i\). Pick the answer that the most traces agree on:

$$ \hat{a} \;=\; \operatorname*{arg\,max}_{a}\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbf{1}\!\left[\,a_i = a\,\right] $$

\(a_i\) = parsed final answer of trace \(i\); \(\mathbf{1}[\cdot]\) = 1 when they match, else 0; the sum just counts votes for each answer.

Why it works: the reasoning path \(r\) is a latent variable. We want \(p(a\mid x)=\sum_r p(a,r\mid x)\) → summing over many paths to the same answer marginalizes out the path. Greedy decoding commits to one path; voting integrates over many.

Many wrong chains scatter across different wrong answers; correct chains concentrate on the one right answer → the mode is usually correct.

Self-consistency, from Wang et al. 2022, is the cleanest piece of math in the deck. The model defines a joint distribution over an answer and a reasoning path; the answer we actually care about is the marginal, summing the joint over all paths. We can't enumerate paths, so we Monte-Carlo it: sample N chains-of-thought at nonzero temperature, parse each one's final answer, and take the answer with the most votes — that's the argmax of the indicator sum, which is just counting. The intuition for why it beats greedy decoding: greedy commits to the single most-likely path, but the most-likely path isn't always correct. There are usually many distinct reasoning routes to the right answer, so correct traces pile up on one value while wrong traces spread thin across many different wrong values. The mode of the votes is therefore a much better estimate than any single sample. The failure mode to flag: if the model has a systematic bias, all the wrong chains can agree on the same wrong answer, and then voting confidently amplifies the error rather than averaging it out.

The math · (b) GRPO advantage

GRPO: the group is the baseline

For a prompt, sample a group of \(G\) completions and score each with the verifiable reward \(r_i\). The advantage of completion \(i\) is its reward, z-scored within its own group:

$$ \hat{A}_i \;=\; \frac{r_i - \operatorname{mean}(r_{1\ldots G})}{\operatorname{std}(r_{1\ldots G})} $$

\(r_i\) = reward of completion \(i\) (e.g. \(1\) if the checker says correct, else \(0\)); \(\operatorname{mean}\) over the group = the baseline; \(\operatorname{std}\) normalizes scale.

No learned critic. PPO trains a separate value network for the baseline; GRPO just uses the group's mean → half the memory, simpler, more stable. Above-average completions get \(\hat{A}_i \gt 0\) (reinforced); below-average get \(\hat{A}_i \lt 0\) (suppressed).

The full objective adds a KL leash to a frozen reference policy:

$$ \mathcal{J} \;=\; \mathbb{E}\!\left[\, \hat{A}_i \cdot \rho_i \,\right] \;-\; \beta\, \mathbb{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(\pi_\theta \,\|\, \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}\right) $$

\(\rho_i\) = clipped probability ratio \(\pi_\theta/\pi_{\mathrm{old}}\) (the PPO-style surrogate); \(\beta\) = KL coefficient. The KL term stops the policy drifting far from the reference → guards against reward hacking & entropy collapse. Drop it / set \(\beta\) too low and the model degenerates.

GRPO, from the DeepSeekMath and R1 papers, is the algorithm everyone asks about. The key trick is in the advantage: instead of training a separate value network to predict expected reward — which is what PPO does and which doubles your memory footprint — GRPO samples a whole group of G completions for the same prompt, scores them all with the verifiable reward, and uses the group's own mean as the baseline. The advantage is then just the reward z-scored within the group: subtract the mean, divide by the standard deviation. A completion that beat its siblings gets a positive advantage and is reinforced; one that did worse than its group average gets a negative advantage and is pushed down. That's why "the group is the baseline" — no critic to train, no critic to go unstable. The reward itself is verifiable: a deterministic checker returns one for a correct math answer or passing unit tests, zero otherwise — no learned preference model like RLHF. The second equation is the actual objective: the clipped PPO surrogate using that advantage, minus a KL penalty pulling the policy toward a frozen reference. The KL leash is load-bearing — it stops the policy from sprinting off to whatever degenerate text maximizes reward, which is how you get reward hacking, language mixing, and entropy collapse. Set beta too low or remove it and the model quickly degenerates into gibberish that games the checker.

The math · (c) test-time scaling

Accuracy rises ~linearly in log-compute, then saturates

  1. Spend more test-time compute (more samples, longer CoT, deeper search).
  2. Early on, accuracy climbs ~linearly in the log of compute → each doubling buys a roughly constant gain.
  3. Then it saturates: extra compute searches paths the model can't reach, or re-finds known answers.
  4. The precondition: a generation–verification gap — the model can generate a correct answer somewhere in N tries even if it can't pick it greedily.

If pass@1 \(\approx\) pass@N there is no gap → sampling & search buy nothing. Search only helps when pass@N \(\gg\) pass@1: a verifier (or vote) recovers the answer the model could produce.

Two ideas, both heavily probed. First, the shape: plot accuracy against the log of test-time compute — whether that compute is number of samples, chain length, or search width — and you get a curve that rises roughly linearly in log-compute and then flattens into a plateau. Linear-in-log means each doubling of compute buys a roughly constant additive gain in the productive regime; that's the same diminishing-returns shape as train-time scaling laws. Why it saturates: once you've sampled enough, extra samples just re-discover answers you already found, and search starts exploring branches the base model simply can't produce, so you hit a ceiling. Second, and this is the deeper point the source insists on: test-time scaling only works when there is a generation-verification gap. That gap means the model can generate a correct solution somewhere among N attempts even though its single greedy attempt is wrong — verifying or voting is easier than generating. The clean diagnostic is pass@1 versus pass@N: if pass@N is much larger than pass@1, the answer is reachable and a verifier or majority vote can recover it, so search pays off. But if pass@1 already equals pass@N, the model never produces the right answer at all, the gap is zero, and no amount of sampling or search will help — you need a better generator, not more test-time compute. That distinction is exactly what separates problems where reasoning helps from ones where it's wasted.

import re
from collections import Counter

def extract_answer(text):
    # grab the last "#### <number>" or final integer in the trace
    m = re.findall(r"-?\d+", text.split("####")[-1])
    return m[-1] if m else None

def self_consistency(model, prompt, n=64, temperature=0.7):
    answers = []
    for _ in range(n):
        # sample a DIFFERENT chain-of-thought each call (temp > 0)
        trace = model.generate(prompt, temperature=temperature)
        a = extract_answer(trace)
        if a is not None:          # drop unparseable traces
            answers.append(a)
    # majority vote == argmax of the vote counts
    votes = Counter(answers)
    return votes.most_common(1)[0][0], votes

# (an ORM, a PRM's product of step scores, or a unit-test pass-rate)

def weighted_self_consistency(model, prompt, verifier, n=64, t=0.7):
    score = defaultdict(float)
    for _ in range(n):
        trace = model.generate(prompt, temperature=t)
        a = extract_answer(trace)
        if a is None:
            continue
        w = verifier(prompt, trace)     # confidence in THIS trace
        score[a] += w                   # sum weights, not counts
    if not score:
        return None
    # pick the answer with the most TOTAL verifier mass
    return max(score, key=score.get)

# best-of-N is the special case: argmax over single-trace score

The one-line change that unifies three methods. Instead of every trace casting an equal vote, we ask a verifier — an outcome reward model, a process reward model's product of per-step scores, or for code the fraction of unit tests that pass — for a confidence weight on each trace, and we sum those weights per answer instead of counting. The teaching point is the two comments at the bottom: plain self-consistency is exactly this with the verifier pinned to one, and best-of-N is this with the aggregation changed to take the single highest-scoring trace rather than summing across agreeing traces. So self-consistency, weighted voting, and best-of-N are three points on one continuum: how much you trust an external verifier versus the crowd's agreement. Weighting helps when the verifier is genuinely informative; if the verifier is noisy or gameable, equal-weight voting can actually be more robust, because best-of-N over-optimizes a flawed reward model.

Make the self-consistency math concrete on a realistic AIME-style example. Take a model that's capable but unreliable: each independent chain-of-thought has about a 35% chance of being right. Run it once greedily and you're at 35% — basically a coin flip on a hard problem. Now sample 64 chains at temperature 0.7 for diversity. The correct answer 204 shows up in, say, 22 of them, because there are several valid routes to it and they all converge on the same number. The 42 wrong chains don't converge — they make different mistakes and land on a dozen different wrong numbers, so the biggest wrong pile is only 6. Majority vote picks the 22-vote pile and gets it right. The structural reason this works every time the model is "right more often than any single wrong answer": correct reasoning concentrates on one value, wrong reasoning diffuses across many. The number that proves the headroom existed is pass@64 — the chance the right answer appeared in at least one of 64 tries — which for a 35% model is about 99%. That huge gap between pass@1 at 35% and pass@64 near 99% is the generation-verification gap, and majority voting is the cheapest way to cash it in: no reward model, just a parser and a Counter.

Worked example · (2) one long CoT vs many short + vote

Fixed budget: 6,400 generated tokens. Same model, same problem. How should you spend them?

This is the compute-allocation question — the same FLOP budget, two strategies, and the answer is "it depends on the problem," which is exactly what interviewers want you to articulate. Fix the budget at 6,400 generated tokens. Option A pours it all into one long chain-of-thought: a single trajectory that can revise itself, backtrack, and verify, which is what o1-style long-CoT models do. The risk is correlation — it's one sample, so if the model anchors on a wrong framing in the first few hundred tokens, the remaining 6,000 tokens just elaborate the mistake, and you also pay full sequential latency because token N waits for token N minus one. Option B slices the budget into 64 short 100-token chains and majority-votes — the section 9 loop. That buys independence: 64 uncorrelated shots, and the vote marginalizes out the path, so it shines precisely when there's a wide generation-verification gap and the final answer is checkable. It's also far faster in wall-clock because the 64 samples run in parallel. The trade-off cuts both ways: if the problem genuinely needs ten serial deduction steps, 100 tokens can't even reach an answer and breadth is wasted; if the steps are individually easy but the model is just noisy, breadth crushes depth. The empirically best systems usually combine them — many medium-length traces scored by a verifier (best-of-N or PRM search), or a long CoT with self-consistency layered on top — rather than going all-in on either extreme.

When to use what · routing

Walk the tree top-down. The root question is checkability, because it determines whether a verifier exists — and a verifier is what makes best-of-N and search pay off. Left branch: math and code have deterministic checkers (answer match, unit tests), so an RLVR-trained model plus verifier-based best-of-N converts extra samples directly into accuracy. Middle: multi-step tasks with no automatic check (open derivations, planning) get long chain-of-thought with self-consistency voting, or PRM-guided step search if you have a process reward model. Right: simple recall or strict formatting — "capital of France?", JSON reshaping — should skip reasoning entirely; the first instinct is already correct and thinking only adds latency and a chance to overthink. Bottom: if latency is the hard constraint, force short CoT or route adaptively. The meta-point: pick the lane by checkability first, then size the compute by difficulty and your latency budget.

When to use what · the effort dial

One budget, three ways to spend it

Given a fixed compute budget, you can spend it in parallel, in sequence, or on search — and you should spend more on hard problems, less on easy ones.

Fixed budgetFLOPs / tokens → Parallel voteN short CoTs → Sequentialone long CoT → Searchtree / MCTS → Adaptiveper-difficulty
  1. Parallel — draw N samples, vote/verify. Low latency, robust to a single bad trace; needs answers that cluster or a verifier.
  2. Sequential — one long self-revising trace. Deep dependency & self-correction; high latency, can derail.
  3. Search — tree / MCTS over steps. Best for large branching spaces with a strong step-verifier; most expensive.
  4. Adaptive allocation — estimate difficulty (e.g. early-sample agreement) and give hard-but-solvable problems more budget, easy ones almost none.

Compute-optimal > uniform: a uniform budget over-spends on easy and under-spends on hard. Allocate where marginal accuracy-per-FLOP is highest.

The effort dial has three positions and a controller. Parallel scaling is embarrassingly parallel and low-latency — many short CoTs aggregated by majority vote or a verifier; it shines when correct answers cluster or you have a good verifier, but voting reinforces a confidently-wrong dominant mode. Sequential scaling is one long, self-revising trace — it builds on and corrects earlier work for deep dependencies, at the cost of latency and the risk of locking onto a wrong path. Search (tree / MCTS) uses a step-level verifier to prune a large branching space — powerful but an order of magnitude more LLM calls and largely sequential. The controller on top is adaptive, per-difficulty allocation, which is the compute-optimal idea from Snell et al.: estimate each problem's difficulty — cheaply, via agreement among a couple of early samples or a difficulty predictor — and route budget so genuinely hard-but-solvable problems get more and easy ones get almost none. A uniform budget wastes compute on the easy tail and starves the hard middle; adaptive allocation gets higher accuracy at the same total cost.

Pitfalls · misconceptions

People think X, but actually Y (1/2)

⚠ Pitfall
  • Think: more thinking always helps. Actually: on simple recall/format tasks long CoT hurts — the model talks itself out of a correct first instinct — and it always raises latency & cost.
  • Think: the visible CoT explains the answer. Actually: CoT can be unfaithful — right answer reached by a wrong or irrelevant trace; the stated steps may not be what the model actually used.
  • Think: a verifiable reward is safe to optimize hard. Actually: reward hacking — length gaming, hard-coding test outputs, exploiting a lenient answer-extractor — meets the reward without solving the task.
First three, and the first is the one teammates get wrong constantly. "More thinking always helps" — no: on factual recall or strict formatting the first-instinct answer is usually right, and extended reasoning gives the model room to introduce distractors, rationalize a wrong alternative, or drift off the required format. Net negative accuracy plus guaranteed extra latency and cost — the phenomenon is overthinking. Two: the visible CoT is not a reliable explanation. Faithfulness research shows a model can reach the right answer through a trace that is decorative, post-hoc, or simply not the causal path it used; test it by perturbing or truncating the CoT and seeing if the answer changes. Three: a verifiable reward feels unhackable but the policy optimizes the signal, not the task — it games length, special-cases known test inputs, hard-codes expected outputs, or exploits a sloppy regex extractor. The reward goes up while real reasoning stays bogus.

Pitfalls · misconceptions

People think X, but actually Y (2/2)

⚠ Pitfall
  • Think: pass@64 is high, so the model is great. Actually: pass@k counts a problem solved if any of k tries works — it measures coverage, not single-shot reliability. pass@1 is the honest number.
  • Think: if you don't state k, the score still compares. Actually: always report k, temperature, and the selector (maj@k / BoN@k) — otherwise numbers aren't comparable and overstate deployed performance.
  • Think: a high benchmark score = real reasoning. Actually: contamination — AIME/MATH/GPQA and their solutions are likely in pretraining, so scores can reflect memorization. Test on fresh/post-cutoff problems and perturbed variants.
Three evaluation traps. The pass@64 one is the most seductive: pass@k marks a problem solved if any of k samples succeeds, so it climbs with k and measures the model's coverage — whether a correct trace exists in the sample set — not whether the model reliably produces one in a single shot. In production you usually get one answer, so pass@1 is the honest deployed number; a big pass@1-to-pass@64 gap is a selection/reliability problem you fix with a verifier or RL, not evidence of greatness. That's why you must always disclose k, the temperature, and the aggregation method — maj@k and BoN@k additionally depend on the voter or verifier — or the comparison is meaningless and inflated. Finally contamination: the popular reasoning benchmarks and their worked solutions are widely published and almost certainly in pretraining data, and reasoning models are precisely sold on reasoning, so a memorized answer masquerades as reasoning. Detect it with freshly released problems (a new AIME year), number/phrasing perturbations that should preserve difficulty, and private held-out sets; report on contamination-controlled subsets.

How reasoning models actually got built

🚀 What's new
  • CoT → sampling → search. Chain-of-thought, then self-consistency voting, then Tree-of-Thoughts — test-time compute became a first-class lever.
  • Verifiers & process supervision. "Let's Verify Step by Step" showed step-level PRMs beat outcome-only rewards for selecting correct solutions.
  • RL from verifiable rewards. STaR’s generate-verify-retrain loop → GRPO (DeepSeekMath) → DeepSeek-R1, which got reasoning to emerge from pure RL.
  • Test-time scaling laws. Snell et al. quantified accuracy-vs-compute; s1 showed budget-forcing controls trace length with a tiny SFT set.
  • Products. OpenAI o1/o3 (hidden traces) and DeepSeek-R1 (visible) made long-CoT reasoning mainstream.
The arc of the whole field in one slide. It starts with chain-of-thought (Wei) making intermediate tokens a tool, then self-consistency (Wang) showing you can sample many traces and vote, then Tree-of-Thoughts (Yao) generalizing voting into deliberate search. In parallel, the verifier line: "Let's Verify Step by Step" (Lightman) showed process reward models that grade each step beat outcome-only rewards for selection on hard math.   • What distinguishes a reasoning model (o1/o3, R1) from a standard instruction-tuned LLM? Easy▸ reveal
    Reasoning models are trained (largely via RL on verifiable rewards) to generate a long internal chain-of-thought before answering — spending test-time compute to plan, branch, and self-correct — and usually expose a thinking/effort budget. Same transformer architecture; the difference is training objective and decoding behavior. They trade latency & tokens for accuracy on multi-step tasks, not on simple ones.
  • Why do intermediate CoT tokens let a transformer solve problems one forward pass cannot? Med▸ reveal
    A transformer has fixed depth, so one pass does a bounded number of serial steps. Each emitted token is fed back as input, so generating intermediate tokens unrolls computation along the sequence — adding serial depth via the autoregressive loop — and the scratchpad acts as external working memory for intermediate results.
  • Define test-time compute and give three ways to spend more of it. Easy▸ reveal
    The FLOPs/tokens spent generating an answer after training is fixed. Three mechanisms: longer chain-of-thought (more serial tokens); parallel sampling + aggregation (self-consistency or best-of-N with a verifier); and search (beam/lookahead/MCTS over reasoning steps). Also tool calls and self-refinement loops.
  • Explain self-consistency; why does majority vote over CoT beat greedy on math? Easy▸ reveal
    Sample multiple CoT traces at nonzero temperature, then take the most frequent final answer (marginalize over paths). It wins because there are many valid paths to the right answer but errors are diverse and scattered — correct answers cluster, wrong ones disperse. It counts final answers, not trace quality — it does not pick the "best" trace.
  • Best-of-N vs majority voting — what does BoN require that voting doesn't, and when prefer each? Med▸ reveal
    BoN picks the single highest-scoring candidate, so it needs a verifier/reward model; voting just counts answer frequency and needs no scorer. Prefer voting for discrete checkable answers that cluster; prefer BoN when you have a strong/deterministic verifier or free-form outputs that don't vote cleanly. Weighted (verifier-weighted) voting combines both.
  • What is the generation-verification gap, and why is it the precondition for search/BoN? Med▸ reveal
    The asymmetry where verifying/scoring a candidate is easier or more accurate than generating a correct one in one shot. When that gap exists, sampling many candidates and selecting via the verifier yields net gains. No gap → selection adds no information and BoN/search can't beat a single good sample.
  • What is RLVR, and how does its reward differ from RLHF's learned preference reward? Easy▸ reveal
    RL from Verifiable Rewards: the reward comes from automatically checking the final answer (answer match, unit tests, compiler) — objective, usually binary, hard to game on the answer itself. RLHF instead uses a learned reward model trained on human preferences — subjective, approximate, and exploitable. RLVR is limited to domains where correctness is checkable.
  • Explain GRPO; how does it estimate advantages without a critic? Med▸ reveal
    Group Relative Policy Optimization samples a group of G completions per prompt, scores each, and sets each advantage to its reward standardized within the group: Aₙ = (rₙ − mean(r)) / std(r). The group mean is the baseline, so no value network is needed; then a PPO-style clipped update with a KL penalty. All-equal-reward groups give zero advantage → no gradient.
  • What is reward hacking in RLVR on math/code? Give two examples. Med▸ reveal
    The policy maximizes the reward signal without genuinely solving the task. (1) Grader exploitation — hard-coding expected test outputs or exploiting a lenient answer-extractor so a wrong solution scores correct. (2) Format/length gaming — emitting a right-looking boxed number via an invalid route, or padding the trace because length correlates with reward.
  • Correct final answers but incoherent/irrelevant reasoning — what's happening, and how to detect/fix? Hard▸ reveal
    Outcome-only RLVR rewards the endpoint, so the model can reach it via shortcuts while the visible trace is decorative or unfaithful. Detect: perturb/truncate the CoT and check if the answer changes; have a PRM/judge score step validity. Fix: add process supervision, penalize unfaithful traces, or rejection-sample coherent correct traces.
  • Argue against "reasoning always improves accuracy, so always max thinking." Med▸ reveal
    On simple factual/formatting tasks long CoT can introduce errors (overthinking — second-guessing a correct first instinct), lowering accuracy while always raising latency and cost. Beyond a model's capability, extra thinking doesn't help either. Counterexamples: "capital of France?", strict JSON reformatting, simple classification — direct answering is faster and at least as accurate.
  • What is compute-optimal allocation across difficulties, and why beat a uniform budget? Hard▸ reveal
    Give each problem the test-time compute that maximizes marginal accuracy per FLOP: easy problems get little (already solved), hard-but-solvable get more, hopeless ones aren't wasted on. A uniform budget over-spends on easy and under-spends on hard. Estimate difficulty (early-sample agreement, a predictor) and route budget accordingly for more accuracy at the same cost.
  • pass@1 is low but pass@64 is high — what does the gap mean, and how do you act? Med▸ reveal
    The model can generate correct solutions (coverage exists) but can't reliably produce/select one in a single attempt — a selection/reliability gap, not pure capability. Act: at inference add a verifier/reranker or self-consistency/BoN; in training do RL or rejection-sampling SFT on the correct traces to make the right path the default. A strong verifier is often the highest-leverage fix.
  • Why is CoT faithfulness a safety/eval concern, and how do you test it? Hard▸ reveal
    If the visible CoT doesn't reflect the actual computation, you can't trust it for oversight or as evidence — it may rationalize, hide the real (possibly unsafe) reason, or be right by luck. Test causally: perturb/truncate the CoT and see if the answer changes; inject hints/errors and check whether the model silently uses or acknowledges them.

Takeaways

What to remember

  • CoT is computation: intermediate tokens add serial depth a fixed-depth pass lacks — thinking is spending test-time compute.
  • Checkability picks the lane: verifiable → RLVR + best-of-N; multi-step → CoT + self-consistency / PRM; simple → no reasoning.
  • The gap is the precondition: search & BoN only pay off when verifying is easier than generating.
  • Spend compute where it helps: parallel vote, sequential long-CoT, or search — allocated per difficulty, not uniformly.
  • RLVR powers modern reasoning: STaR → GRPO → R1 — but watch reward hacking, length gaming, and entropy collapse.
  • Be honest in eval: report k & sampling; pass@1 is the deployed number; guard against contamination & unfaithful CoT.
The recap — the spine of the lecture, now earned. Chain-of-thought is literally extra serial computation, so "thinking" is the act of spending test-time compute. The single most useful decision rule is checkability: it tells you whether a verifier exists and therefore which lane — RLVR plus best-of-N for math/code, CoT plus self-consistency or a PRM for un-checkable multi-step work, and no reasoning at all for simple recall and formatting. The generation-verification gap is the precondition that makes any selection method worthwhile. How much to spend is the effort dial — parallel, sequential, or search — allocated adaptively by difficulty, not uniformly. The RL lineage from STaR through GRPO to R1 is what built today's models, with reward hacking and entropy collapse as the failure modes.
