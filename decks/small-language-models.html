Small Language Models & Edge AI

Why small often wins — cost, latency, privacy, offline — and how a 1–3B model gets strong enough to ship on a phone: distillation, quantization, pruning, efficient architectures, great data, and on-device runtimes.

←→ navigate · S speaker notes · O overview · F fullscreen · append ?print-pdf to export

Welcome. The headlines chase the biggest frontier models, but a huge slice of real products runs on the small end — models that fit on a phone, a laptop, or a cheap VM. This module is about why you'd deliberately choose a 1–3B model over a frontier API, and how those small models got good enough to be worth choosing. The single sentence the whole lecture turns on: on-device decode is memory-bandwidth bound, so quantization isn't optional — it's the price of admission. We'll walk the shrink pipeline (distill → quantize → prune → package), do the phone memory-budget arithmetic, meet the strong SLM families, and end on the cascade pattern: let a small model handle most traffic and escalate only the hard cases.

Core ideas to land

Five things to take away

  • Quality of data beats quantity — for small models, clean dense tokens matter more than raw volume.
  • Quantization is non-negotiable on-device — FP16 weights rarely fit; INT4 is the default.
  • On-device decode is memory-bandwidth bound — you wait on reading weights, not on FLOPs.
  • Structured pruning is what actually speeds up commodity HW — unstructured zeros don't.
  • Cascade: small handles most, escalate the hard cases — route, don't pay frontier prices on every call.
This is the executive summary — I come back to it as the recap. Watch how they chain. Why it matters

Five reasons to choose small

Privacydata never leaves device · Offlineworks on a plane / clinic · Latencyno network round-trip · Costno per-token API bill · Controlown the weights & updates
  • Privacy: on-device inference means raw user data never leaves the phone — nothing to leak, subpoena, or breach.
  • Offline & latency: a flight-mode translator or a clinical scribe in a no-signal hospital can't depend on a cloud call.
  • Cost & control: no per-token bill at scale, and a regulated org owns its weights, data residency, and update cadence.
Spell out the business case, because "small is cheaper to train" is the least interesting reason. Privacy is the big one: if inference runs on-device, the user's photos, messages, or health data never touch a third party — that turns a compliance nightmare into a non-event. Offline is a hard requirement, not a nice-to-have, for two categories worth naming: anything that must work without connectivity (in-flight, remote field work, a basement) and anything in a regulated, air-gapped setting (a hospital, a defense network). Latency follows: skipping the network round-trip can make a small local model feel faster than a big cloud one. Cost flips from per-token opex to a one-time download. And control — owning the weights, the data residency story, and the update schedule — is exactly what GDPR/HIPAA-bound industries need. We unpack the latency claim on the next slide because it's the counterintuitive one.

Why it matters · the latency surprise

Lower tok/s can still mean lower end-to-end latency

  • But: network RTT + queueing + TLS + cold starts
  • Tail latency spikes under load
On-device SLM
  • Lower raw tok/s on a phone NPU/GPU
  • But: zero network — no RTT, no queue

Users feel time-to-first-token and tail latency, not datacenter throughput. Local often wins the metric that matters.

This is the slide interviewers love. The trap is comparing the wrong number: a datacenter GPU posts way more tokens-per-second than a phone, so people assume the cloud is always faster end-to-end. But end-to-end latency for an interactive feature is dominated by time-to-first-token and the tail, not steady-state throughput. The cloud path pays a fixed tax every request — DNS, TLS handshake, network round-trip, server-side queueing, occasional cold starts — and that tax balloons under load right when you care most. The on-device model has none of that: the moment the user hits enter, it's already decoding. For a short reply, near-instant first token plus a modest local tok/s can beat a faster-but-remote model. The lesson: optimize and report the metric the user feels, which is usually TTFT and p99, not the headline tok/s.

Intuition

Capability per parameter, not raw parameters

  • Old mental model: more parameters = more capability. Roughly true at fixed training quality.
  • But two 3B models can be worlds apart — the training recipe is the hidden variable.
  • A small model trained on dense, clean, well-curated data learns more per parameter (the Phi "textbooks" bet).
  • Distillation lets a small student inherit a big teacher's behavior without the teacher's size.
  • So "smaller but smarter" is real — today's 3B rivals last generation's 7B because the data and recipe improved.
The reframe to plant early: stop thinking in raw parameter count and start thinking capability-per-parameter. Parameters are capacity; the training recipe decides how much of that capacity becomes useful skill. Two models of identical size can differ enormously depending on what they were fed and how they were trained — this is exactly why SLMs improved far faster than parameter count alone would predict over the last few generations. Three levers do the work: better data (Phi's textbook-quality synthetic corpus packs more signal into every token, and small models are more sensitive to data quality because they have less capacity to waste), distillation (a small student trained on a large teacher's outputs beats the same small model trained from scratch on raw web text), and architecture/efficiency tricks we'll cover later. Net: a well-trained 3B today can match a 7B from a generation ago. That's the whole optimism of this module.

Intuition · the budget

Everything shares ~8GB on a phone

The model isn't alone — weights, KV cache, activations, and the OS all draw from one small pool.

OS + app~3–4 GB, always + Weightsquantized, the big rock + KV cachegrows with context + Activationstransient, per token = ≤ ~8 GB totalor the OS kills you
  • The OS reserves a big chunk and will kill your app if the system runs low — you don't get all 8GB.
  • Weights are the fixed cost; the KV cache is the sneaky one — it grows linearly with context length.
This is the constraint that governs every on-device decision, so make it physical. A typical phone has ~8GB of RAM, and you share it with everyone: the OS and other apps hold ~3–4GB at all times, and the OS aggressively kills background — and even foreground — apps under memory pressure. So your real budget is more like 4–5GB. Into that go three things: the weights (fixed, and the single biggest rock — this is why quantization is non-negotiable), the KV cache (grows linearly with context length and model size, and on long chats it can rival the weights themselves), and activations (transient working memory per forward pass). We'll do the actual arithmetic in the next section, but the intuition to lock in now is: you are not memory-rich, the OS is your landlord, and the KV cache is the line item people forget until their app gets killed mid-conversation.

How it works · the shrink pipeline

From frontier teacher to phone — one pass

The standard recipe to ship a capable small model: borrow skill, then shrink, then package.

Drive this off the widget — it's the spine of the whole "how" section. Step 1: pick a strong teacher; you want a model whose capability you can transfer. Step 2: distillation — the student trains on the teacher's generated answers (and ideally its soft logits), which is far richer supervision than raw web text, so the small student punches above its weight. Step 3: quantization — convert FP16 weights to INT4/NF4 for roughly a 4× memory cut; this is the step that actually makes it fit on a phone. Step 4: pruning — remove redundant structure; emphasize structured pruning (whole heads/channels/layers) because that's what dense hardware can exploit. Step 5: package into a runtime format — GGUF for llama.cpp, Core ML or MLX for Apple. Step 6: ship it, and now you have the privacy/offline/latency wins from section three. Order matters loosely: distill while in full precision, then quantize/prune, then package — you don't quantize first and then try to distill into 4-bit weights.

How it works · the memory budget

Can a 3B INT4 model live in 8GB? The arithmetic

Line itemEstimateWhy

Weights (3B, INT4)~1.5–1.8 GB3e9 params × ~0.5 bytes (4-bit) + overhead

FP16 weights alone would be ~6GB — over budget before the OS even loads. INT4 is what makes it fit. Long contexts can blow it up via the KV cache.

Now the concrete arithmetic that section four set up. Start from the top: the OS and other apps hold ~3–4GB you'll never get. Weights for a 3B model at INT4 are about 3 billion params times half a byte each, so ~1.5GB plus quantization scales and metadata — call it 1.5–1.8GB. Compare that to FP16, which would be 3e9 times 2 bytes = ~6GB — that alone busts the budget before the OS is even in the picture, which is the whole reason quantization is mandatory. The KV cache at a few thousand tokens of context is a few hundred MB, but it scales linearly, so a 32K-context chat can make it the largest single consumer — this is the line item that silently kills apps. Activations are a few hundred MB of transient working memory. Add it up and a 3B INT4 model fits in ~4–6GB with real headroom on an 8GB phone — but that headroom is thin and the KV cache is what eats it, so on-device you quantize the cache, use windowed attention, and cap context. The takeaway: weights set the floor, the KV cache sets the ceiling.

Approaches · 1 of 5

Knowledge distillation classic

What: a small student learns to mimic a large teacher instead of learning raw data from scratch (Hinton et al., 2015). How: run the teacher on data; train the student to match its outputs — hard labels, intermediate features, or soft logits. Solves: the teacher's soft outputs carry "dark knowledge" (which wrong answers are plausible), a far richer signal than one-hot labels.

Pros
  • Student can beat a same-size model trained from scratch
  • Soft targets are a smoother, denser learning signal
Cons
  • Student is largely capped near the teacher; inherits its biases
  • Needs teacher inference over the whole training set — not free

vs baseline: training a small model directly on hard labels wastes the teacher's uncertainty; distillation transfers how the teacher thinks, not just the right answer.

Read more ↗

Start here because distillation is the oldest and most general "make small strong" lever. The key intuition is dark knowledge: a teacher that says "70% cat, 25% dog, 5% car" tells the student that cats and dogs are similar and cars are not — structure a one-hot label throws away. Training on those soft targets gives the student a smoother loss surface and lets a small student reach accuracy a same-size from-scratch model cannot. The catch is the ceiling: the student rarely exceeds the teacher and copies its mistakes and biases. And you pay teacher inference cost across the corpus. This sets up the three flavors on the next slide — response, feature, and logit distillation.

How it works · Knowledge distillation

Distillation — step by step

Teacherlarge model → Run on dataget outputs → Pick signallabel / feat / logit → Match lossstudent copies → Studentsmall, strong
  1. Run the frozen teacher over your data, capturing its outputs.
  2. Response (hard-label): use the teacher's top answer as a pseudo-label — simplest, cheapest.
  3. Feature: also match the teacher's intermediate hidden states — richer, needs aligned layers.
  4. Logit (soft-label): match the full probability distribution at temperature T — the "dark knowledge."
  5. Train the student to minimize that match loss → small model, teacher-like behavior.

Soften with temperature: p_i = softmax(z_i / T) — high T spreads probability mass so similar-class info survives.

Walk the three flavors as a ladder of richness. Response-based (hard-label) distillation just trains the student on the teacher's predicted answers — this is exactly what Alpaca-style instruction distillation and reasoning-trace distillation do, and it is the easiest to run across modalities. Feature-based distillation additionally forces the student's hidden layers to resemble the teacher's — more signal but you must align layer widths and pick which layers to match. Logit (soft-label) distillation, the original Hinton recipe, matches the full softmax distribution, softened by a temperature T so the small probabilities on plausible wrong classes are visible. In practice for LLMs, response-level distillation on generated text dominates because you rarely have matching teacher logits, but the soft-label idea is what makes the whole thing work.

Approaches · 2 of 5

Pruning classic

What: delete weights the model barely uses, then fine-tune to recover. How: score importance (magnitude, gradient) → remove the lowest → heal. Two regimes: structured (drop whole heads / channels / layers) vs unstructured (zero out individual weights). Solves: shrinks a trained model without retraining from scratch.

Pros
  • Structured pruning actually speeds up commodity HW — smaller dense matmuls
  • Lottery-ticket intuition: a small subnetwork can match the full model
Cons
  • Unstructured gives sparse weights — needs special kernels to see any speedup
  • Aggressive pruning hurts accuracy; recovery fine-tune is required

vs previous: distillation builds a new small model; pruning carves the small model out of the big one's existing weights.

Read more ↗

The headline interview point: structured vs unstructured. Unstructured pruning zeroes individual weights and can reach very high sparsity with little accuracy loss — but a sparse matrix with scattered zeros still runs as a dense matmul on a normal GPU or phone, so you save memory only if you have special sparse kernels (and most commodity hardware does not exploit it). Structured pruning removes whole units — attention heads, FFN channels, even entire layers — leaving a smaller dense network that every framework runs faster out of the box. That is why structured pruning is what people reach for when they want real on-device latency wins. The lottery-ticket hypothesis is the theoretical backbone: a randomly-initialized dense net contains a sparse subnetwork ("winning ticket") that, trained in isolation, matches the full model — evidence that most parameters are redundant and aggressive pruning can be near-lossless.

How it works · Pruning

Pruning — step by step

Trained modeldense → Scoreimportance → Removeheads/chan/layers → Fine-tuneheal ↩ Smaller netiterate
  1. Start from a fully trained dense model.
  2. Score each unit's importance — magnitude, gradient, or activation contribution.
  3. Structured: drop whole heads / channels / layers → smaller dense model, faster on any HW.
  4. Unstructured: zero individual low-magnitude weights → sparse, needs sparse kernels.
  5. Fine-tune to recover lost accuracy; optionally repeat (iterative pruning).

Lottery ticket: the surviving subnetwork, re-trained from its original init, can match the full model — the structure was the "winning ticket."

The loop is score → remove → heal, and doing it gradually (iterative magnitude pruning) beats one big cut because each round lets the network redistribute load before you cut again. Emphasize the recovery fine-tune: pruning always damages the model, and a short fine-tune restores most of it — skip it and accuracy craters. Tie the lottery-ticket idea back to whether near-lossless pruning is possible: Frankle & Carbin showed that the sparse winning ticket, rewound to its original initialization and trained alone, matches the dense model, which is why we believe big models are massively over-parameterized and prunable. For SLMs the practical move is structured pruning followed by distillation fine-tuning — that is exactly the recipe behind models like NVIDIA's Minitron and the pruned Llama variants.

Approaches · 3 of 5

Quantization for edge core

What: store weights (and maybe activations) in low-bit formats instead of FP16. How: map a float range onto an integer grid via a scale; INT8, INT4, or NF4. Solves: the single biggest lever for fitting a model on a phone — INT4 is ¼ the size of FP16, dropping a 7B model from ~14 GB to ~3.5 GB.

Pros
  • Huge memory cut; faster bandwidth-bound decode
  • Weight-only INT4 is near-lossless on perplexity
Cons
  • Outlier activations wreck a naive shared scale
  • Below ~4-bit, quality drops fast without special tricks

vs previous: pruning removes parameters; quantization keeps every parameter but stores each in fewer bits — orthogonal, and routinely stacked on top of pruning.

Read more ↗ (QLoRA / NF4)

Quantization is the on-device lever — memory is the binding constraint on a phone, and quartering the weights is the most direct fix. Lay out the two axes the next slides assume. What you quantize: weight-only (compress storage, dequantize to compute — where INT4 GPTQ/AWQ live) vs weight-and-activation (quantize both so the matmul runs in INT8 — real compute speedups). When you quantize: PTQ (post-training, calibrate on a small set, cheap) vs QAT (simulate rounding during training, expensive, recovers accuracy at very low bits). The villain to flag now is outlier features: a few activation channels carry values orders of magnitude larger than the rest, and one shared scale either clips them or wastes precision — that motivates AWQ on the next slide.

How it works · Quantization

Quantization — step by step

FP16 weightsfull precision → Calibratefind ranges → Pick formatINT8/INT4/NF4 → Roundto grid → Pack GGUFllama.cpp
  1. INT8 — 8-bit ints, ~2× smaller, almost always safe.
  2. INT4 — 4-bit, ~4× smaller; the sweet spot for edge with per-group scales.
  3. NF4 — "normal float 4", a non-uniform grid that assumes weights are ~normally distributed → spends precision where the mass is.
  4. PTQ vs QAT: calibrate-and-round (cheap) vs train-with-fake-quant (expensive, more accurate).
  5. Pack into GGUF — the single-file format llama.cpp mmaps and runs on CPU/Metal.

Linear map: q = round(w / scale), recover w ≈ q × scale. NF4 swaps the uniform grid for a normal-shaped one.

The mechanism is a linear map — divide by a scale, round, store the int — but the format choices matter for edge. INT8 is the safe default; INT4 with per-group scales (one scale per 32–128 weights) is the practical edge sweet spot. NF4, from the QLoRA paper, is the clever one: instead of evenly-spaced levels it uses a grid derived from the normal distribution, because neural-net weights cluster near zero and are roughly Gaussian — so NF4 places more representable values where the weights actually are, beating plain INT4 at the same bit-width. GGUF is the ecosystem glue: a self-contained file (weights plus metadata, plus the quant scheme) that llama.cpp memory-maps so the model loads fast and shares RAM — it is why local LLMs took off. PTQ is what you run 99% of the time; QAT is reserved for pushing below 4 bits.

How it works · pushing the frontier

Protecting precision & going extreme-low-bit

Naive 4-bituniform scale → AWQprotect salient → 3-bitK-quants → 2-bitedge of usable → 1.58-bitBitNet
  1. Naive low-bit treats all weights equally → quietly destroys the few that matter most.
  2. AWQ (activation-aware): the ~1% of weights tied to large activations are salient — scale them up before rounding so they survive 4-bit.
  3. Per-group K-quants push reliably to 3-bit; 2-bit is the ragged edge of usability.
  4. 1.58-bit (BitNet): weights restricted to {-1, 0, +1} — trained that way from scratch, not post-quantized.
  5. Below 4-bit, you need QAT or native low-bit training to hold quality.

1.58 = log₂(3): three values per weight. BitNet replaces multiplies with adds → tiny, fast, edge-friendly.

This is the research-frontier slide. AWQ's insight is that not all weights are equal: a small fraction (~1%) connect to the outlier activation channels and dominate the output, so quantizing them carelessly causes most of the damage. AWQ identifies those salient weights by looking at activation magnitudes and applies a per-channel scaling that protects them before rounding — that is what makes 4-bit near-lossless without mixed precision. Going lower: GGUF K-quants reach 3-bit reliably and 2-bit is where quality gets shaky for general tasks. BitNet b1.58 is the extreme — ternary weights {-1, 0, +1}, about 1.58 bits each, but the trick is it is trained in low precision from the start, so the network learns to live there; this turns matrix multiplies into additions, which is enormous for power-constrained edge silicon. The general rule: post-training quant is fine to ~4 bits; below that you must train for it.

Approaches · 4 of 5

Efficient architecture modern

What: design the small model so each parameter and each token of context costs less. How: share KV heads (GQA), cap attention range (sliding-window), reuse the embedding matrix (tied embeddings), choose depth vs width, use SwiGLU FFNs. Solves: buys capability and on-device efficiency for free at the architecture level — before any compression.

Pros
  • Smaller KV cache & faster decode with little quality loss
  • Tied embeddings save real parameters at small scale
Cons
  • Sliding-window can hurt true long-range recall
  • Depth-vs-width is a tuning trade-off, not a free win everywhere

vs previous: distillation, pruning, and quantization shrink an existing design; efficient architecture changes the design itself so it is born cheap.

Read more ↗ (GQA)

This rung is about building the small model right from the start rather than compressing a wasteful one. GQA (grouped-query attention) is the headline: instead of every query head having its own K/V head (expensive cache) or all sharing one (MQA, slight quality loss), groups of query heads share a K/V head — most of the cache savings, almost none of the quality cost, which is why nearly every recent model uses it. Sliding-window attention caps how far back each token attends, making attention and the cache cost constant in window size rather than growing with context — great for memory, but it can lose genuine long-range dependencies. Tied embeddings reuse the input embedding matrix as the output projection: at small scale the vocab embedding is a big fraction of the parameters, so tying them is a meaningful saving. Depth-vs-width and SwiGLU round it out — the next slide makes each concrete.

How it works · Efficient architecture

The cheap-capability levers — step by step

GQAshare KV heads → Sliding windowcap range → Tied embedreuse matrix → Depth vs widthshape → SwiGLUbetter FFN
  1. GQA: many query heads share a few K/V heads → shrinks the KV cache, the binding edge constraint.
  2. Sliding-window attention: each token attends only to the last W tokens → cache stops growing with context.
  3. Tied embeddings: input embedding = output projection → saves a vocab×dim matrix (big at small scale).
  4. Depth vs width: for a fixed budget, deeper often reasons better; wider runs faster — tune to the target.
  5. SwiGLU: a gated FFN that gives more capability per FLOP than plain ReLU/GELU.

All five are quality-per-parameter or quality-per-byte wins — they stack with distillation, pruning, and quantization.

Make each lever concrete. GQA and sliding-window both target the KV cache, which on a phone is often the real memory wall, not the weights. GQA is the more general win because it loses almost no quality; sliding-window is more aggressive and is why models like Mistral can hold long context cheaply, but you trade away some true long-range recall — production models often interleave windowed and full-attention layers to hedge. Tied embeddings matter disproportionately for SLMs: with a 32K-ish vocab and a small hidden size, the embedding table can be 20–40% of a tiny model's parameters, so sharing input and output projections is real savings. Depth-vs-width is the eternal tuning question — deeper helps multi-step reasoning, wider helps throughput. SwiGLU is the standard modern FFN because it consistently beats plain activations at equal compute. The punchline: architecture choices compound with every compression technique we covered.

Approaches · 5 of 5

Data quality — "textbooks" modern

What: train the small model on dense, clean, pedagogical data instead of raw web scrape (Gunasekar et al., 2023, the Phi line). How: curate or synthesize "textbook-quality" content plus exercises; order it as a curriculum (easy → hard). Solves: quality disproportionately helps small models — limited capacity is best spent on high-signal data.

Pros
  • Tiny models punch far above their weight (Phi)
  • Dense, low-noise signal; less wasted capacity
Cons

  • Heavy synthetic data → distribution narrowing / collapse risk

Read more ↗ (Textbooks Are All You Need)

The final rung is data-centric, and it is why modern SLMs are so good. The Phi line's bet — "Textbooks Are All You Need" — is that a small model with limited capacity cannot afford to waste it modeling the noise, boilerplate, and contradictions of raw web text; feed it dense, clean, pedagogical content and it learns the actual skill far more efficiently. This is exactly why quality helps small models disproportionately: a frontier model has enough capacity to absorb noisy data and still extract the signal, but a 1–3B model does not. Honest caveats: curated/synthetic textbook data can be narrow, it is hard to audit for benchmark contamination (the teacher may have seen the test set), and leaning too hard on a single teacher's synthetic output risks distribution narrowing — the model-collapse failure mode. The next slide makes the curriculum mechanism concrete.

How it works · Data quality / curriculum

"Textbooks" & curriculum — step by step

Topic spec+ persona → Synthesizetextbook prose → Filterkeep dense/clean → Curriculumeasy → hard → Train SLMpunches up
  1. Specify topics (and personas) to steer broad, deliberate coverage.
  2. Synthesize clean, pedagogical "textbook" prose plus worked exercises.
  3. Filter hard for density & clarity — keep only high-signal documents.
  4. Curriculum: order the data easy → hard so the small model builds skills in sequence.
  5. Train the SLM → coding/reasoning ability far above its parameter count.

Curriculum example: teach arithmetic & syntax before multi-step word problems — foundations first, like a real course.

The mechanism is "manufacture a curriculum." Topic and persona steering gives deliberate coverage instead of whatever the web happened to contain; the textbook-plus-exercise format packs structured reasoning and worked solutions into nearly every token, which is the high-information-density signal the Phi hypothesis bets on. The filter is what makes it quality-first — only the cleanest, densest docs survive. Curriculum learning is the ordering layer: just like a human course teaches arithmetic and grammar before multi-step word problems, presenting easy, foundational examples first and ramping difficulty can help a small model converge to better solutions than shuffling everything together — it builds prerequisite skills before they are needed. Tie it back to the collapse caveat: this only works if the synthetic data stays diverse and you keep some real data in the mix, or the small model overfits the teacher's narrow style.

Approaches · 7 · small MoE

Small Mixture-of-Experts capacity vs cost

What: many expert FFNs, but a router fires only a few per token. How: a gate picks top-k experts (often 2 of 8); a token's compute touches only those, so active params ≪ total params. Solves: buys big-model capacity at small-model per-token FLOPs — great when you're compute-bound but not memory-bound.

Pros
  • Big knowledge capacity, small active compute per token
  • Faster decode than a dense model of equal total size
Cons
  • All experts must sit in memory — total params set the VRAM/RAM bill
  • Routing imbalance, harder to quantize, awkward on tight-RAM phones

vs previous: dense families spend every parameter on every token; MoE decouples capacity (total params) from cost (active params) — but moves the new bottleneck to memory.

The one-sentence framing: MoE separates capacity from per-token cost. A dense 7B runs all 7B parameters for every token. An MoE might hold, say, 8 expert FFNs but route each token through only 2 of them, so it has the knowledge capacity of a much larger model while the compute per token stays small — that's why decode is fast. The catch, and the new cost, is the headline for edge: routing is dynamic and unpredictable, so you cannot know in advance which experts a token needs — therefore every expert must be resident in memory. So MoE saves FLOPs (and thus latency and energy) but not memory; on a phone where RAM is the binding constraint, a small MoE can be the wrong choice precisely because the total parameter count, not the active count, sets the footprint. Mention the secondary headaches: load-balancing the router so experts get used evenly, and that experts quantize less gracefully than a dense stack.

How it works · small MoE

Small MoE — step by step

Tokenhidden state → Routergate scores → Top-k expertse.g. 2 of 8 → Combineweighted sum → Next layerfew active FLOPs
  1. A token's hidden state arrives at the MoE layer.
  2. A small router scores all experts and picks the top-k (e.g. 2 of 8).
  3. Only those k expert FFNs run — active FLOPs stay small.
  4. Their outputs are combined by the gate weights.
  5. All 8 experts still had to be loaded in memory — that's the cost.

active params set latency/energy · total params set the memory bill — on-device, memory usually wins.

Walk the routing and then nail the cost asymmetry. The router is a tiny learned layer that produces a score per expert; you keep the top-k, run only those expert FFNs, and blend their outputs weighted by the gate. Because only k of n experts fire, the FLOPs per token look like a small dense model — fast decode, low energy. But step five is the teaching point for edge: routing is data-dependent, so you can't pre-load just the experts you'll need; all of them must be resident. End on the equation line: active params govern latency and energy, total params govern memory, and on-device the memory term is almost always the binding one. That single contrast is exactly the trade-off an interviewer is fishing for when they ask "when is MoE worth it on edge?"

Approaches · 8 · speculative decoding

Speculative decoding latency

What: a cheap SLM drafts several tokens; the big target verifies them in one parallel pass. How: draft proposes γ tokens; target scores all of them at once; a rejection-sampling check accepts the longest correct prefix and corrects the first wrong one. Solves: decode latency — you get multiple tokens per expensive target step with the target's exact distribution.

Pros
  • Output is distributionally identical to the target — zero quality loss
  • 2–3× fewer target forward passes when draft agrees
Cons
  • No win if the draft is too weak (everything rejected) or too slow
  • Extra memory for two models; tuning γ & draft size matters

vs previous: MoE cuts FLOPs per token; speculative decoding produces more tokens per target pass — an orthogonal latency lever, losslessly.

Speculative decoding ↗

The headline that makes this magical: it is provably lossless. The draft (a small fast model) proposes a run of gamma tokens cheaply; the big target model then verifies all gamma in a single parallel forward pass — cheap because verifying tokens you already have is one batched pass, not gamma sequential ones. A rejection-sampling acceptance test, using both models' probabilities, accepts the longest correct prefix and, on the first disagreement, samples a correction from an adjusted distribution. The math guarantees the final stream is distributed exactly as if the target had generated it alone — no quality trade. The speedup comes entirely from the draft being right often: when it agrees you bought several tokens for the price of one target step. It dies at the extremes — a draft too weak gets rejected constantly (wasted work), a draft too large isn't cheap enough to be worth running ahead. Next slide animates the loop.

How it works · speculative decoding

Draft → verify → accept — the loop

Tune two knobs: draft size (fast enough to run ahead, good enough to be accepted) and γ = tokens per round (too many → deep rejections waste the draft's work).

Drive the loop off the widget. Step one, the draft sprints ahead and proposes gamma tokens autoregressively — it's small so this is cheap. Step two, the target ingests the prompt plus all gamma proposed tokens in a single batched pass and produces a probability for each position; this is the expensive step but you do it once for many tokens. Step three, the acceptance test walks the proposals left to right and keeps the longest run the target would plausibly have produced — those tokens cost essentially nothing. Step four, at the first rejection you resample exactly one token from a corrected distribution, which is what makes the whole scheme distributionally exact rather than approximate. Step five, you've advanced several tokens for one target forward pass, then repeat. The meter is the acceptance rate — the lever that decides your speedup. Then the two knobs: the draft must be both fast enough to outrun the target and accurate enough to be accepted (a good draft is usually 10–20× smaller, same tokenizer/family), and gamma trades reach against waste — propose too far and a single early mismatch throws away the tail you speculated.

Approaches · 10 · edge constraints

Edge constraints systems

What: the four physical limits that decide whether on-device inference is viable. How: memory, battery, thermal throttling, and intermittent network each impose a design consequence. Solves: nothing by itself — this is the reality you engineer around, and the KV cache is the one that bites first.

Memory & thermal
  • KV cache grows with context × batch — the binding on-device limit, often beating the weights
  • Sustained decode heats the SoC → thermal throttling slows you mid-conversation
Battery & network
  • Every token costs energy → report energy-per-token, not just tok/s
  • Intermittent/no network → design offline-first, sync later

Decode is memory-bandwidth bound — you stream every weight per token — so a phone's headline NPU TOPS ≠ throughput. Bandwidth, not FLOPs, sets your speed.

vs previous: runtimes assumed the hardware cooperates; this is where the hardware fights back.

The four constraints, with a design consequence for each. Memory: model weights are fixed, but the KV cache grows linearly with context length and batch, and on a long chat it can rival or exceed the weights — it is usually the binding on-device memory limit, which is why quantized or windowed caches matter so much. Thermal: a phone has no fan; sustained generation heats the SoC until the OS throttles clocks, so a chat that starts fast slows after a minute — design for bursts, not marathons, and consider letting it cool between turns. Battery: each token costs joules, so the honest metric is energy-per-token alongside tokens-per-second and time-to-first-token. Network: assume connectivity drops — build offline-first and reconcile when it returns. Then the systems punchline they always probe: on-device decode is memory-bandwidth bound, because producing one token means streaming the entire model out of memory while the compute units sit idle. So a marketing NPU TOPS number describes peak FLOPs, not the memory bandwidth that actually gates decode — an NPU can advertise huge TOPS yet deliver worse real LLM throughput than the same phone's GPU. Select hardware on bandwidth, not TOPS.

Approaches · 11 · RAG + small

RAG + small model deployment

What: retrieve relevant text and feed it to the SLM so it reasons over facts instead of recalling them. How: embed the query → search a vector index → stuff the top-k passages into the prompt → the small model answers from that context. Solves: a small model's biggest weakness — thin world knowledge — by moving facts from weights into the context window.

Pros
  • Fresh, private, citable facts without retraining
  • A 1–3B model punches far above its weight on closed-book gaps
Cons
  • Can't fix weak reasoning — only missing knowledge
  • Bad retrieval → confident wrong answers; long context strains small models

vs previous: the constraints slide capped what the model can hold; RAG sidesteps that by keeping knowledge outside the weights, in a retrievable index.

RAG is the single highest-leverage trick for making a small model useful, because it attacks the right weakness. Scaling a model down costs world knowledge first — a 1.5B simply hasn't memorized much — but it can still read. So instead of asking it to recall, you retrieve the relevant passages and let it reason over them in-context; facts live in a vector index you can update without retraining, which also gives you freshness, privacy, and citations. That's why a tiny model plus good retrieval often beats a much larger closed-book model on knowledge tasks. But be honest about the ceiling: RAG supplies knowledge, it does not supply reasoning — if the question needs multi-hop synthesis the small model still can't do, perfect retrieval won't save it. And it has its own failure modes: retrieve the wrong passages and the model answers confidently from bad context, and stuffing many long passages strains a small model's limited effective context. So RAG closes the knowledge gap, not the reasoning gap.

How it works · RAG + small

Queryuser question → Embedto vector → Retrievetop-k passages → Stuff context+ prompt → SLM answersgrounded
  1. Embed the query into a vector with a small embedding model.
  2. Search the index for the top-k most similar passages.
  3. Stuff those passages into the prompt as grounding context.
  4. The SLM answers from the context — reasoning over facts, not recalling them.
  5. Ideal for on-device: a tiny index + small model = private, offline knowledge.
The pipeline is the standard query → embed → retrieve → stuff → generate, but the on-device angle is what makes it shine here. The embedding model can be small and the index can live entirely on the device, so you get a private, offline knowledge assistant with no API calls. Two things to emphasize: first, the small model is now doing reading comprehension over supplied text rather than closed-book recall, which is a far easier job for limited parameters — that's the whole point. Second, retrieval quality becomes the bottleneck; garbage passages in mean a confident wrong answer out, so the index and the retriever matter as much as the model. And remind them of the ceiling from the previous slide — RAG hands the model facts, but the multi-hop reasoning over those facts is still bounded by the small model's capability.

Approaches · 12 · cascade routing

Cascade / hybrid routing deployment SOTA

What: the SLM handles the easy majority of traffic; only hard cases escalate to a frontier model. How: a router (confidence score, complexity classifier, or a quick self-check) decides per request whether the small model's answer is good enough or must be sent up. Solves: the cost/quality dilemma — most queries get cheap on-device answers, the few hard ones still get frontier quality.

Pros
  • Big cost & latency win — most traffic never leaves the device
  • Frontier quality preserved on the cases that need it
Cons
  • Router errors: over-escalate (cost) or under-escalate (bad answers ship)
  • Two systems to maintain; escalation needs a network round-trip

vs previous: RAG made one small model smarter; cascading admits the small model has limits and routes around them — the production architecture that ties part B together.

This is the architecture that makes SLMs economically irresistible, and the one interviewers love to dig into. The premise: in most real workloads the bulk of queries are easy — greetings, simple lookups, formatting — and a well-tuned small model answers them perfectly for almost nothing, on-device, with zero latency to the cloud. The trick is knowing which queries are not easy. That's the router's job, and it's the whole ballgame: it can use the model's own confidence (token log-probs or a self-consistency check), a learned complexity classifier on the query, or a verifier on the draft answer. Easy → ship the SLM's answer; hard → escalate to a frontier API. The two failure modes are symmetric and both expensive in their own way: over-escalation sends easy traffic to the costly model and burns your savings; under-escalation ships a wrong small-model answer to the user. Calibrating that threshold — usually against a labeled set with a cost/quality target — is the real engineering. Next slide animates the path.

How it works · cascade routing

Query → SLM → route → answer — the loop

The router threshold sets the trade: too eager to escalate → cost climbs; too reluctant → quality drops. Calibrate it on labeled traffic.

Walk the path off the widget. A query arrives; the on-device SLM answers it first — that's the fast, cheap, private default. Then the router evaluates that answer or the query: it can read the model's confidence (low max-probability or high entropy signals "unsure"), run a lightweight complexity classifier, or do a quick self-verification. If the signal says easy and confident, you ship the small model's answer and you never touched the network. If it says hard or low-confidence, you escalate to a frontier model over an API and return that instead. The meter is the escalation rate — the dial that sets your bill. Close on calibration, because it's the crux: the threshold is a business decision encoded as a number, tuned on labeled traffic against a target like "keep 90% on-device while holding accuracy above X." Get it right and you capture most of the cost savings with almost none of the quality loss; that balance is exactly why cascade routing is the deployment SOTA for SLMs and the natural capstone for this part.

The math · (a) weight memory

How much RAM do the weights need?

Weights-only footprint is just parameter count times bytes per parameter:

$$ M \approx N_{params} \times b\ \text{bytes} $$

\(N_{params}\) = parameter count (7B \(= 7 \times 10^9\)); \(b\) = bytes per stored weight.

FP16 stores each weight in 16 bits \(=\) 2 bytes. So a 7B model: \(7 \times 10^9 \times 2 \approx 14\) GB.

Each lower precision just changes \(b\): INT8 \(= 1\) byte, INT4 \(= 0.5\) byte.

PrecisionBytes / param7B weightsvs FP16
FP16 / BF162≈ 14 GB1×

INT4 / NF40.5≈ 3.5 GB¼

Rule of thumb interviewers love: FP16 GB ≈ 2 × params-in-billions.

Start with the one formula every on-device interview asks: memory is parameter count times bytes per parameter. The only subtlety is bytes-per-param, and it is set entirely by the precision you store the weights in. FP16 and BF16 are 16-bit, so two bytes — that is where the "double the billions" rule of thumb comes from: a 7B model is roughly 14 GB just for weights. Halve the bits to INT8 and you halve the memory to about 7 GB; quarter the bits to INT4 or NF4 and you are at about 3.5 GB. Note two real-world caveats so nobody is surprised on the device: group-wise quant stores a small scale per block of 32–128 weights, which adds a few percent overhead, and this number is weights only — the KV cache, activations, and the OS still have to fit alongside it, which is exactly the phone-fit check at the end of this section.

The math · (b) the distillation loss

What the student actually trains on

Two signals, blended: imitate the teacher's soft probabilities and get the hard label right.

$$ \mathcal{L} = \alpha\, T^{2}\, \mathrm{KL}\!\big(p_T^{\tau} \,\Vert\, p_S^{\tau}\big) \;+\; (1-\alpha)\, \mathrm{CE}\big(y,\, p_S\big) $$

\(p_S, p_T\) = student / teacher softmax outputs; \(y\) = the true hard label.

\(\tau\) = temperature — softens both distributions: \(p^{\tau} = \mathrm{softmax}(z/\tau)\). High \(\tau\) exposes the teacher's "dark knowledge" (cat \(\approx\) 0.2, dog \(\approx\) 0.1, car \(\approx\) 0.001).

KL term = match the teacher's full probability shape, not just its top pick.

CE term = standard cross-entropy on the ground-truth label, so the student stays anchored to truth, not just to the teacher.

The \(T^{2}\) factor rescales the soft gradient (it shrinks by \(1/T^{2}\) when you soften), keeping the two terms balanced; \(\alpha\) trades soft vs hard.

This is the loss to be able to write on a whiteboard. The whole idea of distillation is that the teacher's soft probabilities carry more information than a one-hot label: when the teacher says cat 0.7, dog 0.2, car 0.001, it is telling the student that cats and dogs are similar and neither is a car — Hinton called that "dark knowledge." Temperature tau is what reveals it: dividing logits by tau before softmax flattens the distribution so those small runner-up probabilities become learnable signal; at tau equals one you are back to the raw distribution. The KL term pushes the student's softened distribution toward the teacher's softened distribution — matching the whole shape. The cross-entropy term keeps the student honest against the real label, which matters because the teacher is sometimes wrong. Two gotchas: you apply the same tau to teacher and student, and you multiply the soft term by T-squared because softening scales the gradients down by one over T-squared, so without it the hard label would dominate. Alpha is the mixing knob, often around 0.5 to 0.9 toward the soft term.

Does a 3B INT4 model fit an 8 GB phone?

Add up everything that must live in RAM at once, not just the weights:

  • Weights: \(3 \times 10^9 \times 0.5\) byte \(\approx 1.5\) GB, plus group scales \(\to \approx\) 1.5–1.8 GB.
  • KV cache: grows with context. For a GQA 3B at a few-thousand-token context \(\approx\) 0.3–0.8 GB (and it climbs with every token).
  • Activations / runtime scratch: intermediate tensors + framework buffers \(\approx\) 0.3–0.5 GB.
  • OS + other apps: the system already reserves 3–4 GB on an 8 GB phone — you do not get all 8.

Total \(\approx 1.8 + 0.6 + 0.4 + 3.5 \approx\) 6.3 GB → it fits, but with little headroom.

⚠ Binding constraint Not the weights — it's the OS reservation + the growing KV cache. The app's real budget is ≈4–5 GB, and the KV cache is the part that grows during use, so long contexts are what trip the OOM-killer. Shrink it with quantized KV cache + sliding-window attention.
This is the question that separates people who have shipped on-device from people who have only read about it. Naively, "3B at INT4 is 1.5 GB, my phone has 8 GB, easy." Wrong — you have to budget everything resident at once. Weights are about 1.5 to 1.8 GB once you include the per-group scales. The KV cache is the sneaky one: it starts small but grows linearly with the conversation, a few hundred megabytes to most of a gigabyte at multi-thousand-token context, and it is the thing that keeps growing while the app runs. Activations and the runtime's scratch buffers are a few hundred megabytes. And critically, an 8 GB phone does not give you 8 GB — iOS and Android reserve roughly 3 to 4 GB for the OS, the GPU, and other apps, and they will kill your process the moment you push past your share. Add it up and you land around 6 GB, so it fits with thin headroom. The teaching punchline: the binding constraint is the OS reservation plus the growing KV cache, not the static weights — which is why on-device engineering obsesses over KV-cache quantization and sliding-window attention, and why you test at your longest realistic context, not an empty one.

In code · distillation loss

KD loss: soft KL at temperature T + hard CE

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def kd_loss(student_logits, teacher_logits, labels, T=2.0, alpha=0.5):
    # --- soft target: match the teacher's SOFTENED distribution ---
    # divide logits by T, then KL(teacher || student) in log-space
    s_log = F.log_softmax(student_logits / T, dim=-1)
    t_prob = F.softmax(teacher_logits / T, dim=-1)

    # KL expects log-probs for input, probs for target; batchmean = per-sample
    kl = F.kl_div(s_log, t_prob, reduction="batchmean")
    # T**2 rescales the soft gradient (softmax/T shrinks grads by 1/T^2)
    soft = kl * (T * T)

    # --- hard target: ordinary cross-entropy on the real labels ---
    hard = F.cross_entropy(student_logits, labels)

    # blend: alpha toward the teacher, (1 - alpha) toward ground truth
    return alpha * soft + (1.0 - alpha) * hard

# loss = kd_loss(student(x), teacher(x).detach(), y, T=2.0, alpha=0.7)
# teacher is frozen -> .detach() so no gradient flows back into it

The two lines that matter: / T softens both distributions, and * T*T keeps the soft gradient on the same scale as the hard one.

Walk this top to bottom. Block one is just imports. Block two is the soft target: we divide both the student and teacher logits by temperature T before the softmax, which flattens them so the teacher's runner-up probabilities — the dark knowledge — become learnable. Note the asymmetry KL-div expects: log-probabilities for the input (student) and plain probabilities for the target (teacher). Block three multiplies the KL by T-squared; this is the single most-forgotten line — because softening by T shrinks the gradients by one over T-squared, you scale back up by T-squared so the soft loss doesn't get drowned out by the hard loss. Block four is plain cross-entropy on the true labels, and the return blends them with alpha. The comment at the bottom is the other classic gotcha: the teacher is frozen, so you detach its logits — you are never training the teacher, only the student. In practice you precompute teacher logits offline so you don't pay a forward pass through the big model every step.

In code · INT4 quantize

A tiny absmax INT4 quantize / dequantize

import torch

def quantize_int4_absmax(w, group_size=64):
    # group the weights so one outlier only poisons its own block
    w = w.reshape(-1, group_size)              # (n_groups, group_size)
    # absmax scale per group: map [-amax, +amax] -> the int4 grid [-7, 7]
    amax = w.abs().amax(dim=1, keepdim=True)    # largest magnitude in group
    scale = amax / 7.0                          # 4-bit signed range is -8..7; use 7
    q = torch.round(w / scale).clamp(-7, 7)     # nearest grid point, integers
    return q.to(torch.int8), scale              # store int4-in-int8 + fp scales

def dequantize_int4(q, scale):
    # reconstruct an approximation: w_hat = q * scale  (per group)
    return (q.float() * scale)

# --- round-trip sanity check ---
W = torch.randn(256, 64)                        # an fp weight matrix
q, scale = quantize_int4_absmax(W, group_size=64)
W_hat = dequantize_int4(q, scale).reshape(W.shape)
err = (W - W_hat).abs().mean()
print(f"mean abs error: {err:.4f}")            # small -> 4 bits kept most signal

absmax = symmetric, zero-point-free: the scale is just the group's largest magnitude over 7. group_size is the accuracy/overhead knob — smaller groups isolate outliers but store more scales.

This is the toy version of what GGUF and bitsandbytes do for real. The core idea is a linear map: pick a scale that maps the weights' range onto the small integer grid, round, and store the integers plus the scale. We use absmax — symmetric quantization with no zero-point — so the scale is simply the largest absolute value in the group divided by seven, mapping the range onto the signed 4-bit grid. The reshape into groups is the whole reason this works well: a single large outlier weight only ruins the precision of its own 64-weight block instead of the entire tensor, which is exactly why group-wise beats per-tensor. Quantize rounds and clamps to integers; dequantize multiplies back by the scale to get an approximation. The round-trip test prints a small mean error, showing four bits keep most of the signal. Real schemes add the niceties — NF4 uses a non-uniform grid tuned to the Gaussian shape of weights, AWQ scales salient channels up before quantizing — but the skeleton is exactly this.

Worked example · the plan

Shrink a 7B model to fit a phone

Goal: from a 14 GB FP16 7B down to something that lives happily on an 8 GB phone — while keeping as much task accuracy as we can.

  2. Quantize the 3B student to INT4/NF4 with AWQ-style calibration. Weights fall \(6 \to \approx 1.7\) GB.
  3. Structured-prune a few low-importance heads/layers, then fine-tune to heal. Trim toward \(\approx 1.4\) GB and a little faster.
  4. Package GGUF (e.g. Q4_K_M) so llama.cpp can mmap it and run on the device.
This is question 18 from the bank, the canonical "shrink a 7B to a phone" walkthrough — order matters and each step solves a different problem. Step one, distillation, is the biggest single lever: instead of compressing the 7B, we transfer its behavior into a 3B student trained on the soft-KL-plus-hard-CE loss from the math section over a task-rich dataset, which roughly halves the weights to 6 GB and, crucially, keeps far more accuracy than just shrinking the 7B by force. Step two, quantization to INT4 or NF4 with AWQ-style calibration, is the cheapest big win — it does not retrain, just re-stores the weights at a quarter precision, taking 6 GB down to about 1.7. Step three, structured pruning of low-importance heads and layers followed by a short heal-fine-tune, squeezes out a bit more memory and latency — structured, not unstructured, because we want a real speedup on the phone's CPU/GPU. Step four packages it as GGUF so llama.cpp can memory-map and run it. On the next slide we do the actual accuracy and footprint math at each step.

This is the interview-favourite decision tree. The first fork is the one that overrides everything else: if you have a hard privacy, offline, low-latency, or data-residency requirement, you go on-device regardless of capability — the data simply can't leave the device. Decision · closing the gap

Four ways a small model punches up

Base SLMlimited capacity + RAGfacts in-context + Fine-tunenarrow task + Test-time computeCoT · vote · verify + Cascadeescalate hard
  1. RAG — retrieve facts at inference, so the model looks up knowledge instead of memorizing it. Closes knowledge/recency gaps; doesn't fix reasoning.
  2. Fine-tune — specialize on a narrow, stable task; matches or beats a prompted big model on that slice, cheaper per call.
  3. Test-time compute — extended CoT, sample-and-vote, verifier-guided search trade latency/energy for accuracy.
  4. Cascade — let the SLM answer easy queries, escalate low-confidence ones to a frontier model.
The gap between a 1–3B model and a frontier model is real, but four levers shrink it. RAG is the biggest single win for knowledge-bound tasks: the small model retrieves relevant facts and reasons over them in-context, so it doesn't have to store the world — but be honest that RAG fixes knowledge, not capability; multi-hop reasoning still strains a weak model and garbage retrieval gives garbage answers. Fine-tuning wins for a narrow, high-volume, stable slice where you have good data. Test-time compute — chain-of-thought, self-consistency voting, best-of-N with a verifier — recovers reasoning by spending inference compute, which is often impractical on a phone's battery and thermal budget. And cascade ties it together: cheap local model first, frontier escalation for the hard tail. Stack these and a small model punches well above its parameter count.

Pitfalls · misconceptions

People think X, but actually Y (1/2)

⚠ Pitfall
  • Think: "INT4 perplexity barely moved, so the quant is safe." Actually: perplexity is an average over generic text — it hides collapse on rare-but-critical tokens (JSON delimiters, function names), so agentic / tool-calling / long-context tasks can fall off a cliff while perplexity looks fine.
  • Think: "self-hosting an SLM is obviously cheaper than a per-token API." Actually: raw compute is the small part — hidden ops, eval pipelines, on-call/SRE, model updates & security dominate; break-even depends entirely on volume and utilization. Low/bursty traffic favours the API.
  • Think: "distillation matched my eval set, so it's done." Actually: the student often only mimicked the teacher in-domain — distribution narrowing means it breaks on production inputs the eval never covered.
Three of the most common ways SLM projects go wrong. One — the perplexity trap: people quantize to INT4, see perplexity move by a fraction of a point, and ship. But perplexity is a smooth average over ordinary text; it's blind to the rare, high-stakes tokens that tool-calling and structured output depend on — a single mis-predicted JSON brace breaks a function call. Diagnose with task-specific evals (parse-success rate, exact-match on schemas), and fix by calibrating on tool-call data, keeping the lm-head in higher precision, or adding constrained decoding. Two — the "obviously cheaper" fallacy: GPU price is the visible tip; the iceberg is MLOps, eval, on-call, OTA updates, and security. Break-even is driven by volume and utilization, and idle owned hardware is wasted fixed cost. Three — the distillation eval mirage: matching the teacher on your eval often just means the student learned the teacher's in-domain behaviour; the distribution narrowed and it fails on the long tail in production. Test on OOD and production-representative data, not just the eval you distilled toward.

Pitfalls · misconceptions

People think X, but actually Y (2/2)

⚠ Pitfall
  • Think: "higher NPU TOPS = faster LLM." Actually: decoding is memory-bandwidth bound, not compute bound — peak TOPS rarely matters. NPUs also hit unsupported ops & immature compilers, so real throughput often trails the GPU on the same chip.
  • Think: "one 1.5B model can be a great general assistant." Actually: reset expectations — a 1.5B model can't match frontier breadth, reasoning, or reliability. Scope it: define a narrow use set, fine-tune + RAG, constrain outputs, cascade hard cases.
  • Think: "on-device = perfectly private." Actually: telemetry, model-update channels, and cloud fallback can still leak data — and the device itself can be compromised.
Three more. The TOPS myth is everywhere in hardware marketing: an NPU advertising more tera-ops looks like it should be faster, but autoregressive decode at batch 1 reads the whole weight matrix per token and does little math — it's bandwidth-bound, so peak compute is mostly irrelevant. NPUs also frequently lack ops for dynamic shapes or certain attention forms, forcing CPU/GPU fallbacks and data shuffling, so the flexible GPU often wins in practice. Measure real tokens/sec, don't trust the spec sheet. The "1.5B general assistant" ask is a recurring product-management trap — you have to reset expectations: a tiny model will hallucinate more, reason worse, and break outside its training. The fix isn't a bigger promise, it's tighter scope — narrow the use set, fine-tune for it, add RAG for knowledge, constrain structured outputs, and cascade the hard cases up. And a quick honesty check on privacy: on-device shrinks the attack surface but isn't magic — telemetry, update channels, and cloud fallback are all leak paths.

  • What is a "small language model," and what parameter ranges do people mean? Easy▸ reveal
    A model small enough to run on constrained hardware (phone, laptop, edge) or self-host cheaply — typically ~100M–7B params, sometimes up to ~13B. The defining trait is deployability under tight memory/compute/power budgets, not an exact count; "small" is relative to tens-to-hundreds-of-billions frontier models. Don't give a single rigid cutoff as if it's official.
  • Why can an SLM have lower end-to-end latency than a bigger cloud model with higher raw TPS? Med▸ reveal
    End-to-end latency includes network round-trip, queuing, and time-to-first-token — not just generation throughput. A local SLM eliminates RTT and congestion, so TTFT is near-instant; for short responses the fixed network/queue overhead of a cloud call dominates. Cloud's TPS edge only shows on long outputs.
  • Why have SLMs improved faster than parameter count alone would predict? Med▸ reveal
    Better data (curated/dedup'd/synthetic "textbook" data), distillation from strong teachers, training far past Chinchilla compute-optimal (optimal for training cost, not for a fixed inference budget), improved architectures (GQA, better positional encodings, tied embeddings), and stronger post-training (SFT, RLHF/DPO). Quantization and runtimes made them practical. Quality of data and training, not raw size, drove the gains.
  • Why does data quality disproportionately benefit small models? Med▸ reveal
    Small models have limited capacity, so every parameter must be spent efficiently. High-quality, dense, low-noise data teaches more per token and wastes less capacity memorizing noise/boilerplate. Large models have spare capacity to absorb messy web data; small ones don't — so curation and synthetic high-signal data (the phi thesis) give outsized gains.
  • Structured vs unstructured pruning — which actually speeds up commodity hardware, and why? Med▸ reveal
    Structured pruning removes whole units (neurons, heads, channels, layers) → smaller dense matrices that standard dense kernels run faster, so it speeds up commodity CPUs/GPUs. Unstructured pruning zeros individual weights → sparse matrices that need special kernels/hardware (or fixed patterns like 2:4) to help; otherwise it only saves storage, not wall-clock. Unstructured sparsity ≠ general speedup.
  • Compare INT8, INT4, and NF4. What does NF4 assume about the weights? Med▸ reveal
    INT8 = 8-bit uniform, near-lossless. INT4 = 4-bit, 16 levels, smaller but lossier. NF4 (NormalFloat4, from QLoRA) is 4-bit with non-uniform levels chosen via quantiles of a standard normal — information-theoretically optimal for normally distributed data. NF4 assumes weights are roughly zero-mean Gaussian, placing levels denser near zero where most weights lie.
  • Estimate the memory of a 7B model at FP16, INT8, INT4 (weights only). Med▸ reveal
    Bytes ≈ params × bits/8. FP16 = 2 B/param → 7B×2 ≈ 14 GB. INT8 = 1 B → ≈ 7 GB. INT4 = 0.5 B → ≈ 3.5 GB. So roughly 14 / 7 / 3.5 GB. Real footprint is a bit higher due to quant metadata (scales/zero-points per block) and overhead; embeddings count in the params.
  • How does AWQ decide which weights to protect at 4-bit, and why does it matter? Hard▸ reveal
    A small fraction of weight channels are salient — identified by the magnitude of the activations they multiply, not by weight magnitude. AWQ scales those channels up (and inversely scales the activations) before quantizing everything to 4-bit, preserving their effective precision. Activation-important weights carry outsized impact on outputs, so protecting them keeps accuracy high. It's activation-importance, not "keep the largest weights."
  • INT4 perplexity is fine but tool-calling degrades — diagnose and fix. Hard▸ reveal
    Perplexity averages over generic text and hides failures on rare-but-critical tokens (JSON delimiters, function names) that tool-calling needs. Diagnose with task-specific evals (schema exact-match, parse-success rate), inspect logits on format tokens. Fix: AWQ/GPTQ with tool-call examples in calibration, keep sensitive layers/lm-head higher precision, raise bit-width, add constrained/grammar decoding, or QAT on agentic data. Perplexity is not a sufficient quant-quality signal.
  • Why is on-device decoding memory-bandwidth bound, and what does that imply for hardware? Hard▸ reveal
    Autoregressive decode runs at batch 1: each step reads the entire weight matrix (and KV cache) from memory to do relatively little matmul, so arithmetic intensity (FLOPs/byte) is very low — time is dominated by moving weights, not computing. Implication: prioritize memory bandwidth and smaller/quantized weights over raw FLOPs/TOPS; batching and speculative decoding help by amortizing weight reads.
  • Why might an NPU give worse real LLM throughput than the GPU despite higher TOPS? Hard▸ reveal
    Advertised TOPS is peak (usually integer) compute, but decoding is bandwidth-bound, so peak TOPS rarely matters. NPUs often lack ops (dynamic shapes, certain attention) → CPU/GPU fallbacks and data shuffling; immature compilers/quant support; fixed-function pipelines ill-suited to autoregressive decode; sometimes lower usable bandwidth. Don't equate TOPS with real LLM throughput.
  • Explain speculative decoding and the draft model's role. Med▸ reveal
    A small fast "draft" model proposes several future tokens; the large "verifier"/target checks them in one parallel forward pass and, via an accept/resample rule, keeps the longest valid prefix, correcting at the first rejection. Because verifying many tokens is parallel and cheap vs sequential decoding, you get several tokens per target pass when the draft is right — speeding up while keeping output distributed exactly as the target.
  • What property makes speculative decoding distribution-exact? Hard▸ reveal
    Modified rejection sampling: accept a draft token x with prob min(1, p_target(x)/p_draft(x)); on rejection, resample from the normalized residual ∝ max(0, p_target − p_draft). This makes the output distributed exactly as the target for any draft. The draft needn't match the target — the accept/resample rule makes it exact; a closer draft just raises acceptance/speed.
  • Give a decision framework: deploy an SLM vs call a frontier API. Med▸ reveal
    Favour an SLM when the task is narrow, privacy/offline/latency/data-residency are required, volume is high, and acceptable quality is reachable via fine-tune/RAG. Favour a frontier API for broad open-ended tasks needing top reasoning/world knowledge at low volume with fast iteration. Decide on a quality bar (eval it), cost at expected volume, latency/privacy needs, and ops capacity. Often a hybrid/cascade is best.
  • What hidden costs accompany self-hosting an SLM fleet beyond raw compute? Med▸ reveal
    MLOps/infra (serving, autoscaling, monitoring), eval pipelines + continuous regression testing, on-call/SRE and incident response, model updates/retraining and version rollout (OTA for devices), security (patching, isolation, prompt-injection defense), data/labeling, compliance/audit, and the talent to do it all. These ongoing costs often dwarf GPU price and are easy to underestimate.
  • A team insists on one 1.5B model as a broad assistant — what do you reset and propose? Med▸ reveal
    Reset: a 1.5B model can't match frontier breadth, reasoning, world knowledge, or reliability — expect more hallucination, weaker long-context/multi-step, brittleness outside training.
